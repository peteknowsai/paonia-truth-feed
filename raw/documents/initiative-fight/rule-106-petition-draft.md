FACTUAL BACKGROUND

6. On March 27, 2026, Petitioner submitted a citizen initiative petition to the Town Clerk pursuant to C.R.S. 31-11-106. The proposed ordinance is titled "An Initiated Ordinance of the People of the Town of Paonia, Colorado, Prohibiting Government Surveillance Cameras on Public Property and Providing for Enforcement" (the "Initiative").

7. The Initiative proposes a single legislative policy: prohibiting the Town from installing, operating, or maintaining surveillance cameras on public property.

8. The Initiative contains the following provisions, all in service of that single policy:
   a. Section 1: Title
   b. Section 2: Definitions (defining "surveillance camera," "public property," and "Town")
   c. Section 3: The prohibition (the Town shall not install, operate, or maintain surveillance cameras on public property) and a requirement to remove existing cameras within 90 days
   d. Section 4: Exceptions (body-worn cameras on law enforcement, warrant-authorized cameras, and public meeting cameras)
   e. Section 5: Contracts violating the ordinance are void
   f. Section 6: Citizen enforcement (any registered elector may seek injunctive relief; prevailing plaintiff entitled to attorney fees)
   g. Section 7: Amendment and repeal only by vote of registered electors
   h. Section 8: Severability
   i. Section 9: Effective date

9. On April 3, 2026, Respondent rejected the Initiative in writing, citing the following grounds:
   a. The Initiative violates the single-subject requirement of Article V, Section 1(5.5) of the Colorado Constitution
   b. The body-worn camera exception is "governed by the Enhance Law Enforcement Integrity Act" and "local measures may not conflict with or override state law"
   c. "Surveillance of Public Property" constitutes an "administrative function" not within the scope of citizen-initiated legislation
   d. Sections 2 and 5 (contracts) are "administrative matters" not "legislative in nature"
   e. Section 6 (injunctive relief) is "a judicial function" that "cannot be established through local legislation"
   f. Section 7 (voter approval for amendment/repeal) "has been determined to be invalid under Colorado law"

10. On April 3, 2026, Petitioner submitted a written response to the Clerk's rejection, disputing each ground and demanding acceptance within five business days. The Clerk has not accepted the petition.

FIRST CLAIM FOR RELIEF
Abuse of Discretion (C.R.C.P. 106(a)(4))

11. Petitioner incorporates all preceding paragraphs.

12. Under C.R.S. 31-11-106(1), a town clerk "may reject a petition or a section of a petition on the grounds that the petition or a section of the petition does not propose municipal legislation pursuant to section 1(9) of article V of the state constitution."

13. The clerk's authority under this statute is narrow: she may determine whether the petition proposes legislation. She may not adjudicate the constitutionality, enforceability, or wisdom of the proposed ordinance. Those are questions for the courts, not the clerk.

14. The Colorado Supreme Court has held that the initiative power must be "liberally construed" in favor of the people's right to legislate. Vagneur v. City of Aspen, 295 P.3d 493 (Colo. 2013). Any doubt about whether a proposed initiative is legislative or administrative must be resolved in favor of the petitioners.

15. Respondent's rejection constitutes an abuse of discretion for the following reasons:

SINGLE-SUBJECT (Ground 9a)

16. The Initiative addresses a single subject: the prohibition of government surveillance cameras on public property. The definitions, exceptions, enforcement mechanism, contract provision, amendment process, severability clause, and effective date are standard structural components of any ordinance, not independent subjects.

17. Colorado courts have consistently held that provisions relating to a single policy objective satisfy the single-subject requirement even when they address multiple operational aspects of that objective. In re Title, Ballot Title & Submission Clause for 2007-2008 No. 62, 370 P.3d 628 (Colo. 2016).

18. Article V, Section 1(5.5) primarily governs statewide ballot measures. Respondent cites no authority applying this standard to reject a municipal initiative petition under C.R.S. 31-11-106.

BODY-WORN CAMERAS (Ground 9b)

19. Section 4(a) of the Initiative states: "Body-worn cameras on law enforcement officers, governed by separate policy" are excepted from the prohibition. This is an exemption, not a regulation. The Initiative explicitly carves out body-worn cameras from its scope.

20. An exemption is the opposite of regulation. Respondent's characterization of an exemption as regulation of the exempted subject matter is a fundamental misreading of the Initiative's text.

21. The Enhance Law Enforcement Integrity Act governs body-worn cameras. The Initiative does not purport to regulate, modify, or override any provision of that Act. It excludes body-worn cameras entirely. There is no conflict with state law.

SURVEILLANCE AS "ADMINISTRATIVE" (Ground 9c)

22. Whether a municipal government may conduct persistent video surveillance of its residents in public spaces is a policy determination, not an administrative detail. It implicates fundamental questions of privacy, civil liberties, and the relationship between government and citizens.

23. Under the test established in City of Montrose v. Public Utilities Commission, 629 P.2d 619 (Colo. 1981), a legislative act (1) creates permanent law, (2) declares policy, and (3) applies generally. The Initiative satisfies all three elements: it creates a permanent prohibition, declares a policy against government surveillance of public spaces, and applies generally to all Town departments, officials, and contractors.

24. The Colorado General Assembly has itself legislated on government surveillance technology. SB 22-113 regulates government use of facial recognition. HB 24-1130 requires consent for biometric collection, bans selling biometric data, and mandates deletion protocols. If the state legislature can restrict government surveillance through legislation, citizens exercising initiative power, which is coextensive with legislative power under Article V, Section 1, can do the same at the municipal level.

25. Respondent's position, if accepted, would mean that citizens can never legislate about government surveillance. That result is inconsistent with the liberal construction mandate of Vagneur and with the text and purpose of Article V, Section 1(9).

CONTRACTS (Ground 9d)

26. Section 5 provides that contracts entered into in violation of the ordinance are void. This is a standard enforcement provision found in ordinances across the country, not an independent administrative act.

27. Legislative bodies routinely restrict government contracting authority through legislation: procurement rules, prevailing wage laws, conflict-of-interest provisions, sole-source contract restrictions, and prohibitions on contracts that violate existing law. A provision declaring contracts void if they violate the substantive prohibition of the ordinance is a conventional legislative enforcement mechanism.

28. This provision is not severable from the Initiative's purpose. Without it, the Town could circumvent the surveillance prohibition by contracting with a third party to install and operate cameras. The contract provision ensures the prohibition cannot be evaded.

INJUNCTIVE RELIEF (Ground 9e)

29. Section 6 creates a cause of action allowing any registered elector to seek injunctive relief for violations. Creating a cause of action is a legislative function.

30. Every ordinance with an enforcement or penalty provision creates some form of legal remedy. The court then exercises its judicial function by adjudicating whether a violation has occurred and whether relief is warranted. The Initiative does not direct the court to reach any particular outcome.

31. Respondent's rejection confuses creating a right to seek relief (a legislative act) with the exercise of judicial jurisdiction (a judicial act). If this reasoning were correct, no ordinance could ever provide for enforcement through the courts.

VOTER APPROVAL FOR AMENDMENT/REPEAL (Ground 9f)

32. Respondent states that requiring voter approval to amend or repeal an ordinance "has been determined to be invalid under Colorado law" but cites no case, statute, or other authority for this proposition.

33. Even if this provision were invalid, the Initiative contains a severability clause (Section 8) providing that "if any provision of this ordinance is held invalid by a court of competent jurisdiction, the remaining provisions shall continue in full force and effect." The alleged invalidity of one severable provision cannot justify rejection of the entire Initiative.

34. Under C.R.S. 31-11-106(1), the Clerk may reject "a petition or a section of a petition." She could have identified Section 7 as potentially problematic while accepting the remainder. She chose instead to reject the entire petition. That is an abuse of discretion.

SECOND CLAIM FOR RELIEF
Mandamus (C.R.C.P. 106(a)(2))

35. Petitioner incorporates all preceding paragraphs.

36. The Town Clerk's duty to accept citizen initiative petitions that propose municipal legislation is a ministerial duty. The Clerk has no discretion to refuse a petition that proposes legislation on its face.

37. The Initiative proposes a permanent, generally applicable prohibition on government surveillance cameras. Under the Montrose test and the liberal construction mandate of Vagneur, it proposes legislation.

38. Respondent has refused to perform her ministerial duty by rejecting a petition that clearly proposes municipal legislation.

39. Petitioner has no adequate remedy at law. The Initiative cannot proceed to signature collection, and the citizens of Paonia cannot vote on it, unless the Clerk accepts it.

40. Petitioner is entitled to an order in the nature of mandamus compelling Respondent to accept the Initiative petition for circulation.
