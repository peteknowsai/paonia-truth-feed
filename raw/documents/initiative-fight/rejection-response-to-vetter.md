**Re: Rejection of Citizen Initiative Petition — "Surveillance Camera Prohibition"**

Dear Ms. Vetter:

I am in receipt of your letter of April 3, 2026, rejecting the citizen initiative petition for the Surveillance Camera Prohibition ordinance. Your rejection rests on six grounds, each of which is legally deficient. I address them in turn.

---

## 1. Single-Subject Violation (Article V, Section 1(5.5), Colorado Constitution)

The initiative addresses a single subject: the prohibition of government surveillance cameras on public property. The exceptions (Section 4), enforcement mechanism (Section 6), contract provisions (Section 5), and amendment process (Section 7) are standard structural components of any ordinance — not independent subjects. They each serve the single policy goal of prohibiting municipal surveillance cameras.

Colorado courts have consistently held that provisions relating to a single policy objective satisfy the single-subject requirement, even when they address multiple operational aspects of that objective. *See In re Title, Ballot Title & Submission Clause for 2007-2008 #62*, 370 P.3d 628 (Colo. 2016) (single-subject satisfied where provisions share a common policy purpose).

Moreover, Article V, Section 1(5.5) primarily governs statewide ballot measures. Its application to municipal citizen initiatives is not established in the manner your rejection assumes. You cite no municipal case law applying this standard to reject a local initiative petition, because the framework for municipal initiatives is governed by the municipality's own charter and the Municipal Home Rule Act.

## 2. Body-Worn Cameras and the Enhance Law Enforcement Integrity Act

This ground is either a fundamental misreading of the initiative text or a deliberate mischaracterization.

Section 4(a) of the initiative **explicitly exempts** body-worn cameras: *"Body-worn cameras on law enforcement officers, governed by separate policy."*

An exemption is the opposite of regulation. The initiative does not regulate, restrict, mandate, or in any way address body-worn cameras. It carves them out entirely. Citing state preemption of body-worn camera regulation as grounds to reject an initiative that explicitly excludes body-worn cameras from its scope is without legal basis.

If an ordinance banning sugary drinks explicitly exempted milk, one would not reject it on the grounds that dairy is regulated by the state. The exemption *is* the deference to state law.

## 3. Surveillance of Public Property as an "Administrative Function"

Your characterization of government surveillance policy as a mere "administrative function" beyond the reach of citizen legislation is both legally incorrect and profoundly troubling in its implications.

Whether a municipal government may conduct persistent video surveillance of its residents in public spaces is a **policy determination of the highest order** — not a ministerial or administrative detail. It implicates fundamental questions of privacy, civil liberties, and the relationship between government and the governed.

Numerous municipalities have enacted surveillance legislation through their legislative processes, including:

- **San Francisco, CA** — banned government use of facial recognition technology (2019)
- **Portland, OR** — enacted a comprehensive surveillance ordinance restricting city acquisition and use of surveillance technologies (2020)
- **Oakland, CA** — passed a surveillance oversight ordinance requiring council approval for surveillance technology (2018)
- **Cambridge, MA** — enacted a surveillance technology ordinance with citizen oversight provisions (2018)

Under your reasoning, none of these ordinances could have been enacted by citizen initiative, and indeed, none could have been enacted by the legislative body itself, since you classify the subject matter as "administrative." This position is untenable. A town board's decision to install surveillance cameras monitoring public spaces is a legislative policy choice, and citizens have the right to legislate on the same subject through the initiative process.

Moreover, the Colorado General Assembly itself has legislated on government surveillance: SB 22-113 regulates facial recognition use by government agencies, and HB 24-1130 (effective July 2025) requires consent for biometric collection, bans selling biometric data, and mandates deletion protocols. If the state legislature can restrict government surveillance technology through legislation, citizens exercising initiative power, which is coextensive with legislative power under Article V, Section 1, can do the same at the municipal level.

## 4. Contract Provisions (Section 5) as "Administrative Matters"

Section 5 provides that contracts entered into in violation of the ordinance are void. This is a standard enforcement provision, not an independent administrative act.

Legislative bodies restrict government contracting authority constantly: procurement rules, prevailing wage laws, conflict-of-interest provisions, sole-source contract restrictions, and prohibitions on contracts that violate existing law. A provision declaring contracts void if they violate the substantive prohibition of the ordinance is a conventional legislative enforcement mechanism found in ordinances across the country.

Your position would require stripping the enforcement mechanism from every citizen initiative that touches government operations — effectively rendering the initiative power meaningless for any ordinance that the government might implement through contracts.

## 5. Injunctive Relief (Section 6) as a "Judicial Function"

Section 6 creates a **cause of action** allowing any registered elector to seek injunctive relief for violations. Creating a cause of action is a **legislative function**. Every ordinance with an enforcement or penalty provision creates some form of legal remedy or right of action.

The court then exercises its **judicial function** by adjudicating whether a violation has occurred and whether relief is warranted. The initiative does not — and could not — direct the court to reach any particular outcome. It merely opens the courthouse door.

Your rejection confuses creating a right to seek relief (a legislative act) with the exercise of judicial jurisdiction (a judicial act). If this reasoning were correct, no ordinance could ever provide for enforcement through the courts, which would nullify the enforcement provisions of virtually every law on the books.

For the record, existing surveillance ordinances in other jurisdictions include nearly identical citizen enforcement provisions. Oakland's surveillance ordinance allows "any person who has been subjected to a surveillance technology in violation of [the] Ordinance to recover actual damages" and seek "injunctive relief, declaratory relief, or a writ of mandate," plus reasonable attorney's fees. Berkeley's surveillance ordinance similarly provides for attorney's fees recovery up to $15,000 for prevailing complainants. These provisions have been operative for years without legal challenge to their legislative character. (See UC Berkeley School of Law, "Local Surveillance Oversight Ordinances," February 2021.) Section 6 of my initiative is modeled on these established precedents.

## 6. Voter Approval for Amendment or Repeal (Section 7)

While there is case law addressing the authority of legislative bodies to bind future legislatures, citizens acting through the initiative process occupy a different constitutional position. The initiative and referendum powers are reserved to the people and are construed liberally in their favor. *See City of Colorado Springs v. Bull*, 143 Colo. 155 (1960); *Vagneur v. City of Aspen*, 295 P.3d 493 (Colo. 2013).

Critically, even if Section 7 were found to exceed the scope of the initiative power, it would not justify rejection of the entire petition. **Section 8 of the initiative contains an explicit severability clause**, providing that if any provision is held invalid, the remaining provisions shall continue in full force and effect. The appropriate course is to allow the initiative to proceed and let the courts, if called upon, sever any provision found to be invalid — not to reject the entire initiative on the basis of a single potentially severable provision.
