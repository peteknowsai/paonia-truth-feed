FACTUAL BACKGROUND

The Initiative

8. Petitioners submitted a citizen initiative petition to the Town Clerk pursuant to C.R.S. 31-11-106. The proposed ordinance is titled "An Initiated Ordinance of the People of the Town of Paonia, Colorado, Establishing a Moratorium on Autonomous Robots, Protecting Public Safety and Privacy, and Providing for Enforcement and Review" (the "Initiative").

9. The Initiative proposes a temporary moratorium on the operation of autonomous robots in public spaces within Town limits. It contains seven sections:

   a. Section 1: Title ("Robot Moratorium")
   b. Section 2: Definitions (defining "Autonomous Robot" and "Public Space")
   c. Section 3: The moratorium -- no person or entity shall operate autonomous robots in public spaces within Town limits, with an exemption for emergency response robots operated by public safety agencies
   d. Section 4: Enforcement -- violations may be reported by any person and constitute a civil infraction
   e. Section 5: Sunset and review -- the moratorium expires December 31, 2027, and the Town is encouraged to develop regulations through public process
   f. Section 6: Severability
   g. Section 7: Effective date (30 days after certification of election results)

10. The Initiative does not create any fees, licenses, or permits. It does not target any specific contract, contractor, or person. It does not direct any administrative action. It establishes a general, temporary prohibition applicable to all persons and entities equally.

The Rejection

11. On April 10, 2026, Respondent rejected the Initiative in a letter to Petitioners. The letter stated the following grounds for rejection:

   a. "The proposed initiatives seek to amend or supplant administrative decisions and actions. Decisions that require careful study and specialized expertise, as well as discretionary judgment, generally are administrative in nature, and thus are outside the scope of the initiative power reserved to the people under state constitution."

   b. "The attached petition is administrative in nature in that it seeks to control the Town's executive function in the management and operation of Town property. The Town's previous administrative decision to contract an ADA analysis of Town sidewalks led to the use of automated machinery by the Town's contractor."

   c. "Supplanting contracts or exercising rights under contracts are plainly administrative matters, and government decisions to enter into a contract with a specific entity are not legislative decisions."

   d. "The proposed ordinance encroaches on the administrative processes reserved to the administrative staff of the Town as authorized by the BOT, and allowing the initiative process to either dictate or negate administrative actions normally undertaken by Town staff has been determined to be a misuse of the initiative process and contrary to the initiative rights established by the Colorado Constitution, Article V, Section 1, Paragraph 5.5."

12. In addition to the foregoing, Respondent's rejection letter contained a numbered list of seven section-by-section objections:

   a. "Prohibiting the use of autonomous robots by people or entities in public spaces is a purely executive act, and thus, the proposed measure cannot be placed on the ballot."

   b. "Section 2 - The definition of autonomous robots is substantive to the proposed prohibition of the same and cannot be individually severed from the ordinance."

   c. "Sections 3 and 4 are exemptions and/or omissions only applicable to paragraph (1) of the proposed measure and do not stand alone as proposed measure, and therefore cannot be individually severed from the ordinance intended purpose."

   d. "Section 5 is a substantive statement relating to the proposed prohibition of autonomous robots and cannot be individually severed from the purpose of the ordinance."

   e. "Section 6 is a general statement of a proposed effect of the prohibition of autonomous robots and cannot be individually severed from the ordinance."

   f. "Section 7 - injunctive relief is judicial relief that cannot be legislated by local government."

   g. "Section 8 - requiring voter approval to amend or repeal an ordinance has been determined as void pursuant to Colorado Law."

Pattern of Rejections

13. The rejection of this Initiative is not an isolated event. Respondent has rejected every citizen initiative petition submitted to her office since at least May 2025, applying the same "administrative" characterization each time:

   a. On May 28, 2025, Respondent rejected a citizen initiative regulating short-term rental lodging, characterizing fee provisions as "administrative."

   b. On April 3, 2026, Respondent rejected a citizen initiative prohibiting government surveillance cameras, citing six grounds including that surveillance of public property is an "administrative function."

   c. Petitioners revised and resubmitted their initiatives, removing every provision Respondent had specifically objected to. Respondent has continued to reject petitions on increasingly broad grounds.

   d. Petitioner McCarthy formally demanded the legal analysis supporting the May 2025 STR rejection on June 25, 2025. Respondent never provided it. Approximately ten months later, the Town disclosed the analysis -- not to Petitioner, but to a citizen advisory committee on short-term rentals, where it was used to instruct committee members that certain policy recommendations were "administrative" and could not even be suggested to the Board. The analysis that Respondent withheld from the initiative petitioner was repurposed to further restrict citizen participation in a separate public process.

14. In every instance, Respondent's central rationale has been that the proposed initiative is "administrative" rather than "legislative." The practical effect of Respondent's position is that no citizen initiative petition can proceed in the Town of Paonia regardless of its content.

FIRST CLAIM FOR RELIEF
Abuse of Discretion -- The Initiative Is Legislative (C.R.C.P. 106(a)(4))

15. Petitioners incorporate all preceding paragraphs.

16. Under C.R.S. 31-11-106(1), a town clerk "may reject a petition or a section of a petition on the grounds that the petition or a section of the petition does not propose municipal legislation pursuant to section 1(9) of article V of the state constitution."

16. The clerk's authority under this statute is narrow and ministerial: she may determine whether the petition proposes legislation. She may not adjudicate the constitutionality, enforceability, or wisdom of the proposed ordinance. Those are questions for the courts, not the clerk.

17. The Colorado Supreme Court has held that the initiative power must be "liberally construed" in favor of the people's right to legislate. Vagneur v. City of Aspen, 295 P.3d 493 (Colo. 2013). Any doubt about whether a proposed initiative is legislative or administrative must be resolved in favor of the petitioners.

18. Under the test established in City of Montrose v. Public Utilities Commission, 629 P.2d 619 (Colo. 1981), a legislative act (1) creates law that is permanent in nature, (2) declares public policy, and (3) applies generally to all persons and situations within the jurisdiction. The Initiative satisfies all three elements:

   a. It creates a temporary law establishing a moratorium on autonomous robots in public spaces. A moratorium with a defined duration is no less "permanent" than any ordinance subject to future amendment or repeal. It operates as binding law from its effective date through its sunset date.

   b. It declares a public policy: that autonomous robots should not operate in public spaces until the community has had an opportunity to evaluate and regulate them through public process.

   c. It applies generally to all persons and entities. It does not target any individual, contractor, or contract. Any person or entity seeking to operate an autonomous robot in public spaces is equally subject to the moratorium.

THE INITIATIVE DOES NOT SUPPLANT A CONTRACT (Grounds 11b and 11c)

19. Respondent's rejection rests heavily on the claim that "the Town's previous administrative decision to contract an ADA analysis of Town sidewalks led to the use of automated machinery by the Town's contractor," and that the Initiative therefore seeks to "supplant contracts."

20. The Initiative does not mention, reference, or target any contract. It does not mention ADA analysis, sidewalk surveys, or any specific contractor. It establishes a general prohibition on all autonomous robots in public spaces, regardless of their origin, purpose, or contractual relationship to the Town.

21. A general legislative prohibition does not become "administrative" merely because its effect may touch upon an existing contract. If that were the law, no legislative body could ever enact environmental regulations, safety standards, zoning restrictions, or technology moratoria if the regulated activity happened to be performed under a government contract.

22. The Colorado General Assembly routinely enacts legislation that affects existing government contracts. Procurement reforms, prevailing wage laws, environmental standards, and technology regulations all constrain what government contracts may authorize. These are legislative acts. The same power is available to citizens exercising the initiative power, which is "coextensive with the legislative power of the governing body." C.R.S. 31-11-101.

23. Respondent conflates the origin of the robots (a contract for ADA analysis) with the subject of the Initiative (autonomous robots in public spaces generally). The Initiative would apply equally to robots deployed by any person for any purpose, whether under contract with the Town or not.

THE INITIATIVE DOES NOT ENCROACH ON ADMINISTRATIVE PROCESSES (Ground 11d)

24. Respondent asserts that the Initiative "encroaches on the administrative processes reserved to the administrative staff of the Town" and is "contrary to the initiative rights established by the Colorado Constitution, Article V, Section 1, Paragraph 5.5."

25. Article V, Section 1, Paragraph 5.5 of the Colorado Constitution provides that "[t]he initiative and referendum powers reserved to the people by this section are hereby further reserved to the registered electors of every city, town, and municipality as to all local, special, and municipal legislation of every character in or for their respective municipalities." This provision reserves initiative power to the people. It does not restrict it.

26. The Initiative does not direct the Town's administrative staff to take any specific action. It does not tell them how to manage contracts, whom to hire, how to schedule work, or what equipment to use. It establishes a single, general rule: autonomous robots may not operate in public spaces during the moratorium period. How staff complies with that rule is left entirely to their administrative discretion.

27. Setting general rules that constrain government conduct is the definition of legislation. Every ordinance -- from speed limits to building codes to noise restrictions -- constrains what government and private actors may do. That constraint is the purpose of law.

28. Moratoria are among the most common forms of municipal legislation. Colorado municipalities routinely enact moratoria on development, drilling, construction, and other activities while policy frameworks are developed. A moratorium on autonomous robots is no different in kind. It is a temporary legislative pause, not an administrative directive.

RESPONDENT'S POSITION NULLIFIES THE INITIATIVE POWER

29. If accepted, Respondent's reasoning would mean that citizens may never use the initiative process to legislate about any activity that the Town has contracted for, authorized, or that involves "specialized expertise." Because virtually all municipal functions involve contracts, staff expertise, and administrative implementation, this reading would render the citizen initiative power meaningless in the Town of Paonia.

30. This result is irreconcilable with Article V, Section 1 of the Colorado Constitution, which reserves the initiative power to the people as a co-equal legislative authority. It is irreconcilable with C.R.S. 31-11-101, which declares the initiative power "coextensive with the legislative power of the governing body." And it is irreconcilable with Vagneur's mandate that "any doubt must be resolved in favor of the petitioners."

31. Respondent has now rejected every citizen initiative petition submitted to her office since May 2025, each time characterizing the proposed ordinance as "administrative." This pattern demonstrates that Respondent is not performing a case-by-case ministerial review of whether each petition proposes legislation. She is applying a blanket policy of rejection.

SECOND CLAIM FOR RELIEF
Mandamus (C.R.C.P. 106(a)(2))

32. Petitioners incorporate all preceding paragraphs.

33. The Town Clerk's duty to accept citizen initiative petitions that propose municipal legislation is a ministerial duty. The Clerk has no discretion to refuse a petition that proposes legislation on its face.

34. The Initiative proposes a temporary, generally applicable moratorium on autonomous robots in public spaces. Under the Montrose test and the liberal construction mandate of Vagneur, it proposes legislation.

35. Respondent has refused to perform her ministerial duty by rejecting a petition that clearly proposes municipal legislation.

36. Petitioners have no adequate remedy at law. The Initiative cannot proceed to signature collection, and the citizens of Paonia cannot vote on it, unless the Clerk accepts it.

37. Petitioners are entitled to an order in the nature of mandamus compelling Respondent to accept the Initiative petition for circulation.
