CU Denver New Directions Master's Program
Update for Local Governments
For 30 years, CU Denver's Center for New Directions in Politics & Policy has
been developing the knowledge and skills of local Colorado leaders through
weekend intensive courses taught by experienced practitioners. This fall the
program is welcoming a new director: former Denver City Councilwoman
Robin Kniech, who spent 12 years governing in Denver and engaged with
regional entities like the Denver Regional Council of Governments, statewide
organizations like County Commissioners Acting Together (CCAT), and national
networks of local elected officials.
The New Directions program originally focused only on local government, but
through the years has broadened to include other forms of local leadership and
management, including non-profit and labor organizations. Today, the program
integrates these fields. Students interested in local government will acquire
relevant and practical public sector knowledge and skills, along with context on
the political realities and inequalities impacting their communities and learning
about the community organizations they're likely to partner and engage with. In
the same way, students interested in community-based leadership will leave
with a deeper understanding of how local governments operate.
Things to know about New Directions
Weekend intensive courses (3 Sat/Sun weekends per course)
In person, with hybrid possibilities and some on-line courses
Affordable program
One-time scholarships available for new students focused on affordable
housing, experienced with civic engagement or leadership,
DACA/Dreamers, and based on need
Partnered with Colorado DOLA's Best and Brightest Fellowship to place
graduate students in small jurisdictions for 2 years with salary of $57k (Yr

1)/$63k (Yr 2) + retention bonuses
Small class sizes and personalized experience

Subject: Planning Commission Meeting Comments - November 04, 2023

Dear Planning Commissioners,
Due to work travel and weather-related flight delays, I regret that I will be
unable to attend tonight's meeting. I respectfully request that town staff print
and include these comments in tonight's packet for discussion. I would like to
submit the following comments for consideration regarding tonight's agenda
items.
Regarding Agenda Item 1 - Procedures - Clarification, I would like to submit the
following recommendations and supporting arguments for consideration:
1. Establish clear procedures that place final decision-making authority for
Planning Commission operations with the Commission itself, specifically:
- Final authority for meeting cancellations should rest with the Planning
Commission Chairperson

- Decisions regarding joint meetings with the Board of Trustees should be
determined by the Planning Commission Chairperson
- Implementation of a formalized agenda-setting procedure where:
* The Chairperson submits final agenda items in consultation with staff no
later than 10 days before scheduled meetings
* Staff distributes the final agenda no later than 7 days before regularly
scheduled meetings
The legal and procedural basis for these recommendations is substantial:
1. Statutory Authority and Commission Autonomy:
Colorado Revised Statutes (C.R.S.) § 31-23-204 explicitly grants planning
commissions the power to "adopt rules for the transaction of business,"
including control over agenda-setting and scheduling. When town staff
unilaterally controls these elements, it bypasses the commission's legal
mandate to self-govern its procedures. This current setup effectively limits the
commission's intended autonomy and authority, contrary to the statute's intent
for the commission to act as an independent, community-focused entity.
2. Public Representative Role:
Planning commissions were specifically created as representative bodies to
serve the community's planning and development interests directly. When town
staff controls agenda priorities and meeting cancellations, it risks shifting focus
toward internal administrative or political priorities rather than community
needs. This arrangement reduces the commission's capacity to function
independently and limits its ability to respond directly to community concerns—
a fundamental aspect of its statutory role.
3. Best Practices in Local Governance:
Throughout Colorado, planning commissions typically retain final agenda
authority to prevent administrative interference. For example, in Elbert County,
the planning commission sets its own agenda while utilizing staff input only for
support. This structure reflects the statutory recognition that planning
commissions should operate autonomously. Our current approach deviates
from these established standards, potentially diluting the commission's
decision-making power and public accountability.
4. Legislative Intent:
Colorado law intentionally places planning commissions in a position of public
accountability by granting them statutory authority to manage their own
procedural rules. When town staff hold power over agendas and meeting
decisions, it blurs this legal delineation and contradicts the legislative intent to
establish planning commissions as independent governing bodies. This
independence is crucial for the commission to act without undue influence from
administrative bodies.
Regarding civility and meeting decorum, I would like to address several specific
concerns from recent meetings that need attention to ensure professional

discourse and fair participation:
1. Points of Order: During our last meeting, town staff called Points of Order.
This is procedurally incorrect - Points of Order should only be called by
Planning Commission members, not staff. This is a fundamental rule of
parliamentary procedure that needs to be respected.
2. Professional Discourse: During the last meeting, Chairperson Hunter made
the comment "I resent Chairperson McCarthy for thinking he is the only one
with a family." This was apparently in response to my request to consider
school breaks and in-service days in our scheduling. If there are concerns
about scheduling requests, these should be addressed directly and
professionally, focusing on the merits of the request rather than personal
accusations.
3. Non-Verbal Conduct: During my remarks at the last meeting, Chairperson
Hunter was repeatedly shaking her head. Such non-verbal expressions of
disagreement during another Commissioner's speaking time are unprofessional
and can create an intimidating environment that discourages open discourse.
4. Meeting Management: At the end of the meeting, while I was speaking, Town
Staff (Ruben) muted me mid-sentence when Chairperson Hunter interrupted
me. When questioned about this action, the explanation given was that it was
"a one-sided conversation." This is deeply concerning for several reasons:
- Town staff should not be muting Planning Commission members, especially
during their allocated speaking time
- The decision to mute should only come from the Chair following proper
parliamentary procedure
- This action suggests potential bias from town staff favoring certain members
and opinions over others
- Staff must maintain strict impartiality in managing meeting logistics
These incidents indicate a need for:
- Clear guidelines on staff roles during meetings
- Review of proper parliamentary procedure
- Establishment of professional standards for both verbal and non-verbal
conduct
- Neutral and consistent application of meeting management procedures
- Commitment to allowing all Commissioners to complete their thoughts without
interruption
Regarding Agenda Item 4 - Discussion and Work on the Comprehensive Plan,
specifically the Housing Element, I have serious concerns about transparency
and process that need to be addressed:
1. Process and Transparency Issues:
- The draft Housing Element introduces 28 new policies that were not part of
the extensively vetted Housing Needs Assessment and Action Plan

- These new policies lack the rigorous documentation, data support, and
community input that characterized the Housing Needs Assessment process
- There has been no explanation provided for how these new policies relate to
or derive from the community engagement process
- The policies lack specificity and include no implementation examples or
resource analysis
2. Deviation from Board Direction:
- The Board of Trustees specifically directed that the Housing Needs
Assessment and Action Plan be incorporated as the Housing Element
- This directive was discussed and agreed upon in numerous meetings
throughout the past year
- The current draft deviates significantly from this direction without explanation
3. Community Priority Omissions:
- The draft inexplicably omits short-term rental regulation, which received the
highest number of votes (21) during our public engagement process
- This omission directly contradicts documented community priorities and
undermines the extensive public input process
4. Documentation Requirements:
Before the Planning Commission can properly evaluate the Housing Element,
we need either:
- Detailed documentation justifying each new policy addition, including
evidence of community support that meets the same standard of
documentation provided in the Housing Needs Assessment, or
- Simple incorporation of the Housing Needs Assessment and Action Plan as
originally directed by the Board
I cannot support including this element in its current form without proper
documentation of how these policies relate to our community engagement
process and the Housing Needs Assessment. Posting policies on a website
does not constitute the level of transparency and public engagement our
community deserves and expects.
Thank you for considering these comments. I look forward to our continued
work together at the next meeting.
Respectfully,
Pete McCarthy
Planning Commissioner

Direct Lending contemplates the
use of standardized documentation,
which enables the funding of
multiple loans under a
comprehensive Master Financing
Agreement (MFA)

Bond Financing involves creating
individual debt obligations, each
memorialized with its own set of
bond documents

INTEREST RATES &
EARLY PAYOFF
FLEXIBILITY

Direct Lending interest rates are
set based upon the financing term,
borrowers creditworthiness, ability to
pay off the loan early, and market
interest rates for all debt
instruments

Bond Financing interest rates are
set on the day the bond is sold to the
public and fixed for its entire term
The bond s total interest cost is
calculated by analyzing each serial
bond and term loan individual
interest rates, the cost of issuance,
and time value of money

TIME & FEES

Direct Lending accelerates the
funding process to 30 days or less
and allows the municipality s
executive team and its legal counsel
to work directly with the lender and
its legal counsel

Bond Financing involves many
more parties and various finance
professionals, as stated above It
may take months to assemble a
team, place the item on the ballot,
and sell the bonds to an underwriter

fundamental rule of parliamentary procedure that needs to be respected.

Chairperson McCarthy for thinking he is the only one with a family." This was apparently in response to

mid-sentence when Chairperson Hunter interrupted me. When questioned about this action, the
explanation given was that it was "a one-sided conversation." This is deeply concerning for several
reasons:
- Town staff should not be muting Planning Commission members, especially during their allocated
speaking time

- This action suggests potential bias from town staff favoring certain members and opinions over
others
- Staff must maintain strict impartiality in managing meeting logistics

- Clear guidelines on staff roles during meetings

changes in height shall be measured from the dimensions of the

appurtenances and any modifications that were approved prior to

to the body of the structure that would protrude from the edge of the
