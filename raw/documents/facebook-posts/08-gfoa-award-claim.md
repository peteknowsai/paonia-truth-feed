# Facebook Post — What an Award Can and Cannot Tell Us

At the April 28 Board of Trustees meeting, a citizen asked the Trustees to exercise greater oversight of the Town's finances. Mayor Paige Smith responded. Near the end of her response, she said this:

*"There is nothing untoward going on. I'm sorry that you're getting the impression that there is, but there is not. ... we have gotten award for our last two budgets from the government finance association."*

I want to walk through that statement carefully, because the award is real but it doesn't say what the Mayor used it to say.

**1. The count.**

The Government Finance Officers Association — the "government finance association" the Mayor referenced — publishes a complete public list of every government that has won its Distinguished Budget Presentation Award. The Town of Paonia appears on that list one time. For the FY 2025 budget. The Town's entry on GFOA's roster reads: "Total Awards: 1."

Paonia is not on the FY 2024 winners list. Paonia is not on the most recent published FY 2026 list. Whether the Mayor was speaking from a pending internal status, a misremembering, or a conflation with another credential, I don't know. What I know is that GFOA's records, as of the meeting and as of today, document one award, not two.

**2. What the award actually measures.**

This is GFOA's own description of the program, on their official website: *"The goal of the program is not to assess the financial health of participating governments, but rather to ensure that users of their financial statements have the information they need to do so themselves."*

Read that line carefully. The awarding organization itself states, in plain language, that the award is not a verdict on whether the underlying finances are healthy. It is a verdict on whether the budget *document* — the formatting, the section headings, the disclosures — meets a checklist of presentation standards.

A useful comparison: the award is to a budget document what a clean copy edit is to a manuscript. It tells you the writing is properly organized. It does not tell you whether the writing is true.

**3. How the award works.**

The Town applies for the award by paying GFOA an application fee, which for a town Paonia's size runs $380 to $800. The Town also pays annual GFOA membership dues separately. The reviewers who decide the award are paid GFOA staff plus volunteers drawn from the same profession. Approximately 1,800 to 1,900 governments win the award every year. Roughly 80 of those are in Colorado.

This is not, in itself, a scandal. Trade associations issue paid credentials all the time, and the discipline of preparing a budget document to meet GFOA's checklist has real internal value. The Town has done a thing. The thing is real.

**4. What the award cannot do.**

A budget *presentation* award does not certify the integrity of the underlying numbers, only the clarity of how those numbers are disclosed. It does not address whether the Town Administrator's combined role as Treasurer is appropriate. It does not address whether the internal controls would have prevented the 2012 embezzlement that took $480,000 from this very Town. The GFOA explicitly says so.

There is a specific, on-paper reason this matters in Paonia. On April 14, 2026, the Town produced the Caselle Application Rights Report in response to a separate records request (CORA C 26-10). Caselle is the financial system the Town uses to record and approve every transaction. The report shows that Mr. Wynn — under the user ID "TManager" — is permitted to perform both *Enter Invoices* and *Approve Invoices for Payment.* Under standard governmental accounting controls, those two functions are required to be separated between two different people, so that the system's records can demonstrate one person's action serving as an independent check on the other's. When the same user performs both, that chain-of-evidence trail does not exist in the records at all. The two events become structurally indistinguishable as a control matter.

There is an exception to this principle for small governments, but the exception is instructive. The New York State Office of the State Comptroller — the most-cited authority on local government internal controls — says that when a small town's treasurer must perform multiple financial functions because there isn't staff to separate them, the required compensating control is **periodic review by a board member of the treasurer's work, especially monthly bank reconciliations.** When duties cannot be separated through staff, board oversight is the substitute. Not a presentation award.

The Town's stated compensating control is a Finance Committee of the Mayor and one Trustee. Two facts about that committee are on the record. First, no Finance Committee minutes, agendas, invoice review logs, or records of disbursements flagged or questioned by the Committee appear anywhere on the Town's public-facing channels. The Town's official CivicClerk meeting portal has not noticed a single Finance Committee meeting in the thirty public meetings posted there going back to January 6, 2025. The Town's own "Town Staff, Board of Trustees, and Committee Members" webpage lists one committee, the Short-Term Rental Ad Hoc Committee, and does not list a Finance Committee at all. A Colorado Open Records Act request asking the Committee for any record of a disbursement it has flagged in the last two years was filed on May 9, 2026. Second, at the same April 28 meeting, the Mayor described her own posture toward the Town Administrator in these terms: *"I have complete faith in our administrator,"* and a few sentences later, *"I have no question about the integrity and the honesty of administrative [Wynn]."* The compensating control the NY State Comptroller's office describes is not a courtesy review. It is an adversarial check — a board member examining the Treasurer's work with the willingness to question what is found. A reviewer who has "complete faith" and "no question" is not, by definition, performing that function.

This is the substance of the citizen's concern, and it is exactly the kind of question the GFOA award is silent on. The award is a credential of *presentation*, not of *control*. It certifies that the Town's budget document is well organized and properly disclosed. It does not certify that any one person cannot initiate, approve, record, and disclose a transaction without an independent check anywhere in the chain.

When a citizen raises an oversight concern at a public meeting, the appropriate response is to engage the substance — to point to the specific controls that demonstrate the answer to the question being asked. Citing a paid presentation credential issued by a professional trade association is a category error. The credential the Mayor invoked is not designed to do what she asked it to do.

**The narrower point:** The Mayor said "our last two budgets." GFOA's records show one.

**The broader point:** Even if the count had been right, the credential is a paid award for budget *formatting*, issued by a trade association, recipient by ~1,900 governments per year. Citizens at public meetings deserve substantive answers to oversight questions, not credentials that the awarding body itself says don't measure what the citizen is asking about.
