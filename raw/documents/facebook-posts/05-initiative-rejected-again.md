It happened again.

On March 27, I submitted a citizen initiative to ban surveillance cameras on public property in Paonia. Today, April 3, the Town Clerk rejected it.

This is the second citizen initiative she has rejected. The first was my short-term rental initiative last May. Same clerk. Same result. Same framework: call everything "administrative" and refuse to let citizens vote.

Let me walk you through their reasons and what my initiative actually says.

They say my initiative violates the single-subject rule because it covers surveillance cameras, contracts, body-worn cameras, and judicial remedies. But it's one initiative about one subject: whether the Town of Paonia should be allowed to surveil its citizens with cameras on public property. The exceptions, enforcement, and amendment provisions are standard parts of any ordinance. By this logic, every law ever written violates single-subject because it has definitions, enforcement, and severability clauses.

They say my initiative regulates body-worn cameras and conflicts with state law. Here's what my initiative actually says, Section 4(a): "Body-worn cameras on law enforcement officers, governed by separate policy" are EXEMPT from the ban. That's an exemption. The opposite of regulation. I explicitly carved out body cams to avoid any conflict with state law. The Clerk is citing state preemption for a provision that says "this doesn't apply to body cams."

Sound familiar? Last May, my STR initiative said "no fees." The Clerk said I was "dictating fees" and rejected it as administrative. Now my surveillance initiative says "body cams are exempt" and the Clerk says I'm "regulating body cams." The pattern is: mischaracterize what the initiative says, then reject it based on the mischaracterization.

They say surveillance of public property is an "administrative function" that citizens can't legislate. Whether the government gets to point cameras at you in your town park is not an administrative detail. It's a fundamental policy question about the relationship between a government and its citizens. Colorado's own legislature passed laws restricting government use of facial recognition and biometric data (SB 22-113, HB 24-1130). If the state legislature can restrict government surveillance through legislation, citizens can do the same through initiative. That's what Article V, Section 1 of the Colorado Constitution guarantees.

They say voiding contracts that violate the ordinance is "administrative." Legislatures restrict government contracting in every jurisdiction in the country. Procurement rules, prevailing wage laws, conflict-of-interest provisions. A clause that says "contracts violating this law are void" is a standard enforcement mechanism found in thousands of ordinances.

They say allowing citizens to seek injunctive relief is a "judicial function." Every ordinance with an enforcement provision creates a legal remedy. That's what legislatures do. They create causes of action. Courts then exercise their judicial function by hearing the cases. The Clerk is confusing writing a law with enforcing one.

Two initiatives submitted. Two initiatives rejected. Zero initiatives allowed to go to the voters.

Meanwhile, the Town found time to deploy $50,000 in Verkada surveillance cameras with zero public comment, approve Daxbot sidewalk robots without public notice, and call a special meeting within five days to respond to a flyer about the budget. But they can't find a way to let citizens vote on whether they want to be surveilled in their own town park.

I am filing a formal response demanding acceptance within 5 business days. If they refuse, I will seek judicial review in Delta County District Court. I will also be resubmitting this initiative as separate single-subject petitions, removing their only arguable objection.

They can reject as many initiatives as they want. I'll keep submitting them. The question of whether this town should surveil its citizens belongs to the citizens, not to the Town Clerk.

Pete McCarthy
119 1/2 Dorris Ave, Paonia
