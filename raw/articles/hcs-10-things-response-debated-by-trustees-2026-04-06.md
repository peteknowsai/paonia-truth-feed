# 10 Things Response Debated by Trustees

A flyer distributed around town and posted on the Paonia Facebook Message Boards titled “10 Things Paonia Should Know About Its Town Administrator” led to a special meeting of the Paonia Board of Trustees on Monday, March 30.

The post, written by former Paonia Planning Commissioner Pete McCarthy, stated that Town Administrator Stefen Wynn had been fired from a job in Neptune Beach, Florida, for “flagrant neglect of duty.” The post characterized the town’s new security camera system as surveillance; complained that the town is allowing “century-old trees” to die; stated that Wynn was solely in charge of the town’s coffers and implied that Wynn was misusing funds because “savings are disappearing.” 

The flyer prompted trustee Lucy Hunter to write and distribute a personal response to the ten points. Hunter confirmed that Wynn was dismissed from the Neptune Beach job, but said the reason was that he was a whistleblower who reported financial fraud and mismanagement. She explained that the Paonia security cameras were installed to combat persistent vandalism of town property; the “savings” are capital funds from grants and low-interest loans, being spent down to repair town infrastructure; and the trees are slated to be evaluated by an arborist.
        

Then, Mayor Paige Smith wrote a draft letter of rebuttal to the “10 Things” post, saying it contains “deliberately damaging and inaccurate attempted ‘gotchas,’  meant to denigrate the town administrator.” 

Smith wrote that Wynn “has in fact served our community with distinction, integrity, and professionalism.” The trustees recently gave Wynn a merit raise based on his performance over the past year. 

She asked the trustees to approve publishing the lengthy letter as a display ad in all three of the county’s newspapers, at an estimated cost of about $2,400, saying that “If this unfounded negativity is left unanswered, then perception becomes reality and a lasting impression may be erroneously created that we are operating outside of the public interest.” She was particularly concerned about the town’s many grants and low-interest loans slated for capital projects, saying that such charges, if not refuted, could  “make us a lending risk in the present and into the future.”

Smith stressed that the town's current financial systems to guard against malfeasance include two sets of internal controls and oversight by the board’s finance committee.

The mayor opened the special meeting by asking for public comment, handling the crowd of perhaps, 35 attendees with a firm but polite hand. Seventeen people spoke, including the flyer's author, Pete McCarthy.

The comments focused on several points: the mayor’s letter and whether the town should spend $2,400 to publish it; the security cameras the town has installed in the park, at town hall, and several other locations; the charges of financial malfeasance made by McCarthy against Wynn; and what should happen to the older trees in Paonia. Short-term rental issues, a lack of communication from the trustees to the public, and the departure of former town employee Kaja Bowman were also topics. Following is a selection of comments. 

Lisa Short: “Last year, this board chose not to waive the $2,500 fee for the Learning Council to hold the farmers market in town park, something that clearly benefits Paonia, both economically and socially. The board cited discomfort with spending taxpayer dollars on something that not all citizens agreed was a worthwhile event. Now, we're in a position where a similar amount of money is being considered to fund newspaper responses to a citizen flyer based on publicly available records. That contrast is hard to ignore.”
        

Emily Foster: “You have a PR problem. We know that. It needs to be addressed. But I don't think this is the way to do it. I don't think this is the way any of us want to see our taxpayer money spent. I don't think this is actually going to solve anything. I think it's pouring gasoline on the fire, and we're not actually fostering the type of conversation that's going to resolve and get to the bottom of the real serious issues that we have here in terms of the divisions in this community.

Rodney Byrge (Paonia Public Works employee): “I want to say thank you for approving the security cameras. I don't think people know that just today, our ballot box was vandalized and almost destroyed. Because of those cameras, we will know who did it. The BMW rally paid for a remodel of the town park restrooms. Within a week of that remodel, a group of locals set it on fire, broke brand new toilets, and graffitied it. The cameras are there to hold people accountable for the crimes that are happening here that nobody talks about, that don't make the news. It is running rampant in this town, and that's why they're there.”

Pete McCarthy: “If you look at my posts, you will see they are well-researched. Yours are not. You just call me a liar, basically, which is defamatory, by the way, if we want to start throwing out legal terms. I would be a little more circumspect in the words you use to describe me. I will not back down. I will make sure that all of this comes to light. Take your full page out. Do whatever you want to do. I'm going to be right here. I'm going to hold you all to account.”

Jess Dervin-Ackerman: “We need to be able to have discourse where people don't agree. especially in this moment. We are a community in the North Fork Valley that is very diverse ideologically. And we need to be able to be neighbors, to support each other in emergencies. And that takes these kinds of skills. So I will just say, I'm here to learn alongside everybody to do that better. And I'm here to commit to that as a member of this community.”

At the conclusion of the public input, the trustees spoke. 

“I do think the (mayor’s) letter includes some assertions that aren’t necessary, that aren’t necessarily factual, and that aren’t helpful,” said Karen Tarnow. “If this letter is to go forward, we should take another swipe at it, dissect fact from assertion, and just be real clean about putting information out there.”

“In listening to people, I am leaning more toward not spending money,” said Trustee Hunter. She suggested “posting the letter on the town webpage and the Town of Paonia Facebook page, using official town avenues to get this out.”

After passing a motion to that effect, the board agreed to revise the Mayor’s letter, and Tarnow and Smith edited it on the fly until it was free of the assertions that concerned Tarnow.

The mayor’s letter is available on the town website, and all material from the special meeting, including McCarthy’s post and the mayor’s original draft, is in the board’s March 30, 2026, packet. A video recording of the meeting is available on the town’s YouTube channel.
