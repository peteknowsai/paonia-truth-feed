The Paonia Procurement Map

How the Town of Paonia bought things from January 2024 through May 2026 — every contract, change order, task order and sole-source award that appears in 76 Board of Trustees meeting packets, measured against the Town's own purchasing rules. What the pattern shows, and — just as carefully — what it does not.

Compiled May 14, 2026 · 76 meeting packets · ~150 contracting actions · $2.95M of itemized disbursements

Read this first — the rigor rule

A single procurement document cannot tell you whether a bad outcome was caused by disorganization or by deliberate steering — the paperwork looks identical either way. This document's job is to gather the evidence that would distinguish them, not to declare a verdict the evidence can't support. Where you see the word "flag," read it as "a question worth answering," not "a finding of wrongdoing." The conclusion in Section 11 says exactly how far the evidence goes and where it stops.

1The bottom line

Across two and a half years and 76 meetings, the Town of Paonia ran a procurement system with two distinct faces.

On hard, visible commodity and construction work — a water-tank rehab, janitorial service, street signs, an IT contract, playground chips — the Town frequently did competitively bid, drew real multi-firm fields, and took the low bid. That half is on the record and it matters.

On high-value professional services and the big infrastructure-engineering and security contracts — legal, engineering, finance, HR, the water-program consultants, the camera system — the competitive process required by the Town's own code was bypassed again and again: through sole-source awards, through "suspend the rules" motions, through change orders that doubled contracts without a fresh bid, and through amounts sized to sit just under the threshold where the rules switch on.

That selective pattern runs through a single chokepoint. The Town Administrator/Treasurer — hired in 2023 with no competitive search of his own, and the author of the very purchasing policy that names him as the sole-source approver — is the presenter, recommender, or signatory on the large majority of the no-bid awards. And at least five vendors in the pattern have documented personal or insider ties to Town officials, including one where the relationship is written into the sole-source justification itself.

What the evidence supports saying

The competitive-bid process — mandated by the Town's own Municipal Code and by Colorado statute — is routinely bypassed on exactly the contracts where it matters most, in ways that consistently favor a small set of out-of-area firms and pre-existing relationships, with one official as the single point of control over the entire chain.

What the evidence does NOT support saying

It does not show money flowing back to any official, a kickback, or a proven quid-pro-quo. "Pay-for-play" remains a hypothesis the pattern is consistent with — not a demonstrated fact. The disorganization is also genuine and pervasive, and disorganization can produce every red flag below on its own. Section 12 lists the specific things that would move this from "steering-shaped pattern" to "proven."

2How this was built

Every regular, special, and work-session packet of the Paonia Board of Trustees from January 9, 2024 through May 12, 2026 was retrieved directly from the Town's CivicClerk portal — 76 packets in all, the entire tenure of the current Town Administrator plus a baseline. Each was converted to text and read for every instance of money leaving the Town to an outside party: contracts, change orders, task orders, professional-services agreements, bid awards, quotes, leases, and the bi-weekly disbursement registers.

3The yardstick: the Town's own rules

The standard this map measures against is not an outside opinion. It is the Town of Paonia's own Purchasing Policy — drafted by the Town Administrator, adopted as Ordinance 2024-03 / Resolution 2024-14 on September 17, 2024, and now embedded in Municipal Code Chapter 4-6. Its thresholds:

Purchase valueWhat the Town's own policy requires
to $1,000Nothing. No requisition, no competition.
$1,001–$5,000Three documented quotes attached to the requisition.
$5,001–$10,000Three written quotes plus prior Town Administrator approval. The Administrator may waive the quote requirement in writing.
$10,001 +A formal, public bid process — solicitation posted ~30 days — plus Board of Trustees approval.
sole-source > $5,000Board approval required, after a favorable Administrator recommendation; a written Sole Source Justification form is required at $10,001+.
change ordersA project of $50,000+ with a change-order increase of 10%+ requires Administrator approval.

Two more provisions matter enormously, because the pattern in Section 5 runs straight into them:

The Town's policy bans exactly what the pattern does

• Anti-splitting (Manual §2.3): "Departments are forbidden to artificially divide purchase requisitions to circumvent any competitive bidding requirement."
• Consequence (Town Code): "Purchases outside this process are void and unenforceable under the Town Code."

Behind the Town's own rule sits Colorado statute. C.R.S. § 31-15-712 requires competitive bidding for public-improvement construction at a far lower line — $5,000 — and calls dividing a project to dodge that requirement "unlawful." (The section is captioned "cities"; whether it binds a statutory town like Paonia directly is a question for the Town Attorney — but the Town's own $10,001 policy applies regardless, and is the yardstick used throughout this document.)

Sources: Paonia Purchasing Policy Manual / Ord. 2024-03, transcribed verbatim in the 09-23-2025 and 12-09-2025 packets · C.R.S. § 31-15-712 (Justia / FindLaw / 2024 CRS Title 31).

4The procurement chain

Every pattern in this document narrows to one node. The Town of Paonia consolidated its Town Administrator and Town Treasurer into a single position (Resolutions 2023-07 and 2023-08) and on July 11, 2023 appointed Stefen Wynn to hold both.

The first no-bid was the hire itself

No competitive-search record, candidate pool, interview process, or selection-committee documentation for that appointment appears anywhere in the public record reviewed. The Town hired him roughly six months after the Neptune Beach, Florida city council voted unanimously to fire him for "flagrant neglect of duty" — the cited trigger there being a failure to ensure permitting for a water tower. Paonia's signature infrastructure crisis of this period is a water tank.

From that seat, the same official:

  • Wrote the purchasing policy — by the Town's own account, "through our Town Administrator's own initiative" — the policy that names the Town Administrator/Treasurer as the sole-source approver and the waiver authority.
  • Is the presenter, recommender, submitter, or signatory on the large majority of the contracting actions in this map — and was a scoring-panel member on the competitive RFPs too.
  • Prepares the annual budget that contains his own compensation (the Board approves it).
  • Is one of only two "Position 1" signers on every Town bank account.
  • Is the recurring authorized executor — the motions repeatedly read "authorize the Town Administrator to execute all necessary documents."

None of that is, by itself, evidence of wrongdoing. Concentrating procurement authority in a small-town administrator is common and often unavoidable. It is recorded here because it is the structural precondition that makes every other pattern possible, and because the same person occupies every checkpoint that would otherwise catch the others.

5Eight patterns

These are the recurring shapes in the record. Each is documented; each also carries the most innocent reading available, because both readings produce the same paperwork.

1 — Professional services are essentially never competed

Legal, engineering, finance, planning, HR — across the entire window, the Town's high-value professional-services engagements rode existing relationships, "master agreements," referrals, or sole-source memos. Not one of the following had a documented competitive solicitation: the Town Attorney (Buchner; Kelly PC before him; the NSW water-attorney engagement; Butler Snow bond counsel), the standing water engineers (RESPEC, SGM), the finance contractor (Professional Management Solutions), the HR firm (New Focus HR), and the planning consultants (Phoenix Rising, Urban Rural Continuum, Sustainable Futures).

The fair reading

A town this size has few local specialty firms, and continuity with an engineer who knows the system has real value. But the Town's own policy still requires a formal bid above $10,001, and "we already know them" is not one of its listed exceptions.

2 — Change orders are the bid-avoidance mechanism

The single most consistent finding. Contracts are awarded at a modest figure, then enlarged — sometimes past double — through a sequence of change orders, none of which is ever competitively tested. See Section 6 for the four worst threads. The headline: a $97,860 tank-inspection contract that reached $200,713, and a $64,634 design contract that reached $197,734.

3 — "Not to exceed" ceilings are padded ~25% above the vendor's own quote

When the Board sets a not-to-exceed number, it has repeatedly set it about a quarter above the documented price:

  • SGM construction-management scope — approved at $384,058 against SGM's own quoted total of $307,789 (+24.8%).
  • Misco Water lagoon aerators — approved at $132,000 against a staff estimate of ~$105,846 (+25%).

A ceiling set that far above the real number is room a project can grow into without ever going back to the Board.

4 — Contracts move before the Board votes

Repeatedly, the vote was a formality after the fact:

  • Two RESPEC water awards signed by the vendor (Aug 15 and Aug 21, 2024) before the Aug 27 Board meeting.
  • The Sustainable Futures code-rewrite contract carried an "effective date" before the Board approved it.
  • A $1.5M VoTech building-purchase offer signed by the Mayor before any Board action (the Board later had to terminate it).
  • Rundle Construction's Notice of Award was issued December 15, 2025 — before the Board's late-December presentation and January ratification.
  • Roop Excavating's ~$30,000 tie-in work was, per the minutes, "done ahead of formal approval."
  • Earthworx change orders effective — and one invoiced — before the Board "accepted" them; the City of Delta inspection contract ratified after the prior one had lapsed.

5 — Amounts cluster at the thresholds; scopes get split

The recurring round number is $10,000 — the exact line above which the Town's competitive process and its sole-source-justification requirement both switch on. The Urban Rural Continuum addendum and the SGM temporary-tank task order were each written at a tidy $10,000.00. The Net2Phone phone contract and the Istonish firewall ($9,290) were each explicitly justified in the staff report as "less than $10,001" — therefore no competition required. In 2024 a cluster of no-bid software buys landed just under $25,000 (iWorQ $17,600, RESPEC task order $21,600, Caselle $21,610). The SGM tank initiative was presented as a $10,000 design task now with a $212,300 tank purchase held back as a "Decision Later." The Smith Center was approved as one "up to $50,000" bucket spanning three separate scopes and vendors.

Against the Town's own words

The Town's policy expressly forbids "artificially divid[ing] purchase requisitions to circumvent any competitive bidding requirement." Splitting a $222,300 tank project into a $10K task and a $212K "later," or a $50K building rehab into sub-scopes, is the shape that rule describes.

6 — Big-ticket items procured by "suspending the rules"

  • Verkada camera system, $50,288 — the August 12, 2025 motion expressly waived Town Code §4-6-80 "allowing for a sole source bid." A code waiver, on the record, to skip competition.
  • TYMCO street sweeper, $394,500 — the Board "suspended purchasing rules, answered 8 sole-source questions in the affirmative." The "comparison" offered was two other cities buying the same sole-source vendor — not competing brands.
  • Precision Concrete Cutting, $42,831 — declared sole-source on a single staff sentence, with no written Sole Source Justification form, though the Town's own policy requires one above $10,001.

7 — Vendors with personal or insider ties to Town officials

At least five. They get their own section — Section 8 — because this is the pattern that most cleanly separates "disorganized" from "steered."

8 — The paperwork does not hold together

Disorganization is itself a finding, because a procurement system this loose cannot reliably catch steering even if someone looked. A partial list from the record:

  • One SGM change order appears with three different dollar figures ($32,500 / $35,200 / $40,000) across the agenda, the attached memo, and a scorecard.
  • The water-tank construction award: staff report says $916,250, the motion the Board actually passed says $957,750 — a $41,500 gap, unexplained.
  • The Safe-Streets consultant: the staff report and scoring sheet name KLJ as the top-scoring firm; the motion awards the work to Consor — which does not appear in the 8-firm scoring table at all.
  • A KLM change-order contract was drafted naming "The Landmark Resort" as the counterparty; the Board had to fix it by motion.
  • A RESPEC invoice booked at $450 was actually $10,419.95 — a 2,200% understatement caught only by chance before checks printed.
  • Multi-decade leases presented with blank staff reports; change orders approved with no dollar figure in the packet; the bi-weekly disbursement register absent from most packets entirely.

8The relationship vendors

Disorganization explains a missing form. It does not explain a vendor with a personal connection to the official who picked them. The record contains at least five.

New Focus HR — the relationship is in the sole-source file itself

In June 2025 the Town sole-sourced its HR work — a $7,500 handbook, a $150/yr subscription, and open-ended HR consulting at $175/hour with no ceiling — to New Focus HR, an Indianapolis, Indiana firm. The contract was signed by Stefen Wynn. The Town's required "Sole Source Justification" attachment is an email from the vendor, and it reads, to Wynn: "Stefen, I hope that you are enjoying your time in Indy? We look forward to attending the race on Sunday, although the temperatures may be a bit cool compared to past races."

Wynn's career and degree are from Indiana — Town Manager of Albion, Indiana (2016–2019); MPA from Indiana University South Bend. New Focus HR is Indiana-domiciled. The document the Town used to justify skipping competition is, on its face, evidence of a pre-existing personal relationship between the buyer and the seller. And it is not a one-off: New Focus HR appears in 12 packets through February 2026. The firm advertises clients in Florida and Colorado — the two states Wynn worked in after Indiana; whether it followed him job-to-job is the open question.

Each of these, individually, has an innocent explanation — a small town has a small vendor pool, and recusal was noted in the car-wash case. But the Town's own purchasing policy contains a conflict-of-interest rule for exactly this situation, and the question the map raises is not "is any one of these damning" — it is "why are there five, and why does the procurement system have no working filter for them."

9What cuts the other way

An honest map shows the evidence against its own thesis. There is real counter-evidence, and it is the reason this document does not allege corruption.

Competitive bidding genuinely happens — and it happened a lot

  • The 2MG water tank construction itself drew 3 sealed bids; the low bidder won.
  • The Astonish IT contract ran a real RFQ that drew 10 proposals with a staff scoring panel.
  • The ADA-consultant selection scored 7 firms; the lowest-cost firm also scored highest and won.
  • The janitorial contract, the tank-pad site civil work, the Hemlock brush-clearing RFP, playground chips, an electrician ($6,763 low bid of 3), and the Brimar street signs ($3,182 low bid of 3) were all competitively procured.
  • In August 2025 the Town opened four RFQs at once — and the Rundle Construction culvert contract carries a competitive Notice of Award.

Other facts that cut against a steering reading:

  • The pattern is selective, not total. "Wynn touches everything" is false — the cleanest, most competitive awards are frequently the ones other staff (Public Works, the Deputy Treasurer, the Clerk) ran.
  • The Board shows friction. It terminated the VoTech offer, a trustee dissented on a Phoenix Rising change order, and the Board overrode staff to pick the in-budget garbage-cart option over the over-budget one staff recommended.
  • Some sole-sources are legitimately grantor-driven — the Urban Rural Continuum housing addendum was sole-sourced because the state grantor (DOLA) authorized it.
  • Out-of-area is not inherently suspicious — a town of 1,500 genuinely has no local tank-coating inspector or water-rights attorney.
  • The change-order causes are often specific and plausible — buried concrete tanks, an unexpected cold snap, a contractor who demobilized. Subsurface surprises on an old tank site are real.

The control that is also a vendor

When residents asked who checks the Town's books, the Board's answer named Professional Management Solutions as its third-party financial "internal control." The ledger shows PMS is also a paid vendor of the Town — 55 separate invoices over the period — and its multi-year finance contract was never competitively bid. A firm cannot be an independent check on the entity that is, at the same time, one of its recurring clients. This is a structural conflict, not an accusation — but it is the kind of structural conflict a functioning procurement system is supposed to prevent.

11The verdict

It is both, and they are entangled.

There is a genuinely disorganized procurement shop here — conflicting figures, blank staff reports, a contract naming the wrong town, an invoice off by 2,200%, registers missing from packets. That disorganization is real, and on its own it can generate every individual red flag in this document.

But disorganization is random. It does not have a direction. And this pattern has a direction: competition is bypassed specifically on the high-value professional-services and infrastructure contracts; the beneficiaries are a stable, small set of out-of-area firms and pre-existing relationships; the mechanism — change orders, threshold-hugging, "suspend the rules," sign-first-vote-later — recurs with the consistency of a practice, not an accident; and it all flows through one official who also wrote the rules and occupies every checkpoint that would catch it. Random disorganization does not reliably produce five vendors with personal ties to Town officials, or a sole-source justification that is literally an email about going to the races together.

What the evidence establishes: Paonia's competitive-bid process — required by its own Municipal Code — is not a meaningful constraint on its largest contracts. It is bypassed routinely, selectively, and through repeatable methods, in favor of relationships, by a single point of control. That is a serious governance failure on the public record right now, regardless of motive. What the evidence does not yet establish is motive: there is no proof in these 76 packets of money flowing back to any official. The honest finding is a steering-shaped pattern — and a clear, finite list of what would confirm or dispel the steering itself.

12What we still don't know — and how to find out

These are the specific, answerable questions that separate "steering-shaped pattern" from "proven." None requires litigation; most require only public records or out-of-town digging.

  1. Did New Focus HR follow Wynn? Whether that Indiana HR firm billed Albion, Indiana while Wynn ran it (2016–2019), or Neptune Beach, Florida during his tenure. If a vendor tracked an administrator across three states, that is no longer a coincidence. (Public records in two other towns — no Paonia CORA needed.)
  2. The original contracts that aren't in the packets — the original KLM ($97,860), Markley's Precision ($916,250), Viaflex (~$320K), and Earthworx (~$374K) agreements, and the RESPEC/SGM master engineering agreements under which task orders are issued sole-source. Were any of them competitively bid?
  3. The Professional Management Solutions contract — its term, its price, and how a firm became both the Town's finance contractor and its cited "internal control."
  4. The Verkada contradiction — the April 2026 memo called it a "state bid"; the actual August 2025 motion "waived Code §4-6-80" for a sole-source. Both cannot be true. Get the 8/12/2025 minutes and the Mitchell & Company contract.
  5. The KLJ → Consor substitution — why the Safe Streets contract went to a firm that was not in the scoring table.
  6. Cumulative SGM billings across every task order in the Wynn period — SGM both serves as the Town's engineer and reviews and recommends the change orders of the other contractors it works alongside.
  7. The Town Administrator's exact spending-authority dollar limit — referenced repeatedly, never stated. Pull Municipal Code Chapter 4-6 in full.
  8. The disbursement registers missing from ~50 packets — the native PDFs would complete the vendor ledger and show whether the missing registers hide anything the present ones don't.
  9. The open threads — the $212,300 temporary-tank purchase; which vendors actually get paid out of the Smith Center $50,000; the STR facilitator's final fee; the ~$1M BRIC generator procurement. Watch whether each goes to bid.
