A neighbor's notes · Town of Paonia

Fair Questions

I read about two years of Town of Paonia board packets — every contract, every spending vote, January 2024 through May 2026. Most of what's in them is exactly what you'd expect: routine, sensible, fine. This is not a list of accusations. It's the short list of things that genuinely made me stop and go "huh" — and the plain questions I think deserve a plain answer.

Before anything else, three things I want to be clear about:

One — the vast majority of Town spending is legitimate, necessary, and properly handled. Payroll, utilities, fuel, insurance, road salt. I'm not pointing at any of that. The Town also runs real competitive bids all the time — the IT contract drew ten proposals, the big tank-construction job was bid out, even the street signs got three quotes. Credit where it's due.

Two — every single item below might have a perfectly good explanation. That is literally why they are questions and not charges. If staff has a clean answer, wonderful — that's the whole point of asking. A board that asks good questions and gets good answers is a board doing its job.

Three — I'm not an accountant and you don't need to be one to follow this. If you can follow "did we shop around before we spent the money," you can follow all of it.

First, how buying is supposed to work

The Town has its own written purchasing policy. The short version: small stuff, staff can just buy. But once a purchase crosses about $10,000, it's supposed to go out for competitive bids and come to the Board for approval. There's also a rule against splitting one big job into little pieces to duck that line.

Competitive bidding isn't red tape. It's the thing that lets a trustee look a taxpayer in the eye and say "we got the best price we could find" — instead of "we used a company we already knew." When the bidding step gets skipped, you haven't necessarily been robbed. You've just lost the only proof you weren't. Every question below is really the same question: did we shop around — and if not, why not?

The questions

Question 1

The HR firm from Indiana

In mid-2025 the Town needed help rewriting its employee handbook and getting ongoing HR advice. Reasonable thing to need. Instead of getting a few quotes, the Town hired — sole-source, no competition — a firm called New Focus HR. It's in Indianapolis, Indiana. The deal was $7,500 for the handbook plus ongoing consulting at $175 an hour with no ceiling on it, and the firm has shown up in the Town's packets a dozen times since.

Why a firm from Indiana, for a small town in Colorado? Here's the part worth knowing: the Town Administrator built his career in Indiana — he was a town manager there, and his graduate degree is from an Indiana university.

When you skip competitive bidding, the policy says you have to file a "sole source justification" — a document explaining why there was genuinely no other choice. The Town filed one. It's an email. From the vendor. And it opens: "Stefen, I hope that you are enjoying your time in Indy? We look forward to attending the race on Sunday... compared to past races."

A sole-source justification is supposed to answer one question — why was there no other option. This one answers a different question entirely: how do the buyer and the seller know each other.

The question for staff

  • How was New Focus HR identified, and did anyone solicit a competing quote?
  • Does the Town Administrator have a prior personal or professional relationship with this firm or its owner — and did this firm do work for him at his previous jobs in Indiana or Florida?
  • That open-ended $175/hour consulting — what's it cost the Town so far, and is there any cap?

Question 2

A habit of hiring people connected to Town Hall

Once you notice the HR thing, you start noticing it's not alone. Across these two years, several Town vendors turn out to have a personal connection to a Town official:

The vendorThe connection
Paonia Car WashOwned by the Town's own Police Chief. The Town signed a contract with it. (The dollar amount never made it into the minutes.)
Sustainable FuturesRun by a former Interim Town Administrator of Paonia — given the $38,000 job to rewrite the Town code.
Grit and GraceDescribed in the Town's own staff report as "a local company owned by a PD officer's wife." Became the Police Department's sticker vendor.
Farnsworth ConstructionThe contractor's own note in the packet mentions the Town Administrator already had its insurance certificate "from my project for a private residence" — i.e. it had done private work at his home.

Now — this is a small town. Everybody is connected to everybody. Your plumber coaches your kid's soccer team. That, by itself, is not the problem, and I want to be fair about that.

The problem isn't that people know each other. It's when "we know a guy" quietly becomes the procurement process — and nobody wrote down that they noticed.

The Town's own purchasing policy has a conflict-of-interest section for exactly these moments. The question isn't whether any one of these is shady. It's whether the system has any working filter at all.

The question for staff

  • For each of these, is there a written conflict-of-interest disclosure on file, and were competing quotes obtained?
  • Who makes the call when a potential Town vendor is connected to a Town official — and what's the process?

Question 3

The outside firm that checks the books is also on the payroll

When residents have asked "who independently checks the Town's finances?", the Town's answer has named an outside firm — Professional Management Solutions — as one of its financial controls. Good idea in principle: an independent set of eyes.

Except the Town's own check register shows Professional Management Solutions is also just... a regular paid vendor. It appears 55 separate times in the disbursements I could read, and its multi-year contract was never competitively bid.

Having Professional Management Solutions vouch for the Town's books, while Professional Management Solutions is one of the Town's contractors, is a little like having your brother-in-law appraise the car he's selling you. He might be honest. But that's not an independent check — that's a colleague.

The question for staff

  • What exactly does Professional Management Solutions do for the Town — bookkeeping, or oversight, or both?
  • Was its contract ever put out to bid? And if PMS helps prepare the books, who independently checks PMS?

Question 4

Twice, the Board was asked to vote to set its own rules aside

Two of the biggest single purchases in this whole period came to the Board with a recommended motion that, in plain English, said: skip the normal purchasing rules for this one.

The Verkada camera system (about $50,000) was approved by a motion that expressly "waive[d]" the Town's purchasing code to allow a no-bid purchase. The street sweeper (about $394,000) was approved after the Board "suspended purchasing rules" and answered a set of "sole-source questions" yes. In the sweeper's case, the "comparison shopping" presented was two other towns buying from the same vendor — not other brands.

When the motion in your packet starts with the words "waive the purchasing code" or "suspend the rules" — that's not boilerplate to read past on your way to the vote. On a big purchase, that is the vote.

The question for staff

  • For each of these — why couldn't it be competitively bid? What specifically made it sole-source?
  • The cameras were described elsewhere as "vetted through a state bid process." Is there a document showing that — a contract number, a price comparison — or just the assertion?

Question 5

The vote keeps arriving after the handshake

Several times, the deal was effectively done before the Board ever voted on it. A $1.5 million offer to buy a building was signed by the Mayor before the Board had acted (the Board later had to un-do it). The contractor on a culvert job had its "Notice of Award" before the Board's ratification vote. The code-rewrite contract had an effective date that pre-dated its approval. On another job, the minutes flatly say the work was "done ahead of formal approval."

If the ink's already dry when it reaches the Board, the vote isn't a decision anymore — it's a photograph of one. And a board that's only ever handed photographs isn't actually steering the car.

The question for staff

  • How often are contracts signed, or work started, before the Board votes?
  • What is the Town's rule on committing to a vendor before Board approval — and who is allowed to do it?

Question 6

Numbers that land suspiciously close to the line

Remember the roughly-$10,000 line — under it, no competitive bidding required. A couple of purchases were written up in the staff report with that line stated out loud as the reason no bidding happened: a phone-system contract, and a firewall purchase priced at $9,290 — explicitly justified as "less than $10,001, so no competitive process required." A couple of consulting jobs landed at a tidy, round $10,000.00 exactly.

$9,290. Not $11,000. Not $7,000. $9,290 — which happens to be $711 under the line where you'd have had to get competing bids. Maybe that's just what a firewall costs. But it's worth asking who's doing the arithmetic, and which direction they're rounding.

The question for staff

  • Who sets these dollar amounts, and how?
  • Has any larger job ever been broken into smaller pieces — each one conveniently under the bidding line? (The Town's own policy forbids that.)

Question 7

The contest where the announced winner wasn't the scored winner

Here's one that's simple to follow. The Town ran a real, proper competition for a grant consultant — wrote up a request, got proposals from eight firms, scored them. The staff report and the scoring sheet named the top scorer: a firm called KLJ. Good — that's the system working.

Then, at the next meeting, the Board's motion awarded the work to a different firm — Consor — which doesn't appear in the eight-firm scoring table at all.

If you're going to run a scored competition, the scored winner should win. When the trophy goes to someone who wasn't on the scoreboard, the obvious question is: what happened between those two meetings?

The question for staff

  • The scoring named KLJ as the top firm. The award went to Consor. Why — and who decided that, and where is it documented?

Question 8

One person is wearing a lot of hats

None of the items above is necessarily wrong on its own. But step back and notice who's standing at the center of all of them. The Town Administrator is also the Town Treasurer — one person, two jobs that usually check each other. He wrote the purchasing policy the Town now follows — the policy that names the Town Administrator as the person who approves sole-source purchases. He's the one who presents, recommends, or signs most of the bigger contracts. And his own hire, into that combined role, has no competitive search anywhere in the record.

This isn't an accusation — it's a diagram. When the same person is the referee, a player, and the person who wrote the rulebook, even the most honest official in the world is operating without a safety net. And nobody should want to operate without one.

The question for the Board

  • Should the Treasurer role — the one that's supposed to be a check on spending — sit with someone other than the Administrator who does the spending?
  • Should sole-source approvals require a second signature from outside the Administrator's office?

What I'm not saying

I'm not saying anyone broke the law. I'm not saying money was stolen. I haven't seen a dollar flow into anyone's pocket, and I'm not implying one did. I know most of the Town's spending is ordinary and well-run, and I know small towns work on trust and relationships — that's part of why we live here.

What I am saying is narrower and, I think, fair: on a recurring set of bigger purchases, the competitive-bidding step — the step that protects the public — got skipped, and it got skipped in ways that kept landing near the same people and the same out-of-town firms. Maybe every one of those has a clean explanation. Then let's hear it.

What would actually help

Not a fight. Not a flyer. Just this: a trustee asks these questions, out loud, in a public meeting, and staff answers them on the record. That's it. If the answers are good, the Town comes out stronger and I'll happily say so. If the answers are thin — well, then we'll all have learned something worth knowing. Either way, asking is the job.

Compiled from 76 Town of Paonia Board of Trustees meeting packets (Jan 2024 – May 2026), the Town's adopted Purchasing Policy, and the Town's own disbursement records — all public, all retrievable from the Town's CivicClerk portal. Every item above traces to a specific dated packet. This is a list of questions, offered in good faith, by someone who would genuinely like to hear the answers.
