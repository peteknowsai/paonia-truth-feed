A single contract, taken apart

The HR Contract, Explained

How the Town of Paonia hired an Indiana firm to run its Human Resources — and why the paper trail keeps raising the same question.

This page is built for one purpose: so you can see clearly what happened. It is not the Facebook post and not for publishing — it's the picture behind it. Everything here is tagged so you always know how solid it is:
Documented = in the Town's own packet or the firm's own website   Inference = the documents point here, but it's interpretation   Not established = an open question — do not treat as fact

The 30-second version.

In June 2025 the Town of Paonia hired New Focus HR — a firm in Noblesville, Indiana, about 1,200 miles away — to rewrite its employee handbook and be its outsourced HR department. Documented

There was no competitive bid. The contract is $7,500 up front, $150/year forever, and $175/hour of open-ended consulting with no cap. Documented

The document the Town filed to justify skipping the bid is an email chain in which the vendor and the Town Administrator chat about meeting up at the race in Indianapolis — and the contract was already signed and dated before that justification was even started. Documented

The Town Administrator built his whole career in Indiana. The firm's every client and reference is in Indiana. Whether that's why this happened is the open question — but it's a fair one, and the paper trail is what makes it fair. Inference

The vendor
Kristen Deutsch

President of New Focus HR, LLC — Noblesville, Indiana. Sent Paonia the proposal, signed it first, and — this matters — wrote the answers to the Town's own "why no bid" questionnaire herself.

Submitted the staff report
Samira Vetter

Town Clerk. Met Deutsch on May 21, 2025; brought the item to the Board on June 10. Her staff report vouches for the firm's municipal experience — a firm she had known for about 20 days.

Signed for the Town
Stefen Wynn

Town Administrator & Treasurer. The contract's signatory. The vendor's emails are addressed to him first. He spent 2016–2019 as Town Manager of Albion, Indiana; his graduate degree is from Indiana University.

2The geography problem

Start with the thing that should make anyone blink. Paonia hired an HR firm on the other side of the country.

PAONIA, CO
pop. ~1,500
NOBLESVILLE, IN
pop. ~70,000
← about 1,200 miles / a two-day drive →
"Small town helping small town" isn't what this is. New Focus HR is based in Noblesville — a 70,000-person suburb of Indianapolis, not a rural peer. Meanwhile Albion, Indiana — the town Stefen Wynn ran before Colorado — has about 2,200 people, genuinely comparable to Paonia. The contract is even written to be governed by Indiana law, in Indiana courts, and says "all work will be conducted in the New Focus HR offices" — in Indiana. Documented

Could Paonia have found this in Colorado? Easily. A compliant municipal employee handbook is a routine service offered across the state — including by organizations the Town already pays into. Paonia is a member of CIRSA (the Colorado intergovernmental insurance pool — it's in the record; CIRSA audited the Town's park electrical in 2024 and ran a Board training), and CIRSA provides member towns HR resources and model personnel policies. The Colorado Municipal League does too. Documented So the question isn't whether help existed closer — it's why Indiana.

4The money — what it sounded like vs. what was signed

The Board's stated reason this didn't need a bid: it was "under $10,000." That's only true if you look at one piece and ignore the rest.

What made it sound routine

$7,500
  • One-time, fixed fee for the employee handbook
  • "Under $10,000" — so, the Board was told, no formal bid required

What the contract actually obligates

$7,500  +  $150/yr  +  $175/hr
  • $7,500 handbook — plus
  • $150 every year, ongoing, for "updates"
  • $175/hour of HR consulting — no cap, no ceiling, no not-to-exceed — billed every two weeks "as utilized." The same $175/hr even applies to "quality review meetings."
  • 18%-a-year late fees; the Town indemnifies the firm

An open-ended hourly rate with no cap is not a $7,500 purchase. Nobody knows what this contract's ceiling is — because it doesn't have one. One of the questions worth asking is simply: what has it cost so far? Inference terms Documented

5The "justification" that justifies nothing

Here's the actual sole-source questionnaire — the Town's eight questions, and the vendor's own answers. Read down the right column. A sole-source justification is supposed to prove there was no other choice. Look at how little of it does that.

2. What highly specialized prior experience is vital here?"...16 years...60 years of HR expertise on our team."Generic experience, not uniqueness

Three of the eight answers are literally "Not applicable." Nothing in this questionnaire establishes that New Focus HR was the only firm that could do this work — because it wasn't. There are hundreds of firms that write municipal employee handbooks. This isn't a justification for a sole source. It's a sole source wearing the costume of one — and the vendor sewed the costume. 6The relationship — what the emails actually show

This is the part you can't un-see. The Town's official file labels this email chain the "Sole Source Justification email." Here is what's in it.

"Stefen, I hope that you are enjoying your time in Indy? We look forward to attending the race on Sunday, although the temperatures may be a bit cool compared to past races."

What it tells us: this is not a cold sales contact. "Past races" — plural — describes a history. "Your time in Indy" — she knew where he was. A vendor and a buyer who go to the races together, more than once, are not strangers. Inference — but a strong one

"Thanks so much for reaching out and I will look forward to hearing back from you."

What it tells us: Paonia contacted New Focus HR first — not the other way around. Someone at the Town reached 1,200 miles to Indiana to start this. The obvious question: who, and why this firm? Documented

To: Stefen Wynn <StefenW@townofpaonia.com>; Samira V <SamiraV@townofpaonia.com>

What it tells us: every email is addressed to Wynn first, Vetter second — even though Vetter is the one who "submitted" the staff report and signed the questionnaire email. The social line ("enjoying your time in Indy") is aimed squarely at Wynn. The relationship in this file runs to him. Documented

7Everything points the same direction

Take any single thread of this and you could shrug it off. The problem is that every thread runs to the same place.

The firm
New Focus HR, LLC — Noblesville, Indiana
Its references
The 6 references it gave Paonia: Greencastle, Sheridan, Winchester, Cicero, Ligonier, Bluffton — all in Indiana. Zero in Colorado.
Its client list
Every named client on its own website — ~13 governments — is in Indiana. It brags it serves "over 15% of Indiana's municipalities."
Its CO presence
It markets itself as having clients in 6 states incl. Colorado — but names none. The only documented Colorado client anywhere is Paonia itself.
The contract's law
Governed by the laws of Indiana, Hamilton County. Work performed in Indiana.
The signer's career
Stefen Wynn: Town Manager of Albion, Indiana (2016–2019); MPA from Indiana University.

Every row above is Documented. Wynn arrived in Paonia in July 2023; the contract is June 2025 — so it wasn't "the day he showed up." But the pattern is unmistakable: a Colorado town's HR work flowed to Indiana, signed by the one official whose career is Indiana, justified by an email about an Indiana race.

8The Albion thread — what's real, what isn't

This is the lead you're circling, and it's worth circling — but you have to hold it exactly right, especially before anything goes public.

What we can actually prove
  • New Focus HR's own website lists "Noble County Government (Albion)" as a client.
  • Albion is the county seat of Noble County — the two share the same town.
  • Wynn was Town Manager of Albion (~2016–2019).
  • So: the no-bid vendor's client roster includes the county government seated in the very town Paonia's Administrator used to run.
What we CANNOT yet say
  • It's Noble County government — not the Town of Albion. Wynn ran the town, a different government. No evidence the firm worked for his government.
  • The timing is unknown. We don't know if the firm's Noble County work overlapped Wynn's years there, or came later.
  • Neptune Beach, Florida — Wynn's job between Albion and Paonia — turned up nothing. No connection found.
  • That the firm "followed Wynn," "worked for Wynn," or that any money reached him. None of that is established.

The honest status: a real, specific lead worth running down — not a proven connection. The thing that would turn the left column into a finding is confirming when New Focus HR worked for Noble County, and whether Wynn had any hand in it.

9So what's actually going on here?

Here is the most honest read of this one contract.

What's Documented is already a serious problem on its own, regardless of anyone's motive: the Town skipped competitive bidding on an open-ended contract, wrote the justification after signing, let the vendor write that justification, and reached 1,200 miles for a routine service it could get in Colorado. That's a real failure of the process that's supposed to protect the public's money — and it's true no matter why it happened.

What's Inference — strong, but still inference — is that this happened because of a pre-existing personal relationship between the Town Administrator and the vendor. The "past races" email, the "thanks for reaching out," the all-Indiana everything, the fact that the firm's only Colorado footprint is the town Wynn runs — that all points one direction. It would be a remarkable set of coincidences if it pointed nowhere.

What is Not established — and you should hold this line hard — is that Wynn personally profited, that the firm "followed him," or that a law was broken. There is no evidence of money flowing back to anyone. This looks like relationship-driven favoritism in how a contract was steered — which is a governance problem worth exposing — but "favoritism shaped this" and "corruption was proven" are two different sentences, and only the first one is currently backed by the paper.

10The questions that would actually settle it

You don't need a theory. You need answers to these — and every one of them is answerable.

  1. Who at the Town first reached out to New Focus HR, and how did anyone in Paonia come to know an HR firm in Noblesville, Indiana?
  2. What is the relationship between Stefen Wynn and Kristen Deutsch? What does "past races" refer to — and how far back does it go?
  3. Why was the sole-source questionnaire completed five days after the contract was already signed? And why did the vendor write the answers?
  4. Did New Focus HR ever do work connected to Wynn's previous jobs — the Town of Albion or Noble County, Indiana, or the City of Neptune Beach, Florida — and when?
  5. What has the uncapped $175/hour consulting cost the Town since June 2025 — and what is the total spent on New Focus HR to date?
  6. Why is a Colorado town's contract governed by Indiana law?
  7. Was a single competing quote ever requested? If not — why not, for a service available throughout Colorado?

If the answers are clean, this ends quietly and the Town is fine. If they're not, the questions were the right ones to ask. Either way — asking is the entire job.
