A neighbor's notes · Town of Paonia

The Handbook Question

The Town paid an Indiana firm to write Paonia's employee handbook — sole-sourced, no competitive bid. The finished handbook has never been made public. This is two things: the fair questions about how that happened, and a working blueprint for the handbook Paonia could build itself — with resources it already pays for — that would actually fix the personnel failures of the last two years.

How to read this — two parts, one point.

Part 1 is questions, not accusations. Nothing here says anyone broke a law or pocketed a dollar. It asks the Town to explain its own paperwork. Every question has a clean answer available — that's the point of asking.

Part 2 is the constructive half — proof this didn't have to happen, and a real framework for doing it right. The blueprint is a starting framework to build from with the Town Attorney's review and the Town's existing resources — not a finished legal document. It is offered in good faith, by a resident who would rather help fix this than just point at it.

Part 1

The Questions

What the public record shows about the New Focus HR contract — and what it doesn't.

1.1What happened

In June 2025 the Town of Paonia hired New Focus HR, LLC — an HR consulting firm in Noblesville, Indiana — to rewrite the Town's employee handbook and serve as its outsourced HR department. The work was sole-sourced. No competitive bid was sought. The terms: $7,500 for the handbook, $150/year for updates, and $175/hour for open-ended HR consulting with no cap. The contract was signed for the Town by the Town Administrator & Treasurer, and is written to be governed by the laws of Indiana. From the Town's June 10, 2025 packet

1.2The money — what's actually been paid

Every documented payment to New Focus HR in the Town's own disbursement registers, June 2025 through May 2026:

DateWhatAmount
Aug 2025Handbook (first half) + annual subscription$3,900.00
Aug–Nov 2025Hourly HR consulting — 4 invoices (~10.5 hrs)$1,837.50

Total paid to date$9,575.00

An honest note about that number: $9,575 over a year is not, by itself, a shocking sum — and the uncapped $175/hour meter has run modestly so far (about 11 hours). The amount is not the issue. The issue is that the rate, the firm, and the whole arrangement were never tested against a single competing offer. A competitive bid is how a town finds out whether $175/hour is high, low, or fair — and whether a Colorado firm would have done it better or cheaper. Nobody checked.

1.3Four fair questions

Question 1

Why was this never put out for a competitive bid?

No competing quote — from a Colorado firm or from anyone — was sought, on an open-ended contract. The Town's own purchasing policy contemplates competition. What was the basis for skipping it?

Question 2

Why an Indiana firm for Colorado work?

New Focus HR's entire documented track record is Indiana — Indiana references, Indiana clients, a contract governed by Indiana law. A Colorado employee handbook has to be built around Colorado-specific statutes (see §2.3). Why pay an out-of-state firm to come up to speed on Colorado law that Colorado resources already know?

Question 3

Why isn't the HR function independent of the Town Manager?

Given the personnel disputes and litigation the Town has been working through, the HR function may at some point need to operate at arm's length from the Town Administrator. A firm that holds an uncapped, no-bid contract — with its ongoing hourly work flowing entirely at the Administrator's discretion — cannot easily be both his vendor and a check on him. Was that independence ever considered?

Question 4

How did Paonia come to choose this specific firm?

The vendor's own email thanks the Town for "reaching out" — so someone at the Town contacted a firm 1,200 miles away first. The Town has never explained how it came to select this particular out-of-state firm. (For the record: a search for any prior relationship between the Town Administrator and New Focus HR — through his earlier posts in Indiana and Florida — found none in the public record. This is an open question, not an implied answer.)

1.4The question under all the others: where is the handbook?

This is the one that matters most, and it's the simplest.

The Town has now paid New Focus HR the full $7,500 for the employee handbook — both halves. The Town's own staff status reports show the draft was "complete" by October 2025 and then "under review by the Town Administrator & Treasurer" — and it was still listed that way in packets through February 2026, four-plus months later.

As far as the public record shows: the handbook has never been brought to the Board for adoption, and it has never been made public. It is not in any meeting packet. We searched. If it exists in finished form, the Town hasn't shown it to anyone.

The plain question

The Town paid in full, in January 2026, for a deliverable its own reports said was "complete" in October 2025 — and as of the most recent record, no one outside the Town Administrator's office has seen it, and the Board has never adopted it. Where is the handbook Paonia paid $7,500 for, and when will the public and the Board see it?

Part 2

The Better Way

Proof this didn't have to happen — and a working blueprint for the handbook Paonia could build itself.

2.1This isn't exotic — Colorado towns build their own

An employee handbook is not specialized, mysterious work that requires an out-of-state consultant. It is a standard municipal document, and Colorado towns build, adapt, and publish theirs as a matter of routine. A few you can read online right now:

Manitou Springs
A Colorado town's Employee Handbook, effective May 2025 — current, Colorado-law-compliant, and posted publicly.
Town of Parker
Public Employee Handbook that consolidated the Town's older personnel and operations manuals.
City of Durango
2026 Employee Handbook, published as a public PDF on the city website.

The contrast writes itself: peer Colorado communities post their handbooks for any resident to read. Paonia paid $9,575 to Indiana and can't show you the result.

2.2What Paonia already has — and already pays for

The Town did not need to reach to Indiana. It is already paying for Colorado-specific HR help:

CIRSA
Paonia is already a dues-paying CIRSA member (it's in the record — CIRSA audited Town facilities and ran a Board training). CIRSA — the Colorado Intergovernmental Risk Sharing Agency — provides member towns with employment-policy review, an employment-law liability hotline, HR / supervisory / harassment / respectful-workplace training, and assistance with sensitive personnel investigations. This is HR support, built for Colorado municipal law, that the Town already pays for and apparently did not use.
The Town Attorney
Already retained. Legal review of a handbook draft is exactly the kind of work the Town Attorney is for — and is the step that should localize any template to Colorado law.
Peer towns' handbooks
Other Colorado towns' public handbooks (above) are usable as starting templates — already built around Colorado statutes, free to adapt.
The Town's own staff
The Clerk's office already holds the "Human Resources" function. The work of adapting a template and running a review group is in-house work.

  • Indiana-law template, adapted for Colorado on the Town's dime

2.3Why "Colorado" matters here

A Colorado municipal handbook isn't a generic document. It has to be built around Colorado-specific employment law — and these are statutes an Indiana firm's template is simply not built for:

  • HFWA — the Healthy Families and Workplaces Act: paid sick leave, 1 hour per 30 hours worked, up to 48 hours a year, every employer, no waiting period.
  • FAMLI — Colorado's Family and Medical Leave Insurance program: up to 12 weeks of state paid family/medical leave.
  • POWR Act — Protecting Opportunities and Workers' Rights: expanded anti-discrimination protections and limits on nondisclosure agreements.
  • COMPS Order — Colorado's wage-and-hour rules, including a required signed employee acknowledgment.
  • EPEWA — the Equal Pay for Equal Work Act: pay transparency and job-posting rules.

CIRSA's hotline and policy review exist precisely because this is Colorado-specific and changes often. Paying an Indiana firm to learn it is paying for the learning curve.

2.4The blueprint — what a Paonia handbook should contain

Here is the working framework. The white rows are the standard sections any municipal handbook needs. The red rows are the six sections that aren't boilerplate for Paonia — they are the sections that directly answer documented personnel failures of the last two years, and they're detailed in §2.5.

Model framework — Town of Paonia Employee Handbook
1. Introduction & purpose
What the handbook is, who it covers, how it's amended; statement that it is adopted by the Board and public.
2. Equal opportunity & non-discrimination
POWR-Act-compliant anti-discrimination and equal-opportunity policy.
3. Employment classifications
Full-time / part-time / seasonal / exempt / non-exempt; probationary periods; what each status means.
4. Compensation & timekeeping
Pay periods, overtime (COMPS Order), pay transparency (EPEWA), the salary ordinance.
5. Leave & benefits
HFWA paid sick leave, FAMLI, FMLA, bereavement, military, jury, holidays, PTO, benefits.
6. Standards of conduct & ethics
Conduct expectations, conflict-of-interest, outside employment, acceptable-use of technology.
7. Anti-harassment & anti-retaliation
Answers a documented failure
Bans harassment and retaliation against anyone who raises a concern or cooperates with an investigation. See §2.5.
8. Complaint & grievance procedure
Answers a documented failure
A written path for complaints — including complaints about the Town Administrator — that runs to the Board, not through the subject. See §2.5.
9. Duty to investigate
Answers a documented failure
Complaints must be investigated by a neutral party and the findings documented — not dismissed. See §2.5.
10. Progressive discipline & termination
Answers a documented failure
Documented, graduated discipline and clear termination standards and due process. See §2.5.
11. Personnel records & file integrity
Answers a documented failure
Employees may inspect their own file; entries are dated, accurate, attributable; no alteration or backdating; a way to contest entries. See §2.5.
12. Electronic monitoring & workplace surveillance
Answers a documented failure
Defines what monitoring is and is not permitted; bans covert recording of employees and using surveillance footage for discipline without notice. See §2.5.
13. Safety & loss control
Workplace safety, the CIRSA-required new-employee safety orientation, incident reporting.
14. Separation & the acknowledgment form
Resignation, layoff, return of property, final pay; the signed acknowledgment every employee returns.

2.5The six sections that would have changed what happened

This is the heart of it. Each of these six policies maps directly to a personnel failure that is already documented in the public record — much of it in the Town's own March 30, 2026 letter and its own CORA responses. A handbook with these sections wouldn't be paperwork. It would be the process the Town didn't have.

Complaint & grievance procedure — and the duty to investigate

What it must do: give every employee a written, accessible path to raise a complaint — explicitly including a complaint about the Town Administrator — that is received by the Board directly, not routed through the person being complained about. Set timelines. Guarantee a written response. Require that complaints be investigated by a neutral party and the findings documented.

The documented failure it answers

In September 2025 Deputy Treasurer Kaja Bowman asked the Mayor whether a complaint process even existed. There wasn't one. Instead of an answer, her name was disclosed to the Town Administrator, and she was terminated 23 days later. A separate formal complaint sat unanswered for 16+ months; a Public Works Director's resignation accusations were never investigated. Trustee Hunter herself wrote that the Town needs "a clear, documented, and accessible process that ensures complaints of this nature are received by the Board directly, not routed through the subject of the complaint." This section is that process.
Anti-retaliation & whistleblower protection

What it must do: protect any employee — or appointed official — who raises a concern, files a complaint, or cooperates with an investigation. Define retaliation broadly (termination, discipline, reassignment, hostile treatment). State plainly that retaliation is itself a violation with consequences.

The documented failure it answers

The public record documents a pattern across four people in different roles who raised concerns and faced consequences — an elected trustee, a planning commissioner, a public works director, and a deputy treasurer. Whatever one concludes about any single case, a town with a real anti-retaliation policy has a standard to measure conduct against and a process to enforce it. Paonia has neither in writing.
Progressive discipline & termination standards

What it must do: establish documented, graduated discipline; define what conduct leads to what consequence; set clear standards and a basic due-process path before termination, so discipline is consistent and reviewable rather than discretionary.

The documented failure it answers

One employee was terminated "without cause" 23 days after asking a process question. Another, per the Town's own surveillance logs, was terminated on the basis of footage recorded of her without her knowledge — and informed of the recording only at the moment she was fired. A written progressive-discipline policy is the difference between discipline that can be explained and discipline that can't.
Personnel records & file integrity

What it must do: give every employee the right to inspect their own personnel file; require that entries be dated, accurate, and attributable to who made them; prohibit alteration or backdating; and give employees a way to contest an entry.

The documented failure it answers

The Town's March 30, 2026 letter to citizens records that the Town Administrator made backdated entries in Kaja Bowman's personnel file. A file-integrity policy makes that a clear, named violation — and gives the employee a right to see and challenge what's in their own file.
Electronic monitoring & workplace surveillance

What it must do: define what workplace monitoring is and is not permitted; prohibit covert recording of employees in the course of their routine work; prohibit using surveillance footage as a basis for discipline without disclosure; require notice of any monitoring.

The documented failure it answers

The Town's own Verkada audit logs — produced under a records request — show permanent video archives created on interior Town Hall cameras and deliberately labeled with the names of specific employees, with audio enabled. One open question on the public record is simply: has the Town ever adopted any written policy prohibiting the surveillance of its own employees? The answer, as far as the record shows, is no. This section is that policy.

None of these six is unusual. Every one is standard in a properly built municipal handbook — including, almost certainly, in the public peer-town handbooks linked in §2.1. They are only remarkable in Paonia because Paonia's documented failures are exactly the failures these standard sections exist to prevent.

2.6What it would actually take

Concretely, and without a $9,575 mystery:

  1. A small working group — a Trustee, the Clerk's office, and the Town Attorney for legal review.
  2. Start from a peer Colorado town's public handbook as the template — it already has the Colorado statutes built in.
  3. Run the draft through CIRSA's policy review and employment-law hotline — a service the Town already pays for in its dues.
  4. Build in the six sections in §2.5 deliberately, because Paonia's own history shows exactly why they matter.
  5. Publish the draft. Take public comment. Adopt it by the Board, in the open.

That is a matter of weeks of in-house work and resources already paid for — and it ends with something the New Focus HR contract has not produced in a year: a finished handbook the public and the Board can actually see.

Why this is offered

The first half of this is questions, and they're fair ones — the Town should be able to answer every one. But the second half is the part that matters more. It would be easy to just say "you did the handbook wrong." This says something better: here is how to do it right, with what you already have, and here is exactly how a real handbook would have changed the hardest personnel episodes of the last two years.

A good handbook isn't bureaucracy. It's the set of promises a town makes to the people who work for it — that there's a way to raise a concern, that there's a fair process before you lose your job, that your own file tells the truth, that you won't be watched in secret. Paonia paid $9,575 and still doesn't have those promises in writing. It could — cheaply, locally, and in public. That's the point of all of this.
