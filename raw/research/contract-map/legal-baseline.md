# Legal Baseline — Colorado competitive-bidding law

## C.R.S. § 31-15-712 — "Public improvements by contract"

Confirmed consistent across the 2017, 2023, and 2024 Colorado Revised Statutes.

- **Threshold: $5,000.** "All work done by the city in the construction of works of public
  improvement of **five thousand dollars or more** shall be done by contract to the lowest
  responsible bidder on open bids after ample advertisement."
- **Anti-fragmentation (the statute's own word is "unlawful"):** "It is **unlawful** for any
  person to divide a works of public improvement construction into two or more separate
  projects for the sole purpose of evading or attempting to evade the requirement that works
  of public improvement construction costing five thousand dollars or more be submitted to
  open bidding, unless the total cost of any such project would be less if divided into two or
  more projects than if submitted to open bidding as one project."
- **Professional/technical exception:** the municipality "is not required to advertise for and
  receive bids for such technical, professional, or incidental assistance as it may deem wise to
  employ in guarding the interest of the [municipality] against the neglect of contractors in
  the performance of such work." NOTE: this exception is written narrowly — it covers
  assistance in *overseeing other contractors' work* (e.g., construction inspection), not a
  blanket exemption for every professional-services contract.
- **Negotiation:** if no bids are received, or the governing body deems all bids too high, it
  may negotiate — but "no negotiated price shall exceed the lowest responsible bid previously
  received."

## Open question — does § 31-15-712 bind statutory towns?

The section is **captioned "cities"** and its operative text uses "city" and "city council."
It sits in Title 31, Article 15 ("Exercise of Municipal Powers"), Part 7, which the title of the
article applies to "Cities and Towns" generally. Whether the section binds a statutory town
like Paonia directly — or whether towns are instead governed by their own adopted purchasing
policy plus other Title 31 provisions — is a genuine legal question for the Town Attorney, and
should NOT be overclaimed. (Per project guidance: cite only CO statutory-town authority.)

## What this means for the investigation

1. **Don't hang the case on one number.** Present BOTH yardsticks: the statutory framework
   ($5,000 floor, anti-fragmentation "unlawful") *and* the Town of Paonia's own adopted
   purchasing policy (threshold TBD from the packets — Pete's May 12 speech referenced
   $25,000, which is most likely the Town's internal policy figure, not the statute).
2. **The finding is the PATTERN, not a threshold violation.** No-bid awards, sole-source
   without written justification, change-order ballooning past the original not-to-exceed,
   vendor concentration, and scope-splitting are the evidence — and they hold regardless of
   which exact threshold governs.
3. The agents were briefed with a $25,000 figure; re-tag every extracted contract action
   against BOTH the $5,000 statutory floor and the Town's actual policy in synthesis.
