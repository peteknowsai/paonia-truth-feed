# Planning Commission Work Session - November 20, 2024

**Body:** [[Planning Commission]]
**Type:** Work Session
**Date:** November 20, 2024
**Chair:** [[Suzanne Watson]]
**Present:** Watson (Chair), Commissioner Smith, [[Pete McCarthy]]
**Staff:** [[Stefen Wynn]] (Town Administrator)

## Summary

This is the work session directly referenced in [[Pete McCarthy]]'s [[Resignation Letter]]. The session was scheduled to review the [[Housing Element]] of the [[Comprehensive Plan]], which had been deferred from the November 4 meeting specifically so Pete could participate. The session devolved into hostile exchanges, with the Town Administrator threatening to walk out when Pete raised process questions, and the Mayor dismissing documentation requests.

## Key Moments

### ~20:00 - Pete Joins, Announces Himself

Pete arrives and is introduced for the work session:

> "If you'd like to announce your name, Pete, for this work session it's a little less formal than... Peter McCarthy."

Pete had requested the Colorado statute (31-23-206 G and J) be pulled up, the sections specific to the [[Housing Element]] requirements. This signals his intent to evaluate whether the housing element complies with state law.

### ~22:42 - Pete's Email Comments Referenced

Discussion of the comment table that [[Stefen Wynn]] put together:

> "I was just looking at some of his emails and that he said the housing element didn't... from the housing plan didn't address those parts of the statute and that's why we had to rewrite things."

This confirms Pete had been raising statutory compliance issues in writing for some time.

### ~33:40 - Email Exchanges Surface

> "I was just looking at these emails, this email back and forth, and I saw an explanation that we knew this housing..."

### ~42:37 - Pete Asks About Zoning and Property Rights

Pete raises questions about whether current zoning allows lot consolidation and demolition:

> "Yes Peter, are you able to buy two lots and demolish the houses and build a mansion on... based on our current zoning? Probably."

This line of questioning probes whether the housing policies are substantive or purely aspirational.

### ~57:00 - THE CONFRONTATION (Key Resignation Evidence)

Pete describes feeling attacked when raising concerns. This is the core exchange cited in the resignation letter:

> "I feel very attacked just when I'm bringing up my concerns. So I don't need to bring up any concerns. We can... if bringing up concerns is going to elicit a walk out and a threat or a characterization of me looking back, then I can stop. Would you like me to stop?"

> "Well I'm trying to answer your question."

> "No you're actually trying to stop me from bringing up my concerns."

Pete directly names what is happening: that the administrator is using hostility to suppress legitimate questions from a commissioner.

### ~57:49 - RFP Was Incomplete, Town Acknowledges

> "Your concerns are, you know, what if pieces were missing, which they were. Yes, because the RFP was not complete. Okay, the town takes responsibility for that."

Pete presses on the implications:

> "So we turned in an incomplete master plan, published it to the town saying that this was a final draft?"

> "Absolutely not. I was in that meeting. No. Okay, no Pete, absolutely not."

### ~1:13:10 - Two Months of Stonewalled Requests

Pete documents his repeated attempts to get written explanations:

> "I've asked for that in writing many times going back two months. Even though I asked for a vote to then direct, because I was told I wouldn't be getting those, I asked the rest of the Planning Commission to vote to direct Stephan to give an explanation. Not to incorporate those things into the housing element but to just give an explanation. I've been given no explanation."

This is the "stonewalling" referenced in the resignation letter. Pete asked for written policy explanations, was denied, escalated to a commission vote to direct staff, and was still denied.

### ~1:13:58 - Agenda Clogging Accusation

> "You said that you didn't want to get into it because Pete was... exactly, but it was also too late. You can't clog our agenda that way."

> "I didn't ask..."

The administrator characterizes Pete's agenda items as "clogging" the agenda, dismissing the legitimacy of his requests.

### ~1:17:28 - Procedural Silencing

Discussion reveals how Pete's questions were handled:

> "What I heard though was you asked for that right off the bat and there was silence. But then at the point when Stephan said 'can I go through this' and he started going through it, then Commissioner McCarthy had a question and we ended up talking about this."

> "Stephan asked if I had a comment, but I believe that was because you were raising your hand."

### ~1:39:19 - Walk Out Threat (Key Evidence)

The administrator's hostility escalates:

> "This is going on way too much... apologize... continuing characterizing what I'm saying."

> "Let me ask you, if you could say it differently, you have an attitude in your voice that's very... it's quite... the administrator, you're just threatening to walk out. It feels very critical as opposed to being more constructive."

This is the direct evidence of the administrator threatening to abandon duties, cited in the resignation letter.

### ~1:51:35 - STR Discussion Dismissed

Pete raises [[Short-Term Rentals]] as relevant to neighborhood character policy:

> "Would something about short-term rentals be appropriate here?"

> "I don't think so. That's a very specific use."

Pete pushes back:

> "Is it not within the character of a residential neighborhood to have half the homes be hotels? Seems like that is very relevant."

The administrator insists STRs are legislative, not policy, and cannot be in the comprehensive plan. Pete argues the plan should at least "encourage owner occupation of houses."

### ~2:21:02 - Legal Risk and STR History

Pete raises the precedent of STR regulation and legal threats:

> "In fact it was threatened right during those meetings, right? And they thought it important enough to do even though there would be a lawsuit."

> "So why is protecting and bringing back 40 households back to this community that would have existed 10 years ago... have we done something? Why is that not worth a legal risk?"

Pete references San Francisco's successful defense of STR regulations as precedent.

## Relevance to Wiki Issues

- **[[Resignation Letter]]:** This meeting is the primary evidence cited. Pete's letter specifically references "the November 20th work session transcript" and lists: administrator threatening to abandon duties, mayor and administrator dismissing documentation requests, two months of stonewalled written requests, and administrative retaliation.
- **[[Email Transparency]]:** Pete's written requests over two months went unanswered. Staff refused to provide written policy explanations even after a commission vote directing them to do so.
- **[[Housing Element]]:** The substantive dispute is whether the housing element complies with Colorado statute 31-23-206 G and J. Pete identified missing statutory elements; the town acknowledged the RFP was incomplete but denied the published plan was presented as final.
- **[[Short-Term Rentals]]:** Pete tried to include STR policy language in the comprehensive plan. The administrator blocked it as "legislative" rather than policy, a distinction Pete disputed.
- **[[Planning Commission Authority]]:** The pattern is clear: commissioner raises questions, administrator responds with hostility, mayor sides with administrator, commissioner is characterized as disruptive. The commission's advisory role is rendered meaningless when staff refuses to answer questions.

## Procedural Notes

- This was a work session, not a formal meeting. Less formal structure, but still a public proceeding.
- Pete had been asking for written policy explanations for two months prior.
- The commission had previously voted to direct staff to provide explanations; staff did not comply.
- The administrator threatened to walk out rather than answer process questions.
- The mayor told Pete to "say it differently" and characterized his tone as the problem, rather than addressing the substance.
