# The CCMA Pipeline: How the Same Managers Circulate Through Colorado Towns

When Paonia needed an interim administrator, it did not run a search. It made a phone call. At the May 29, 2026 special meeting, staff explained — on the record — exactly how [[greg-sund|Greg Sund]]'s name reached the Board:

> "We met with the CCMA senior advisor, like the board directed us to… and he gave us Greg Sund's number." [[2026-05-29-meeting-transcript|source]]

This page documents the mechanism behind that call, and what it means for a town deciding how to hire its most powerful unelected official. It is a companion to the citizen proposal [[admin-search|How Paonia Should Hire Its Next Administrator]].

*This is an analysis published by paoniatruth.site. It is not a Town document.*

## The mechanism, in the Town's own words

Asked by Trustee Foster why only one candidate was before the Board, staff described a closed referral loop, not an open market: [[2026-05-29-meeting-transcript|source]]

- The name came from a single person — **the senior advisor to the Colorado City/County Management Association (CCMA / CCCMA)**, the managers' own professional association. *(The advisor's name is unclear in the audio and should be confirmed against the video.)*
- "I checked with [the senior advisor]. **There are no other interim candidates.**"
- "There are **six people on this advisory board**, and they are all constantly talking about who's coming and going."
- "**It's not unusual to only have a single candidate** for this kind of a job."
- The Town found its **previous** interim the same way — "this is a lot like what our last one looked like," again through CCMA. (Paonia's 2023 interim was [[2023-03-20-special-town-board-meeting--|Leslie Clement]], per Town records.)

So the supply of interim managers Paonia can draw on is, by staff's own account, a handful of people known to a half-dozen senior advisors who "talk about who's coming and going." That is not a labor market. It is a roster.

## The roster is real, and it is advertised

The interim-manager roster is not a theory. The Colorado search firm **KRW Associates** markets exactly this: its published recruitment materials advertise an "Interim Resources" program — "a network of potential interim candidates capable of bringing stability." And the CCMA Senior Advisor program (the Colorado affiliate of ICMA's former "Range Rider" program) exists precisely to connect towns in transition with known managers. The same names recur: in 2022, the executive director of the Colorado Municipal League reportedly recommended Sund into Mt. Crested Butte (*Crested Butte News*, March 2022). In 2026, a CCMA senior advisor handed his number to Paonia. See [[greg-sund]].

## What the roster optimizes for — filling seats, not lasting

The business model rewards turnover, not tenure. KRW Associates' published search proposals promise a town a *second* search for little more than expenses if the first hire is dismissed for performance within a year — i.e., **a failed placement is repeat business, not a loss.** Nothing in the pipeline is built to make a manager *stay*.

And the managers know it. Sund described the dynamic himself at the meeting: [[2026-05-29-meeting-transcript|source]]

> "There's times when it's easiest to let the administrator — the manager — go… that happens probably more often than not when you get newly elected people who want to make some changes, and the administrator is seen… as being tied to the old board."

He has lived it: by his own account he "worked for 14 different people on a five-member board" in Dickinson, ND, and chose Trinidad believing it had "gotten over" its history of "fairly constant replacement of managers" — only to find "those issues still had remained."

## The numbers behind the anecdote

A [[the-revolving-door-canvass|paoniatruth.site canvass of 20 small Colorado towns]] found the churn is the norm, not the exception:

- ~**2.3** managers per town, per decade.
- ~**3-year** median tenure.
- **1 in 4** managers arrives already titled "interim."
- A manager was **fired or forced out in 4 of every 10** towns.

Two towns named their own revolving door on the record: Ouray's council hoped to end "a merry-go-round of chief executive officers" (*Ouray County Plaindealer*, 2020), and the *Delta County Independent* (2017) called the pre-Wynn era in Paonia "the fifth administrator in six years."

## The cost — and what it crowds out

The pitch to residents is stark: that no one in the entire state — and certainly no one in Paonia — can do this job, so the Town must import a stranger. By staff's own account there were **six town-manager openings across Colorado** and a senior advisor "constantly" tracking who is available — a seller's market in which towns compete for a thin pool of itinerant managers. That scarcity is treated as a reason to accept the one name offered, rather than as a warning about how the model performs.

And it is not cheap. The proposed contract pays about **$72.50 an hour** — on the order of **$140,000–$150,000 a year at full time** — and the Town agreed to **cover part of the manager's housing**. In a community with a critical housing shortage, that means relocating an out-of-town official and spending scarce local housing to do it. [[2026-05-29-meeting-transcript|source]]

What the Town did not do is look here. No local candidate was sought or trained; the option of growing Paonia's own administrator — laid out in [[admin-search]] — was never put on the table. The pipeline's deepest cost is not the hourly rate or the housing. It is the assumption, never tested, that self-government is something this town must buy from outside.

## The alibi — and why it matters here

The pipeline comes with a built-in explanation for its own failures: when a placement clashes with a community and leaves, the story is that the *town* was impossible — too divided, too political, its citizens too difficult. That narrative does two things. It absolves the model. And it reframes the residents who push back as the problem to be managed around. Sund's confirmed "this is not a democracy" remark was, by his own account, directed at department-head accountability — not at residents — but the underlying posture, that the governed are something to be managed around rather than answered to, is the same one this alibi runs on. See [[interim-tm-candidate-greg-sund]].

That move played out from the floor at the May 29 meeting itself. Former mayor Mary Bachran reminded the Board, "this is not a democracy… we are a republic," and characterized the Town's critics as "a small cadre of people who believe that they are the only ones who can correctly run the town." [[2026-05-29-meeting-transcript|source]]

## In fairness

None of this is a verdict on Sund personally — by multiple accounts he is a competent, experienced manager, and his work on Fisher's Peak State Park is real. The point is structural: a town that sources its leadership from a closed professional roster is buying a short-term placement from a system optimized for turnover, and calling it stability. The alternative — growing and training a local — is laid out in [[admin-search]].

## What happened next

At the May 29 meeting, the motion to hire Sund **died for lack of a second**; Trustee Foster's insistence on references and "documented process" carried the room, and the decision was pushed to a June 3 follow-up meeting. The pipeline produced the candidate. It was the *process* objection — the same one this project raised — that slowed it down. [[2026-05-29-meeting-transcript|source]]
