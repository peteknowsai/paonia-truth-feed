# Three Years of Scorecards: What the Town Administrator Finished, and What He Didn't

*The Town Administrator publishes his own monthly report card. Merged across three years, it tells a story he didn't intend: a handful of projects that have sat unfinished his entire tenure, and most of a year missing from the record entirely.*

AI Analysis

## The instrument is his own

Since July 2023, Town Administrator Stefen Wynn has posted a **Departmental Scorecard** to the Town's website — a month-by-month table of every initiative across every department, with columns for status, estimated cost, start date, and date completed. It is a self-graded report card, published voluntarily. That makes it the fairest possible measure of the office: not an outsider's critique, but the administration's own account of what it set out to do.

We pulled **every scorecard the Town has posted** — 21 of them, July 2023 through February 2026 — and parsed all **1,601 line-items** into **260 distinct initiatives**, then tracked each one from the month it first appears to the month it's marked done or quietly drops off. What follows is the short version.

## Finding 1: Most of 2025 is simply missing

The scorecard page lists a heading for every month of 2025 — April, May, June, all the way to December. But only **January, February, and March 2025** have an actual document behind the link. Every other 2025 month returns "Not Found." We checked the Town's file server directly, at every naming pattern the Town has ever used: **April through December 2025 — nine consecutive months — are not there.**

A "monthly" transparency report went dark for three-quarters of a year and resumed, without explanation, in January 2026. June 2024 is missing too. And there's a tell: the **December 2024** scorecard has no working link on the page, yet the file *does* sit on the Town's server, simply unposted. So the records can exist without being published — which is exactly why the 2025 gap is worth a formal records request. Either nine months of scorecards were never produced, or they were produced and never shown to the public.

## Finding 2: The big projects never crossed the line

Three years in, the marquee structural reforms are still listed as open — not paused, not cancelled, *open* — on the most recent scorecard:

- **Code Re-Write** — on the scorecard every single month since July 2023. **21 months. Still open.**
- **ADA Transition Plan** — since August 2023. **20 months. Still open.**
- **Municipal code additions/updates (Police)** — since the start. **20 months. Still open.**
- **Clerk duty checklists** — since August 2023. **20 months. Still open.**
- **Utility Fee Study, Phase I water improvements, the hydrogeological study** — all running 17–21 months without a completion.

These aren't small punch-list items. They're the governance backbone — the code, accessibility compliance, financial controls — and by the administration's own ledger they have never been finished.

## Finding 3: The format hides as much as it shows

Of the 260 initiatives, **67 reached a real completion date** and **52 are still open**. The other **141 simply vanished** — they appeared for a while, then dropped off the scorecard with no "date completed" ever recorded.

Here's the problem: the scorecard never tells you *why* something disappeared. A vanished item might have been quietly finished, or quietly abandoned, and the document treats those two outcomes identically. (For that reason we don't claim all 141 were failures — some were surely completed without being marked.) But a report card whose own design can't distinguish "done" from "given up on" isn't really holding anyone accountable. It's a list that gets shorter.

The clearest example is in the tracker: **"Finish the northwest corner of the skatepark"** carries the identical status line — *"looking at options to finish the unfinished portion"* — for **ten straight months**, then flips to *"ON HOLD UNTIL FURTHER NOTICE."* Ten months of motion words, then a quiet stop. Multiply that by a hundred line-items and you have a document that radiates activity while a surprising amount of it goes nowhere.

## Why it matters

The same Board that let [[eighteen-months-of-silence|a formal complaint sit unanswered for eighteen months]], and that [[award-claim-vs-record|reached for an award to answer questions about its books]], is the Board responsible for reviewing these scorecards. The scorecard is the one tool that could make the Town Administrator's performance legible to residents — and it works only if it's complete, honest about completion, and actually published. On the current record, it is none of the three.

*Methodology: every figure here comes from the Town's own posted scorecard PDFs, parsed line-by-line and merged across time. "Completed" means a genuine date in the Date Completed column; status words like "in progress" don't count.
