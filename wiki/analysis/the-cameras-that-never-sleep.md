*The Town told residents its cameras were "motion activated." The Town's own contract and audit logs show they record around the clock. The Mayor was asked to correct the record. She refused, and two of those cameras are still filming the public today.*

AI Analysis

Surveillance cameras went up in Paonia. When residents raised concerns about being recorded, the Town offered reassurance. In an official March 2026 statement, it told the public that the cameras were "motion activated" and not a "private property surveillance apparatus." The message was clear: do not worry, these only switch on when something moves, and this is not blanket surveillance of you.

Both claims are false.

The cameras record around the clock. This is not a matter of opinion or interpretation, and it does not depend on anyone's word against the Mayor's. It is proven by the Town's own records: Verkada's own product documentation for the equipment, the Town's signed contract for it, and the Town's own audit logs, which show continuous recording. Every one of those documents sits inside the Town's own files. The "motion activated" reassurance was contradicted by the paperwork the Town itself holds.

When this was shown on the record, the response was not to fix it. A resident demonstrated the contradiction, in public, and asked the Town to take the false claim down. The Town refused. The statement telling residents the cameras were "motion activated" was left up on the town website. And as of today, two of those cameras keep filming the public.

The detail to sit with is not the cameras themselves. Towns adopt cameras. The detail is that residents were told something untrue about them, in an official statement, and that when the Town was handed proof from its own files, it chose to leave the false statement standing rather than correct it.

That is a choice about honesty, and it traces to the same office. A mayor's plainest duty is to give residents accurate information about what their government is doing to them, and to fix it fast when the information turns out to be wrong. Telling the public a surveillance system only watches "on motion" when the contract says otherwise, and then refusing to correct it, is the opposite of that duty.

A recall does not turn off the cameras. It puts a question to the voters that the cameras only made plain. When a mayor's government tells residents something false about being surveilled, is shown the proof from its own records, and refuses to take the false claim down, has it earned the trust to keep watching?

The contract is public. The audit logs are public. The statement is still online. The record is here so you can check it yourself.
