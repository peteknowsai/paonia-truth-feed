# "Controls all the money, alone": what the evidence actually shows

Item #3 on Pete McCarthy's [[ten-things-flyer|10 Things flyer]] read: "Controls all the money, alone. Consolidated Administrator and Treasurer into one role. Prepares the budget that determines his own salary."

The Town's response called this "blatantly false." [[hunter-rebuttal-flyer|Hunter's rebuttal flyer]] and the [[original-draft-with-libelous|Board's official letter]] then named four internal controls meant to refute the claim. This article works through what each side said, what the record actually shows, and what is still unknown.

## What each side said

**The flyer:** Wynn consolidated Administrator and Treasurer. He prepares the budget. $106,000 in admin costs are spread across four enterprise fund pages. The last time Paonia concentrated financial authority in one person, $480,000 was embezzled over five years (2007-2012).

**The Town's response:** "This is blatantly false." Four controls were cited:
1. A Finance Committee (Mayor + one Trustee) that "reviews every invoice"
2. Caselle government accounting software
3. Professional Management Solutions (PMS), a third-party firm
4. The Board, not the Administrator, sets the Administrator's salary

## What the Town does not dispute

Three of the four underlying facts in the flyer are not contested anywhere in the Board's response:

- **Dual role.** Wynn holds both Town Administrator and Town Treasurer (Resolutions 2023-07 and 2023-08). The Board's letter describes the Administrator/Trustee form of government but does not dispute that the two roles are held by the same person.
- **Budget preparation.** The Town Administrator prepares the annual budget. Wynn's compensation is a line item inside that document.
- **Historical precedent.** Between 2007 and 2012, approximately $480,000 was embezzled from the Town by a single employee with concentrated financial authority. (9News, Denver Post, KVNF.) The embezzlement happened while the Town had internal controls in place at the time.

Hunter does correct one framing: the Board, not the Administrator, sets the Administrator's salary. The flyer's "prepares the budget that determines his own salary" is accurate on the preparation step; the approval step is the Board's.

## What "blatantly false" would require

"Blatantly false" is a high bar. To meet it, the Town's four cited controls have to actually function. That requires evidence, not assertion. Here is what each control would need, and what is currently on the public record.

### Finance Committee reviews every invoice

What would prove it: Minutes of Finance Committee meetings, or an invoice log showing specific invoices reviewed on specific dates with approvals or flags recorded.

What the record shows: No public Finance Committee minutes have been published. No invoice-level review log has been produced. When asked what the Finance Committee has ever flagged or rejected, the answer is not on the record. The open question [[finance-committee-effectiveness]] captures this gap.

### Caselle software as an internal control

Caselle is an accounting system. It is a control only to the extent that its audit logs are preserved, accessible to someone other than the subject of the audit, and actually reviewed. This is a question of user permissions and audit trails, both of which Caselle tracks but does not expose publicly.

Three Colorado Open Records Act requests now target exactly this:

- **April 1, 2026:** Caselle ERP User Access Report. Original deadline April 7; Vetter invoked a 7-day extension on the day of the deadline. Extended deadline April 16. As of April 19, past deadline, not produced. ([[cora-tracking-log|tracked here]])
- **April 12, 2026:** Caselle Security Audit Log, Audit Trail Report across all modules, and user account records for 2022-2024.
- **April 19, 2026:** Caselle Management Tracking Report filtered to changes in the User Rights, Users, and Group Rights tables, plus all communications (including with Professional Management Solutions) regarding user permission changes from October 2024 forward.

Until these are produced, "Caselle is a control" rests on the Town's assertion, not on evidence a citizen can check.

### Professional Management Solutions as third-party oversight

What PMS actually does for the Town is not documented on the public record. PMS is a consulting firm providing accounting and management services to Colorado municipalities. Whether it audits the Town's books, prepares them, or both — and who at PMS reviews what — is unknown from public documents. The April 19 CORA request seeks the communications that would answer this directly.

### The Board sets the salary

This is the one control that is documented. The Board approves the budget and conducts the Administrator's performance reviews. The correction to the flyer's phrasing is fair on this point.

## The embezzlement precedent

The 2007-2012 embezzlement did not happen because Paonia had no controls. It happened because the controls in place were not sufficient to catch a person in the middle of the financial plumbing. That is the structural point the flyer made: concentration of authority is itself a risk, regardless of the controls layered on top.

The Town's response does not address this point. It restates that controls exist.

## What remains unknown

After "blatantly false," these questions stand unanswered on the public record:

- What user permissions does Wynn hold in Caselle? Can he approve his own reimbursements, edit historical transactions, or modify audit-log retention settings?
- Are Caselle audit logs preserved for the full period? Who other than Wynn has reviewed them, and when?
- Has the Finance Committee ever flagged or rejected a disbursement?
- What does PMS actually review, and does anything Wynn prepares get independently checked by someone outside the Town?

These are the questions the pending CORA requests are designed to answer. They exist *because* the Town's answer — "we have controls" — is an assertion that has not been independently verified.

## Where this leaves the dispute

"Controls all the money, alone" is a characterization. The underlying facts behind it — dual role, budget preparation, historical precedent — are not disputed. The Town calls the characterization "blatantly false" while the records that would actually prove who has access to what, and who has reviewed what, have not been produced.

"Blatantly false" is a claim that requires evidence. So does "controls all the money, alone." The pending Caselle CORA requests are the mechanism that will show which characterization the record actually supports. A Town confident in its four controls should welcome their production.
