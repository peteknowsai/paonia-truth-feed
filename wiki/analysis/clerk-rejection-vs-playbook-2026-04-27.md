# Clerk Rejection vs. Drafting Playbook (April 27, 2026)

This page does one thing: walk through Town Clerk Vetter's April 27, 2026 rejection of the Surveillance Policy Ordinance citizen initiative, and check each of her stated grounds against the drafting playbook that was specifically built to survive her prior administrative-bar objections.

The conclusion: the petition that was rejected on April 27 followed every applicable rule the playbook prescribed. The Clerk's rejection grounds are not addressed to defects in the petition; they are addressed to the *subject matter* of surveillance regardless of how the petition is drafted. That makes the April 27 rejection materially identical in posture to the April 3 and April 13 rejections — and thus directly suitable for the Rule 106 petition already drafted.

## The four rejection grounds

Quoting the dispositive paragraph from the [[clerk-rejection-2026-04-27|April 27 rejection letter]]:

> *"The subject of Surveillance on Public Property is a managerial and operational function of Town property, which is beyond the scope of citizen-initiated measures. Actions taken by a municipality to manage public spaces and rights-of-way, including monitoring and surveillance, are administrative in nature. The proposed ballot measure seeks to control administrative actions by the Town, and the implementation of the proposal would require existing contractual obligations to be rescinded or modified. The formation, content, and amendment of contracts to which a Town is a party to further its policies are administrative matters, not suitable for an initiative."*

That is four assertions:

1. Surveillance on public property is "managerial and operational"
2. Monitoring and surveillance are "administrative in nature"
3. The proposal "seeks to control administrative actions by the Town"
4. The proposal "would require existing contractual obligations to be rescinded or modified" and contract formation/amendment is "administrative"

## Quote-check against the playbook

### Playbook Rule 1: Frame as policy declaration, not operational command

> Write "It is the policy of the Town that..." — NOT "The Town shall not install/operate X."

The April 20 petition was specifically reframed from a prohibition ("Town shall not...") to a policy declaration. That reframing was the *entire purpose* of resubmitting after the April 3 rejection.

**Vetter's response:** Reframed the objection from "administrative" to "managerial and operational" — a third doctrinal label for the same conclusion. She did not engage with the policy-declaration framing on its merits. The label moved; the result did not.

### Playbook Rule 3: Strip every provision Vetter has already killed

> - Injunctive relief language (she calls it "judicial, void")
> - Voter-approval-to-amend clauses (she says "void under Colorado Law")
> - Contract-voiding language (she calls it "administrative")
> - Unseverable exemptions (she attacked body-cam exemption as non-severable)

The April 20 surveillance petition removed all four categories of provision the playbook flagged. There is no injunctive relief, no voter-approval-to-amend clause, no contract-voiding language, and severability is preserved.

**Vetter's response:** Reached for the contract-rescission objection anyway. Her stated rationale is that *implementation* of the policy would *require* contractual obligations to be rescinded or modified. This is not an attack on language in the petition. It is an attack on the **logical implication** of declaring a public-property surveillance policy when an existing surveillance contract is in force. Under that theory, no citizen initiative on any subject can ever be filed if the Town happens to have signed a contract pointing the other direction. That is not a defect-of-petition holding; it is a categorical bar on initiating any policy that would constrain pre-existing executive contracts.

This is the closest thing in her letter to engaging with the petition's text — and even here, the operative move is to claim that the *consequence* of the policy makes the policy administrative, not that the policy declaration itself is administrative on its face.

### Playbook Rule 5: Single subject, tight scope

The April 20 petition was a single-subject policy declaration. No multi-subject objection appears anywhere in the April 27 rejection letter.

**Vetter's response:** The single-subject ground was dropped relative to the April 3 rejection. The petition successfully cleared that hurdle by being narrowed.

### Minimum Viable Ordinance Structure

> WHEREAS clauses establishing:
>   - Subject matter of permanent and general character
>   - Exercise of police power under C.R.S. §§ 31-15-101, 31-15-401
>
> Section 1 - POLICY DECLARATION (single sentence, pure policy)
> Section 2 - SEVERABILITY
> Section 3 - EFFECTIVE DATE

Per Pete McCarthy's account of the April 20 submission, this is the structure that was used. The only thing she has authority to evaluate under C.R.S. § 31-11-106(1) is whether the petition proposes legislation. The structure above is the canonical minimum-viable form for a legislative policy declaration in a Colorado statutory town.

**Vetter's response:** The rejection does not point to any specific defect in the WHEREAS clauses, the severability provision, or the policy declaration's wording. The rejection argues the *subject* cannot be initiated regardless of how the petition is structured.

## What the rejection does not do

The April 27 rejection letter does not:

- Cite a single Colorado case on the legislative-versus-administrative distinction
- Address *McKee v. City of Louisville* (1977), which holds that an ordinance prohibiting a city from building hospitals is legislative — directly on point for "Town shall not surveil"
- Address *Brooks v. Zabka* (1969), which holds that prohibiting specific city actions is legislative where it establishes permanent policy
- Address *Margolis v. District Court* (1981), which holds that prospective regulation is legislative
- Address *Vagneur v. City of Aspen* (2013), which holds that doubts about initiative scope must be resolved in favor of initiative power
- Apply the three-part legislative-versus-administrative test from *City of Montrose v. PUC*
- Distinguish or even acknowledge the Palmer Lake (Sept 2025) and Estes Park Question 300 (Nov 2025) statutory-town initiatives that successfully reached voters on subjects she would presumably also call "administrative"
- Engage with the structure of the petition or any specific clause within it

The rejection is one paragraph of conclusory assertions plus a contract-implication argument that proves too much.

## Operative consequence

Because the April 20 petition followed every drafting rule the playbook prescribed, and because the April 27 rejection nonetheless rests on subject-matter grounds rather than petition defects, this rejection is structurally the same as the prior two:

- **The Clerk's theory of why surveillance cannot be initiated has now changed three times in 24 days.** "Administrative function" → "purely executive acts" → "managerial and operational function." The doctrinal framing has rotated through three different labels while the result has not moved.
- **The Clerk has not engaged with controlling Colorado caselaw on legislative-versus-administrative.** No reference to *McKee*, *Brooks*, *Margolis*, *Vagneur*, or *Montrose* in any of the three rejection letters.
- **The contract-rescission ground sweeps too broadly.** Under Vetter's theory, no citizen initiative on any subject can constrain a Town contract that already exists. Hundreds of Colorado statutory-town ordinances would be void.

This is the predicate for the Rule 106 petition already drafted. The legal posture has not improved through compliance with the playbook; the Clerk has continued to reject regardless of how carefully the petition was tailored. What the playbook did accomplish is establish a clean record: the petition is structurally clean, the objections shifted, and the rejection cannot be attributed to draftsmanship.
