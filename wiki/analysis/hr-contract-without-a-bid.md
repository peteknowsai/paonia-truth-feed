# An HR Contract Without a Bid, and a Handbook No One Has Seen

*The Town of Paonia has a documented record of personnel problems. Its response was to hire an out-of-state firm to handle its Human Resources — in a way that raises its own set of questions. More than a year later, the public still cannot see what the Town paid for.*

By Pete McCarthy — May 14, 2026

---

This article is not an accusation. It does not claim anyone broke a law or profited improperly, and it draws no conclusion about motive. It is built entirely from the Town's own records — meeting packets, disbursement registers, and staff reports — and it ends, as it should, in questions the Town can simply answer.

But the questions are real, and they stack on top of one another. The Town has a personnel record that is already on the public record. The contract the Town entered into to address that record was handled in a way that departs from ordinary practice at several points. And the work that contract paid for has, so far, produced nothing the Board or the public can see.

## The personnel record

The starting point matters, because it is the context the Town itself was operating in.

In September 2025, the Town's Deputy Treasurer, [[kaja-bowman|Kaja Bowman]], approached Mayor Smith to ask whether a process existed for making a complaint involving the Town Administrator and HR. She was terminated on October 9, 2025 — 23 days later. She is one of [[retaliation-pattern|four individuals]], in different roles and time periods, whose departures or removals followed raising concerns about Town administration. And a formal written complaint to the Board — documenting specific concerns and asking, among other things, for confirmation that a functioning complaint process exists — went more than a year without a response from the Board as a body. Whether the Town has a working complaint process at all [[complaint-response-process|remains an open question]]; the one Trustee who responded individually agreed that the Town needs "a clear, documented, and accessible process that ensures complaints of this nature are received by the Board directly, not routed through the subject of the complaint."

That is the situation in which the Town went looking for outside HR help. New Focus HR's own proposal describes the engagement, in the firm's words, as addressing the Town's "various human resources challenges that they are trying to resolve within their organization."

## The response: an out-of-state firm, no bid

In June 2025, the Board approved a contract with **New Focus HR, LLC**, a firm in Noblesville, Indiana, to redesign the Town's employee handbook and serve as its "outsourced human resources consulting company." The terms: $7,500 for the handbook, $150 a year for updates, and **$175 an hour for open-ended consulting with no cap.** ([[newfocushr-hr-contract|full contract record]])

Four features of how that contract was put together are documented in the Town's own June 2025 meeting packet.

**It was sole-sourced.** No competitive bid was sought, and no competing quote from any other firm appears in the packet — for a service that is not specialized or scarce.

**The justification came after the signature.** New Focus HR signed and dated the agreement on May 23, 2025. The Town did not send the firm its "sole source justification" questions until May 28 — five days after the contract was already signed and dated. A sole-source justification is meant to be the reason competition is skipped; here it was assembled after the commitment was already made.

**The vendor wrote its own justification.** The Town Clerk emailed New Focus HR the Town's eight sole-source questions; New Focus HR — the firm that benefits from there being no competition — wrote the answers and returned them. Three of the eight answers are "Not applicable." Nothing in the exchange establishes that New Focus HR was the only firm capable of the work.

**The contract is governed by Indiana law.** The Terms & Conditions specify that the agreement is governed by "the laws of the State of Indiana," that all work is conducted in the firm's Indiana offices, and the firm's references are six Indiana municipalities. A Colorado town's employee handbook has to be built around Colorado-specific employment statutes — the Healthy Families and Workplaces Act, the state's paid family-leave program, the POWR Act, the COMPS wage order, and the Equal Pay for Equal Work Act. None of those is Indiana law.

## The money

From the Town's own bi-weekly disbursement registers, June 2025 through May 2026, the documented payments to New Focus HR total **$9,575.00** — the full $7,500 handbook fee (both halves), the $150 subscription, and roughly $1,925 of hourly consulting.

To be fair about that figure: $9,575 over a year is not, by itself, an extraordinary sum, and the open-ended hourly rate has been billed modestly so far — about eleven hours. The amount is not the point. The point is that the rate, the firm, and the arrangement were never measured against a single competing offer. A competitive process is how a town learns whether $175 an hour is high, low, or fair, and whether a Colorado firm would have done the work better, cheaper, or both. That test was never run.

## The deliverable that isn't there

This is the question underneath all the others, and it is the simplest.

The Town has now paid New Focus HR the **full $7,500** for the employee handbook — both halves, the second in January 2026. The Town's own staff status reports show the handbook draft was **"complete" by October 2025**, and then **"under review by the Town Administrator & Treasurer"** — still described that way in board packets through February 2026.

A review of every Town of Paonia board packet from January 2024 through May 2026 found that the finished handbook has not been brought to the Board for adoption, and has not been made public. The Town paid in full, in January, for a deliverable its own reports called "complete" the previous October — and as of the most recent record, the Board has never adopted it and no resident has seen it.

## The questions that remain

None of this is a charge. Each of these is a question the Town has the documents to answer:

1. Why was a contract of this kind never put out for competitive bid?
2. Why was the sole-source justification assembled, and answered by the vendor, only after the contract was already signed?
3. Why an out-of-state firm, working under another state's law, for Colorado-specific HR work?
4. How did the Town come to select this particular firm? The Town has not explained it.
5. Where is the employee handbook the Town paid $7,500 for — and when will the Board and the public see it?

## There was a closer way

An employee handbook is not exotic or specialized work. Colorado municipalities build, adapt, and publish their own as a matter of routine, and the resources to do it are ones Paonia already has.

The Town is a dues-paying member of CIRSA, the Colorado Intergovernmental Risk Sharing Agency — confirmed in the Town's own record, where CIRSA appears auditing Town facilities and conducting a Board training. CIRSA provides member towns with employment-policy review, an employment-law liability hotline, and HR, supervisory, and respectful-workplace training, all built around Colorado municipal law. That is HR support the Town already pays for. The Town also retains a Town Attorney, whose review is exactly the step that should localize any handbook to Colorado law. And other Colorado towns' employee handbooks are public documents, usable as starting templates already built around the correct statutes.

A handbook built that way would cost a fraction of $9,575, and — more to the point — it would be public, and it would be adopted by the Board in the open. It could also be written to address, deliberately, the gaps the Town's own recent history has exposed: a complaint procedure that runs to the Board rather than through the person being complained about; clear anti-retaliation protection; documented progressive discipline; rules on the integrity of personnel files; and a policy on the monitoring of employees. Those are standard sections of a properly built municipal handbook. They are only conspicuous in Paonia because the Town's documented failures are exactly the failures those standard sections exist to prevent.

The question was never whether the Town needed HR help. It plainly did, and does. The questions are why the help was procured the way it was — and why, a year and $9,575 later, there is still nothing the public can see for it.
