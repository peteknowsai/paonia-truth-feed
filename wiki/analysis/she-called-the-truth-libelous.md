*A resident handed out a flyer built entirely from the Town's own records. The Mayor responded with an official Town statement calling it "erroneous and libelous." She did not dispute a single source it cited. Under the law, calling true, sourced criticism "libelous" is not just wrong. It is backwards.*

AI Analysis

In March 2026, a resident distributed a flyer about the Town of Paonia. It was not a rumor sheet. Every claim in it was sourced to a public document: the Town's own budgets, court records, and published news coverage. Anyone who wanted to check it could.

Mayor Paige Smith's response was to author an official Town statement branding that criticism "erroneous and libelous." Read that carefully. Not "we disagree." Not "here is our side." Libelous, an accusation that the resident had published damaging falsehoods, issued in the name of the Town government itself.

Here is the problem with that word. In the law, truth is a complete defense to a claim of libel. A statement cannot be libelous if it is true. So to call the flyer libelous, the Mayor needed to show that its claims were false. She never did. In the Town's official response, she disputed not one of the sources the flyer cited. She attacked the criticism without laying a glove on a single fact behind it.

It went further than words. The Mayor pushed to spend public money on newspaper advertisements attacking the resident's criticism. Even this Board would not go that far, and refused the ads. The instinct, though, is the tell. Faced with documented criticism she could not rebut on the facts, the response was not to answer it but to brand it a lie and try to buy space to punish it.

And there is a final turn. In that same official statement, the Mayor made a factual claim of her own, that the Town's cameras were "motion activated." That claim is false, and the Town's own contract and audit logs prove it. So in an exchange the Mayor called "libelous," the criticism was provably true, and the one demonstrably false statement was hers.

This matters beyond one flyer. A mayor who answers sourced criticism by calling it a crime is telling every resident what happens if they speak up with the receipts in hand. It is the same lesson the Town's own employees learned: questioning those in power is treated as the offense. Public officials are supposed to answer criticism, not criminalize it.

A recall does not punish the Mayor for being criticized. It asks a simple question raised by how she answered. When a mayor brands true, documented criticism "libelous" and refuses to correct it, has she shown the judgment and honesty the office requires?
