## Summary

### Key Decisions

- **Agenda approved** -- Motion to approve agenda passed unanimously (voice vote)
- **Priority list for town board adopted** -- Motion by Thomas (seconded by Mike) to issue a prioritized list to the Board of Trustees based on committee survey results. Passed unanimously. Final list:
  1. Tap moratoriums and limits
  2. Raw water management
  3. Data collection
  4. Water treatment operations *(added via amendment by Phil, seconded by Thomas, passed unanimously)*
  5. Forthcoming studies
  6. Grants and loans
  7. Water company agreements
  8. Previous studies
  9. Billing and fees
  10. Demand management
  11. Planned growth
- **Next meeting set** -- March 24 at 5:00 PM (moved earlier to accommodate Travis's schedule)

### Discussion Highlights

- **Committee's role clarified** -- Members discussed the ordinance creating the advisory committee. Consensus: the committee is advisory, not an engineering firm. Their value is interpreting technical reports and translating them into plain language for the board. Mike Drake emphasized suggesting studies rather than conducting them.
- **Priority-setting methodology** -- Thomas compiled ~22 topics from prior discussions and emails, scored them by interdependence and impact (0-3 scale), then surveyed committee members (Tammy, Phil, Mike, Thomas responded). Results showed strong correlation between most impactful and most popular topics.
- **Tap moratorium concerns** -- Phil argued the moratorium question can't be answered without first understanding raw water supply. He estimated the town likely has only half of its 9.9 CFS legal water right in actual available raw water. Unused but purchased taps represent a significant liability.
- **Operations gap identified** -- Phil flagged that water treatment plant operations were missing from the priority list entirely. He characterized the February 2019 water system failure as a "black swan event" but noted underlying operations issues had been simmering for years.
- **Data collection approach** -- Thomas wants anonymized historic meter data (no names or addresses, just unique IDs) to analyze usage patterns over ~5 years. Goal: inform pricing, demand management, and growth decisions. He made clear he has no interest in individual account information.
- **Outside water companies** -- Phil outlined three tiers: 9 public water companies that buy from the town, 18 smaller sub-regulatory companies, and two special cases (Bone Mesa special district, Stewart Mesa large purchaser). Key question: does absorbing outside companies legally trigger obligations to provide sewer and other town services? Phil asked Corinne to get a legal opinion from town attorney Bo. Travis noted the state's only stipulation for absorption would be paperwork and additional sampling sites.
- **Stewart Mesa history** -- Mike (who sits on Stewart Mesa's board) shared that the relationship dates to 1906, born from a dispute over spring ownership. Stewart Mesa's board is open to town takeover as long as service continues.
- **Private working sessions debated** -- Phil asked whether the three engineers (Phil, Mike, Thomas) could meet privately to hash out technical details before bringing them to the full committee. Corinne cautioned this needs a legal opinion from the town attorney. Trustee Dave supported the value of private collaboration but emphasized full committee meetings must remain public.
- **Travis's participation gap noted** -- Corinne pointed out that Travis (appointed 5th member, town staff) hadn't received the survey documents and had no input on the priority list. Thomas said he'd been including Travis and Corinne on emails but hadn't received responses.
- **Lead and copper testing update** -- Travis reported bottles ordered for testing, expected by end of week. Confirmed COVID-19 allowed outdoor sampling from service lines rather than inside homes.

### Public Comment

- **Trustee Dave** -- Expressed support for the committee doing deep technical dives the board doesn't have time for. Wants strong recommendations on staffing, infrastructure, and next steps.
- **Suzanne Watson** -- Thanked the committee. Noted the town is updating its comprehensive plan and Colorado statute 31-23-206(1)(d) requires an adequate water supply component, making the committee's work directly relevant. Said she doesn't think open meetings laws apply to non-elected advisory members.
- **Russ Zick** (lives two miles outside town, receives Paonia water) -- Asked whether data collection includes demand/load analysis. Thomas confirmed yes.
- **Jake, Western Slope Conservation Center** -- Offered the center's connections and resources for water conservation work. Said the center doesn't fund environmental studies directly but could help secure grants. Expressed interest in GIS/mapping collaboration.
- **Christina** (partial name) -- Asked that the meeting recording be posted as soon as possible. Corinne confirmed she is recording and will post to YouTube.

### Notable Quotes

- **Phil:** "I've been drilling down through the documents and one of the interesting things is that the assumption has been that there either has not been very good engineering or what's been there has been misinterpreted. But I found an awful lot of terrific information in some of the older studies that were done back 2010, 2011, and a lot of the issues I think that we are looking at now have been known for a very long time."

- **Mike Drake:** "In simple terms, you have to take all the operational taps that we have, the potential taps that aren't in operation but they could come on at any time, how many taps does the town council want to have the ability to issue -- you've got to put a limit on it or we can't tell you whether or not you can do it -- and then we have to come up with a margin of safety to avoid situations like February 2019."

- **Thomas:** "I would like to get historic meter data from as many data points as we can. I want to make it 100% clear that I'm not looking to find out what any individual or any address has paid or has used. I would like to have anonymized data so that we can make smarter decisions about how we use water, how we price it, and possibly about how we grow the system."
