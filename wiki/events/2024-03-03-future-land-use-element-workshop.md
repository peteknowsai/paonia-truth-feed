## Summary

### Key Decisions
- No formal motions or votes -- this was a public workshop, not an official planning commission meeting (lack of quorum noted at the start)
- The comprehensive plan team confirmed they are **not proposing to lift the water tap moratorium**
- A schedule change was announced: the April 3rd meeting will cover **Economic Development** instead of Transportation (those two topics are being swapped)
- Short-term rentals and ADUs will get their own dedicated workshop the week of March 18th
- Public feedback on the land use element is open for **two weeks** following this meeting

### Discussion Highlights
- **Future Land Use Map:** The team presented a draft future land use map with categories including Downtown Commercial Core, Mixed Use, Downtown Neighborhood (R3), Town Original Neighborhood, Traditional Neighborhood, Conservation Neighborhood, and Urban Farmland. These are **not zoning designations** -- they are meant to guide future zoning decisions.
- **Water moratorium context:** Growth discussed is limited to already-approved taps and potential future Arch Coal taps. Stefan (Town Administrator) emphasized the moratorium is short-term; the comp plan is a 20-year document. He warned that if the moratorium lasts 20 years, water bills will become unaffordable.
- **Urban Farmland zones** cover most of the UGA (Urban Growth Area) surrounding town, intended to preserve agricultural viewsheds and wildlife corridors while allowing some residential development (e.g., seasonal/farm worker housing).
- **Mixed Use areas** were proposed to allow live-work situations and light industrial, addressing a current gap where industrial zones prohibit dwelling units by right.
- **Wildlife corridors vs. commercial development on Hwy 133:** Multiple commenters raised concerns that commercial development along 133 would block wildlife migration from the mountains to the river. The team acknowledged this tension.
- **County relationship:** The town needs to update its Intergovernmental Agreement (IGA) with Delta County, last done around 2011-2013. Without an updated comprehensive plan, the town defaults to the county's planning vision.
- **Density discussion:** Current zoning allows up to 3 units/parcel in red zones (downtown neighborhood) and 2 units/parcel in yellow zones (traditional neighborhood). The team asked where gentle density increases would be appropriate.
- **Policies presented:** Eight draft policies covering character preservation, development consistency, affordable housing, wildlife habitat protection, and others. Public suggested additions around dark sky protections, source water protection, climate action/tree canopy, and prioritizing existing residents over new development.

### Public Comment
- **Dark sky policy** requested -- concern about commercial development (e.g., Home Depot) bringing light pollution
- **Short-term rental concerns** raised repeatedly -- multiple speakers wanted legislation preferring long-term residents over STRs; redirected to the March 18th workshop
- **Arch Coal water taps** -- a commenter noted hundreds of acres tied to coal company taps will likely be developed someday, asked why that wasn't addressed
- **Eviction protections** requested -- finding legal ways to prohibit evicting long-term renters in favor of short-term rentals
- **Source water protection** -- request to add policy protecting drinking water sources on federal land
- **"Preserve what we have first"** -- speaker urged prioritizing existing community, infrastructure, and residents before planning new growth
- **ADU concerns** -- speaker warned that ADU development raises property values, potentially pricing out lower-income buyers (cited Golden, CO as cautionary example)
- **Growth paying its own way** -- briefly raised as a policy concept
- **Industrial zone dwelling** -- a longtime resident in the industrial zone requested allowing live-work arrangements
- **Fire/wildland interface** -- requested as an explicit addition to action items
- **Climate action and tree canopy** -- speaker requested policies encouraging tree planting for cooling as temperatures rise
- **Wildlife corridor fencing** -- concern that urban farmland owners will put up tall fences blocking wildlife access to the river
- **Walkability and density overlap** -- speaker wanted pedestrian access integrated with any density increases, particularly near the old water treatment plant area and the riverfront trail

### Notable Quotes
- Stefan (Town Administrator): "If we have a water moratorium for 20 years and growth doesn't happen in this town, our water bills are going to be so excessive that even if I had all of my kids working, we won't be able to afford our water bills."
- Audience member on preserving community: "There's people existing here that have been born and raised here that for some things that are coming down the pipes won't be able to keep their house or stay in Paonia."
- Stefan on the importance of the comp plan: "If we are undefined and we don't have an improved comprehensive plan, we will default to the county's planning. So part of this process is about getting a plan in place that's reflective of what this community here wants to be and how we want to grow."
