### Discussion Highlights
- **Community survey results presented** — A survey was shared covering development priorities. Residents favored keeping Paonia's charm and character, opposing big box stores and environmentally harmful industries. Top challenges identified: job opportunities, housing for all income levels, and protecting natural resources.
- **Renewable energy ranked as top economic development priority**, followed closely by supporting existing businesses. The presenter emphasized that "growth" means sustaining what's already here, not population growth.
- **Recreational marijuana legalization** — Approximately 82% of survey respondents favored legalizing recreational marijuana. About 50% said tax revenue should go toward roads, water, and sewer infrastructure.
- **Creative District presentation by Susie (Creative Coalition director)** — Reported on 8 years of work in the North Fork Valley. After a 45% loss of creative jobs around 2011 (tied to coal mine closures), creative employment grew from 96 to 180 jobs by 2018. Creative sector earnings for ZIP code 81428 grew from ~$3.3 million (2011) to ~$5.5 million (2018).
- **Creative District partnerships** listed: Paradise Theater, KVNF Mountain Grown Radio, Blue Sage Center for the Arts, Elsewhere Studios, Western Slope Conservation Center, Citizens for a Healthy Community, Chamber of Commerce, Delta County Tourism Cabinet.
- **Space to Create project** — A state initiative (selected over Carbondale and one other finalist) with partners including DOLA and Colorado Creative Industries. Project estimated at ~$3.1 million. The town acknowledged it doesn't have funds to allocate, so the project relies on outside grants. Phase 1 complete; Phase 2 (pre-development) underway.
- **Public art policy discussion** — A request to develop a formal public art policy for the town, covering zoning, insurance/liability, handicap accessibility, and compliance. The ask was framed as logistical/legal support rather than direct funding, with grants and a possible sales tax initiative as funding mechanisms. Carbondale's public art policy cited as a model.

### Public Comment
- A community member raised the idea of **supporting existing creative workers through zoning reform** — making it easier to work from home and reducing regulatory barriers.
- Another resident expressed support for a **sales tax initiative to fund arts and culture**.
- A speaker advocated for **public art installations** (e.g., sculptures) while noting the need for proper accessibility and compliance frameworks.

### Notable Quotes

- Susie: "These are facts and figures that support the creative district and the fact that arts and culture is playing a role in the economy here — not just the economy, but it's a wonderful vibrant community to live in."
- On public art policy: "I don't really see it as a funding ask from the town, more like setting up all the logistics, the legal things for us to be able to continue to create public art."
