Staff report cover (page 595)

"We recognize that video surveillance technology can serve legitimate public safety and property protection purposes when deployed responsibly and transparently. We also recognize the potential for abuse with regard to facial recognition, license plate reading, and immigration enforcement, among other concerns. We would like to formally address the absence of public policy in this regard in a way that allows the intended use of security cameras at Town facilities while strictly prohibiting uses that were not intended."

Budget impact: "Staff time."

Title

AN ORDINANCE OF THE BOARD OF TRUSTEES OF THE TOWN OF PAONIA, COLORADO, ESTABLISHING REQUIREMENTS AND REGULATIONS GOVERNING THE INSTALLATION AND OPERATION OF GOVERNMENT VIDEO SURVEILLANCE SYSTEMS ON TOWN-OWNED OR TOWN-CONTROLLED PROPERTY; REQUIRING THE ADOPTION AND MAINTENANCE OF A PUBLIC USE POLICY; ESTABLISHING RESTRICTIONS ON FOOTAGE RETENTION, ACCESS, AND DISCLOSURE; PROVIDING TRANSPARENCY AND ACCOUNTABILITY MECHANISMS; AND PROVIDING PENALTIES FOR VIOLATION THEREOF.

Recitals (highlights)

  • "Government video surveillance of public spaces implicates the privacy interests of residents, visitors, and employees, and that such interests deserve legal protection."
  • "The absence of a publicly available use policy, clear retention limits, and meaningful oversight creates unacceptable risks of misuse and erodes public trust."
  • "The Federal Trade Commission has taken enforcement action against Verkada, Inc. relating to data security and access control failures, underscoring the importance of robust governance requirements for cloud-connected surveillance systems."
  • Authority cited: Article XX of the Colorado Constitution and Title 31 of the Colorado Revised Statutes.

Section 1 — Purpose and Intent

Stated goals:

  • Authorize regulated use for public safety and property protection;
  • Protect privacy and civil liberties in surveilled spaces;
  • Ensure transparency through mandatory public disclosure of policies and camera locations;
  • Establish meaningful oversight, accountability, and enforcement;
  • "Prevent mission creep, unauthorized access, and indiscriminate monitoring of residents."

Section 2 — Definitions

  • "Authorized Purpose" — purposes enumerated in §4.
  • "Designee" — Town employee/officer/contractor authorized by the Town Administrator to access footage.
  • "Footage" — any video, audio, image, or metadata recorded, stored, or transmitted, "whether stored locally or in a cloud-based environment."
  • "Privacy Impact Assessment" / "PIA" — written analysis defined in §6.
  • "Sensitive Location" — heightened expectation of privacy: restrooms, locker rooms, changing areas, and "interior spaces not regularly open to the public."
  • "Surveillance Camera" — any fixed or mobile electronic device capable of capturing/recording/transmitting/storing video, "including but not limited to IP cameras, network-connected cameras, and cloud-integrated camera systems."
  • "Surveillance System" — one or more cameras, plus software platforms, storage, and cloud components.
  • "Use Policy" — Town-wide use policy adopted under §5.

Section 3 — Scope and Applicability

Applies to all surveillance cameras "owned, leased, operated, or controlled" by the Town. Does not apply to:

  • Body-worn cameras under separate policy;
  • Temporary recording for a specific investigation under warrant or court order;
  • Private cameras on private property, except where the Town has contractual access to footage.

Section 4 — Authorized Purposes (exclusive list)

The Town may install/operate surveillance cameras only for:

  • Protection of Town facilities, buildings, infrastructure, equipment from vandalism, theft, or unauthorized access;
  • Monitoring of Town parking, public parks, recreational areas;
  • Deterrence and documentation of criminal activity in areas with documented incidents;
  • Protection of employees and the public in Town facilities;
  • Traffic monitoring at intersections or rights-of-way "where authorized by separate resolution."

"Surveillance cameras shall not be operated for any purpose not listed above."

Section 5 — Mandatory Use Policy

(a) No surveillance camera or system shall be operated unless the Board has adopted, by resolution, a written Surveillance Camera Use Policy.

(b) Use Policy must address, at minimum:

  • "A complete inventory of all surveillance cameras operated by the Town, including their locations, fields of view, and Authorized Purpose";
  • Categories of personnel authorized to access live or recorded footage and under what circumstances;
  • Maximum retention period — "shall not exceed ninety (90) days for routine recordings absent an active investigation, legal hold, or insurance claim";
  • Footage-sharing standards with law enforcement, agencies, third parties;
  • "Prohibitions on use of footage for facial recognition, license plate reader aggregation, or behavioral analytics absent explicit Board authorization by ordinance";
  • CORA response procedure (C.R.S. § 24-72-201 et seq.);
  • Complaint intake and investigation procedure;
  • Training requirements;
  • Penalties for unauthorized access or misuse.

(c) Posting requirements: prominent location on Town website, available for inspection at Town Hall, "Reviewed and re-adopted or updated at least once every two (2) years."

(d) Material amendments require public meeting with at least 14 days' advance notice.

Section 6 — Privacy Impact Assessment

(a) Before any new install or material expansion/relocation, the Town Administrator (or law enforcement designee) "shall prepare and submit to the Board of Trustees a written Privacy Impact Assessment."

(b) PIA contents:

  • Description: location, field of view, technical specs;
  • Authorized Purpose(s);
  • Privacy interests affected and proportionality;
  • Less-invasive alternatives considered;
  • Cybersecurity measures;
  • Identity and contractual obligations of any third-party vendor.

(c) Board approval required at a public meeting before installation; may be conditioned on additional safeguards.

(d) For cameras already in operation: TA must submit a PIA "to the Board within ninety (90) days of adoption."

Section 7 — Location Restrictions

Cameras shall not be installed:

  • In any Sensitive Location (§2);
  • Where field of view captures the interior of a private residence;
  • Where the camera "would primarily capture lawful activity on private property";
  • In any location not authorized by approved PIA and Use Policy.

"Cameras shall be positioned to minimize incidental capture of areas beyond the scope of the Authorized Purpose, and shall be physically adjusted or shielded as needed."

Section 8 — Access, Security, and Data Protection

  • Access limited to TA, authorized law enforcement under lawful legal process, and §5 Designees.
  • Public access log required: "All access to footage shall be logged in a written or electronic access log recording the date, time, identity of the person accessing footage, the footage accessed, and the stated purpose. The access log shall be treated as a public record subject to inspection upon request."
  • Cybersecurity safeguards: passwords, access controls, encryption in transit and at rest.
  • Breach notice: "Within seventy-two (72) hours of discovery."
  • Vendor restriction: Cloud vendors "shall not use footage for any commercial purpose, including advertising, analytics, or product development."

Section 9 — Oversight and Reporting

  • Annual written report to Board at first meeting of each calendar year, covering: number/location of cameras, summary of all access log entries, complaints and disposition, security incidents, recommended changes/decommissioning.
  • Annual report posted publicly on Town website.
  • Board may, at any time by majority vote, "order an audit of any aspect of the surveillance program, including inspection of the access log, vendor contracts, and security configurations."

Section 10 — Prohibited Uses

"Notwithstanding any other provision of this Ordinance or the Use Policy, Town surveillance cameras shall not be used for:

  • Facial recognition or biometric identification, unless explicitly authorized by a separate ordinance adopted by the Board following a public hearing;
  • License plate reader data aggregation or tracking of individual vehicle movements over time;
  • Real-time or retrospective tracking of specific individuals based on race, ethnicity, religion, national origin, political affiliation, sexual orientation, or other protected characteristics;
  • Sharing footage with federal immigration enforcement authorities without a valid court order;
  • Any commercial purpose, including sale or licensing of footage to third parties."

Section 11 — Interim Operation and Compliance Deadline

  • Existing systems must come into full compliance within 180 days of effective date (the "Compliance Deadline").
  • During interim, existing cameras may operate "solely for the Authorized Purposes enumerated in Section 4," subject to §§7–10.
  • Failure to comply by the deadline → suspension; if compliance can't be achieved within an additional 30 days → decommissioning.

Section 12 — Enforcement and Penalties

(a) Town officers/employees who knowingly violate face "disciplinary action up to and including termination, in addition to any penalties provided by applicable law."

(b) Public complaint process: any person may file written complaint with Town Clerk; TA or law enforcement designee must investigate and respond in writing within 30 days.

(c) Vendor violations may result in contract termination.

Section 13 — Severability

Standard severability clause.

Section 14 — Effective Date

"Thirty (30) days following final passage and publication as required by C.R.S. § 31-16-103."

Notable

  • Section 10's named-class non-discrimination clause (race, religion, sexual orientation, etc.) is unusually explicit for a small-town surveillance ordinance. The same clause prohibits sharing with federal immigration enforcement absent a court order — a direct response to ICE concerns raised in the April 22 Verkada meeting notes and the Hunter/Mejorado preamble.
  • Section 5(b) sets a 90-day max retention "absent an active investigation, legal hold, or insurance claim" — matching the current 30-day Verkada retention reported by staff but allowing headroom.
  • Section 8(b)'s public access log would, if adopted, expose every footage-access event to CORA — a structural transparency change beyond what current Verkada/Town policy records.
  • Section 6(d)'s 90-day retroactive PIA requirement would force the existing system to undergo a privacy-impact review by August 2026 (roughly) if the ordinance is adopted at the April 28 meeting and effective 30 days later.
  • The packet language explicitly states the ordinance is "NOT up for adoption at this time" — only edits/direction.
