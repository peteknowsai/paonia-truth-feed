The one-paragraph version

Greg Sund is an ICMA-credentialed manager who ran permanent posts in Kansas and Colorado through 2019 and has worked only interim jobs since. His last permanent job — Trinidad city manager — ended when he resigned to avoid being fired in September 2019. Thirteen months later he resigned mid-meeting in Walsenburg after that council declined to extend him. The detailed account of why Trinidad soured on him is a complaint letter alleging autocratic, board-bypassing management — but it was written by the man who then took his job, and none of it was ever litigated. At his three most recent stops (Telluride, Mt. Crested Butte, Georgetown) the record is clean to positive. Two patterns are well-supported; a third, broader "bad-manager-everywhere" claim is not.

Pattern 1 — He doesn't stay (perpetual interim since 2019)

Every post since Trinidad has been interim or short-term, and none converted to permanent. He was a finalist for the permanent job at Telluride (2022) and Silt (2024) and was passed over both times.

For Paonia: hiring Sund means hiring a short-term stabilizer, not a long-term manager. That may be exactly what an interim role is for — but the board should be clear-eyed that this is the realistic horizon.

Pattern 2 — Two boards pushed him out in 13 months

  • Trinidad, Sept 5, 2019 — resigned to avoid termination; council members had been working to remove him.
  • Walsenburg, Dec 2, 2020 — a motion to advertise his job tied 3–3, a motion to extend his contract failed 4–2, and he resigned the same day, calling the meeting disrespectful.

Two different elected boards, two conflict-driven exits, close together. This is the most defensible "second pattern" beyond the interim churn: a recurring breakdown in the manager-to-board relationship.

The Trinidad story, honestly told

Trinidad's council never stated a public reason for moving against him. The mayor only expressed disappointment about rumors swirling before the decision. The single detailed account is a five-page complaint letter from department head Michael Valentine (full breakdown), delivered to council right before the resignation. Its charges:

  • Autocratic style; told the finance director "this is not a democracy" and walked out.
  • Personnel handling that allegedly risked discrimination lawsuits.
  • Repeatedly putting items before council without fully informing them.
  • Botched deals and finance calls (La Puerta development agreement, a zoning rewrite, a landfill enterprise-fund ballot package, the Sister Blandina Park plan, a "Noah's Ark" building shell).
  • Months-long staff vacancies and morale collapse.

Two essential caveats:

  1. Valentine became Sund's successor — interim, then permanent Trinidad city manager. The most damning document was authored by the person who got the job. Weigh it as interested advocacy, not a neutral finding.
  2. None of it was litigated. Searches of federal and Colorado court records turn up no discrimination or employment suit against Sund or the City tied to these allegations.

The charge is serious and specific; the source has an obvious stake. Both things are true.

What we checked that does NOT support a broader pattern

In fairness to the candidate, the "bad manager" story does not travel:

Stop What the record actually shows
Walsenburg (2020) He inherited books that were all "out of balance" (errors predating him), worked the fix with the finance director, and quit in protest when the mayor/council turned on him. Reads as reformer, not villain.
Telluride (2022) Lost the permanent job to Vail's sitting town manager. No friction reported.
Mt. Crested Butte (2022) Unqualified public praise from the mayor on departure; the later staff exits were not pinned on him.
Georgetown (2024–25) Confirmed from Board minutes: the town's mass exodus happened before he arrived; he steadied finances and staffing, recruited his own permanent successor, and got a public thank-you. Genuinely clean.
Kansas (2010–17) Thin-to-clean. Only oddity: an abrupt 2015 Ellis County resignation with no stated cause.

The honest conclusion: the documented conflict pattern is concentrated in the 2019–2020 Trinidad/Walsenburg window. His most recent work is clean. A claim that he mismanages everywhere he goes is not supported by the record and should not be made.

Bottom line for the board

Three questions the record says are fair to put on the table tonight — without prejudging the answers:

  1. The Trinidad departure. Ask him directly why he resigned to avoid termination, and what the Valentine letter's allegations were about. A credible candidate should be able to address it. (Note the author's later promotion when weighing his answer.)
  2. The board relationship. Two boards moved him out in 13 months. What changed after 2020 — and what would keep the Paonia relationship from going the same way?
  3. Tenure expectations. Every post since 2019 has been short-term. Is Paonia hiring a months-long stabilizer, and is that what the Town needs right now?

What the record does not support: treating him as a proven serial bad actor. The strong, defensible facts are the interim pattern, the two board-driven exits, and one serious-but-interested-party complaint from Trinidad.

Update — the May 29 meeting

The Board heard the candidate on May 29, 2026, and the analysis above largely held:

  • The interim pattern: Sund confirmed it himself, describing how towns find it "easiest to let the manager go" amid board turnover and recounting boards that churned beneath him (Dickinson: "14 different people on a five-member board").
  • The Trinidad question: asked about the "this is not a democracy" line, he confirmed making it, attributing it to board direction — neither denying the statement nor disputing the letter.
  • The process concern proved decisive. The candidate arrived through the CCMA pipeline as the lone name ("no other interim candidates"); the contract packet was posted only a day ahead; and Trustee Foster declined to hire without reference checks. The motion died for lack of a second, and a June 3 follow-up meeting was set. source

The "this is not a democracy" remark — a closer look

In fairness, the remark was made to a direct report, not to the public. Sund has said it concerned department-head accountability, and at the May 29 meeting he attributed it to direction from the Trinidad board. A comment like that, made once in the heat of a hard moment, would be unremarkable.

What is harder to set aside is that he has had more than seven years to reflect on it. The remark was serious enough to appear in a formal written complaint to the Trinidad council and to figure in the move that ended his tenure there. Yet asked about it again in Paonia, he defended it as board direction rather than reckoning with what it means — and never engaged the underlying question of whether a town government is a democratic institution at all. (At the same meeting, a former mayor told the Board from the floor, "this is not a democracy. We are a republic… so we are not a democracy." source)

A town government is a democratic institution. For the people who staff it to wave that away — even to a subordinate, even years later, without apparent second thought — is a troubling posture in an office whose only job is to carry out the public's will. This is not about a single sentence said once. It is about whether the person hired to execute the public's decisions believes the public is sovereign.
