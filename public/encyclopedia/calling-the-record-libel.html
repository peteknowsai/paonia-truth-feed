They Called the Public Record "Libel"

The tempo is the tell. A formal complaint about the administration drew silence for months. A flyer citing the Town's own documents drew an official rebuttal in five days. The difference was not the facts. It was who the facts were about.

The complaint that drew silence

In November 2024, planning commissioner Pete McCarthy filed a formal written complaint to all trustees laying out his concerns about the administration. He received no replies. He resigned weeks later — again, to silence. A complaint routed through the proper channel produced nothing.

The flyer that drew a special meeting

In March 2026, McCarthy distributed a flyer — "10 Things Paonia Should Know About Its Town Administrator", captioned "All facts from public records" — citing the Town's own budgets, court records, and published news coverage. Within five days, the Board held a special meeting to respond.

Mayor Paige Smith's own agenda report called the flyer "libelous." The special meeting's agenda proposed both a "Statement of Fact" rebutting the flyer and taxpayer-funded display ads in three newspapers. On a 5–1 vote — Smith voting for publication — the Board adopted the response: a "Letter to Town of Paonia Citizens" that branded a resident's criticism "erroneous and libelous."

What the rebuttal never disputed

Calling something "libelous" is a claim that it is false. So the test is simple: which of the flyer's cited facts did the Town's response actually disprove? A line-by-line review of the rebuttal found the answer is none. The Town disputed the framing, the tone, and the messenger — but not a single underlying source the flyer cited. A document built entirely from public records was branded a lie, and the rebuttal left every one of those records standing.

Why this matters

When a government brands its own public record "libel," the charge is aimed less at the resident than at everyone watching who might say something next. The complaint drew silence; the flyer drew a five-day response and the word "libelous." Both responses sent the same message about what happens to people who put the record in front of their neighbors.
