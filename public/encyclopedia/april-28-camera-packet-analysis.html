What the April 28 Camera Packet Actually Says

What the April 28 Camera Packet Actually Says

The April 28 agenda has two camera items: H.8 ("Whether to Turn Some Cameras Off while an Adequate Framework of Governance is Put Into Place") and H.9 (the Hunter/Mejorado draft surveillance ordinance, tabled from April 14). Read together, the packet tells a clearer story than either item does alone.

1. The agenda title for H.8 is misleading

The item is captioned as Consideration of Whether to Turn Some Cameras Off. The recommended motion in Wynn's staff report is:

"Review the attached Town Administrator Memorandum and supporting documentation, and provide policy direction regarding the governance, oversight, and future administration of the Town's camera system."

There is no proposed motion to turn anything off. The action item is "discuss." Whatever the title implies — that staff might disable cameras pending the ordinance — the packet does not deliver that. A trustee who wants cameras off has to bring that motion themselves.

2. Wynn's audio position quietly walked back

The April 14 Wynn memo was structured around denying claims from 10 Things You Should Know About Paonia's New Town Cameras / Lucy Hunter's Rebuttal Flyer - "10 Things" Response. Two weeks later, Section 7 of the new memo now confirms:

"Certain cameras have audio capability, though it must be intentionally enabled and is not universally active."

And in Attachment B (Camera Discussion):

"Yes, the two cameras in the building have audio capabilities. They have been used to see how staff interacted with difficult situations."

Combined with the Verkada vendor list (Attachment G — D50, D80, CD22, CH52-E, CM22, CM41-S, and Bullet series have no audio; "All other cameras will have that setting"), the new posture is: most models can do audio, two interior Town Hall cameras are doing it, and audio recordings have been pulled to review staff conduct. That is a partial concession of one of the original 10 Things You Should Know About Paonia's New Town Cameras claims — without ever naming it as a correction. See also Factual Corrections to the Administrator's April 14, 2026 Memorandum.

The framing line — "To the extent that prior descriptions have simplified how these features operate, I believe this additional context helps clarify the technical distinction" — is the closest the memo gets to acknowledging the April 14 memo was wrong on this point.

3. Process-legitimacy claim is now hedged

April 14 memo: the cameras were approved through public process at multiple noticed meetings.

April 28 memo, Attachment B, verbatim:

"(verify) I believe this was discussed at six separate public meetings, or various pieces of installing and purchasing cameras were discussed in public meetings. No public comment was given at those meetings, but it was offered."

The "(verify)" parenthetical and "I believe … or various pieces" qualifiers downgrade the claim from a statement of fact to a recollection. This matters because the public-process argument was central to staff's earlier defense — and central to why the surveillance citizen initiative was framed as unnecessary.

4. Funding sources reveal "Back the Badge" was tapped

Attachment B answer to "Was the system funded by grants or taxpayer dollars?":

"general fund, water enterprise, sewer enterprise, trash enterprise — of which sales tax capital improvement, surplus disposal of equipment, and back the badge were all utilized to purchase this equipment."

"Back the Badge" is typically a community/donation channel for police equipment — its appearance in the camera funding mix is notable. It also confirms that water and sewer ratepayer dollars helped pay for surveillance, not just enterprise-asset protection cameras at the plants. Worth a CORA on the dollar split per fund.

5. The ICE scenario was raised in a Verkada meeting

Recorded by staff during the April 22 meeting with Verkada (Attachment B):

"If ICE were to come to Verkada with a federal warrant, their answer would be we don't own that data, the user owns that data and federal agencies would need to go to the user with that warrant."

This is a vendor statement, not a Town policy. Verkada's position is "we'll punt to the customer." The Town's policy on what it would do when handed an ICE warrant is not in this packet. The Hunter/Mejorado draft ordinance Section 10 would address it: "Sharing footage with federal immigration enforcement authorities without a valid court order" would be prohibited. Without the ordinance, that decision rests with the Town Administrator.

6. Two cameras inside Town Hall record audio of "difficult situations"

Per Attachment B and Attachment E (Vetter's memo), the Clerk's office uses cameras to "verify customer/employee complaints" and document "accusations/verbal threats/threatening behavior by customers." Two of those cameras are inside the building with audio enabled.

This is legal under Colorado one-party consent if a Town employee is party to the conversation (per Attorney Buchner's response). It also means a member of the public walking into Town Hall to file a complaint or pay a utility bill is being audio-recorded. There is no posted notice referenced anywhere in the packet that informs the public this is happening.

7. The ordinance is being slow-rolled

The Hunter/Mejorado draft ordinance was on the April 14 agenda — tabled. It is on the April 28 agenda — explicitly "NOT up for adoption at this time. Possible motions include edits to the Draft and directing Staff for further edits / review."

Mechanically: even if the Board liked the draft, two readings are required (Section 14: effective 30 days after final passage). At minimum, the earliest possible effective date is now July 2026, and that's only if the Board moves it to first reading on May 12. Item H.9 as packaged for April 28 doesn't even contemplate first reading — it contemplates more discussion.

The ordinance has good provisions (90-day max retention, public access logs, Privacy Impact Assessments, immigration-enforcement carve-out). The pacing means the cameras keep operating under their existing un-policied state for several more months while the ordinance is "discussed."

8. The same packet has the new trustees being sworn in

Item H.1 — Swearing in of new trustees — happens before items H.8/H.9 in the agenda order. So the camera votes are the first significant policy votes for whoever the new trustees are. Whether that helps or hurts the ordinance depends on who got elected and what they ran on.

What to watch on Tuesday

  1. Does any trustee actually move to disable specific cameras (e.g., Town Hall interior audio cameras) pending the ordinance? Staff did not tee that up.
  2. Does the Board move the ordinance to first reading, or just keep "discussing"?
  3. Does anyone ask why the agenda title implies a turn-off vote that staff didn't actually prepare?
  4. Is the audio-recording walkback acknowledged on the record, or does it pass silently?
  5. Public comment — three minutes per speaker, one time per item.

Open questions worth a CORA

  • Per-fund dollar breakdown of the camera-system purchases (general fund / water / sewer / trash / sales tax CIP / surplus / Back the Badge).
  • The "six public meetings" — which specific meetings, with packet pages where camera procurement was on the agenda. The "(verify)" qualifier means staff isn't sure either.
  • Audit log of every Verkada footage access by Town personnel over the last 12 months. (See C 26-09 for what staff has previously produced; the new ordinance Section 8 would make this a standing public record.)
  • Names of the "former employee" who originally requested the cameras (Attachment B) and the staff/department-head approval chain.
