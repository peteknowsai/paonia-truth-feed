Water Infrastructure and Debt

TypeIssue

The town's largest financial commitment: a $9.74M water infrastructure loan, water rate increases, and the ongoing tank rehabilitation project. This is also the administration's primary defense for its budget decisions.

What the Record Shows

The loan

  • The town took on a $9.74M water infrastructure loan adding $263K/year in debt payments through 2045. source
  • The FY2026 adopted budget states water rates "will need to continue to increase over time to support repayment of existing and future debt." (FY2026 Budget, p.5) source

The projects

  • Three water infrastructure projects combined: $10,803,000 price tag. Funded by $10M in grants (50%) and low-interest loans (50%). source
  • 2M gallon treated water tank is being relined.
  • Water meters on mountain spring complexes (sole source of Paonia's drinking water) being installed.
  • 10,000 feet of 50+ year old rusted steel pipe being replaced.
  • Water tank rehab: original estimate $2,121,200. After change orders, projected total ~$1,761,670 (roughly $360K under estimate). Contractor NPC missed substantial completion date of March 24, 2026. Town charging liquidated damages: $300/day through final inspection, $1,000/day thereafter. source

The budget picture

  • FY2026 adopted budget is $16.16M. Only ~$4.6M is the town's own revenue. The rest is grants and loans. source
  • Fund balances projected to drop 23% in a single year ($5.4M to $4.1M). source
  • Bridge Reserve projected down 73.5%. source

The board's position

  • The board characterizes the grants and loans as "the most significant recent achievement for our small Town" and the "ONLY way to afford these expensive projects." source
  • The board states these projects would not have been possible "without the leadership, level of competence and long-range planning provided by our Town Administrator." source

Pete's position

  • Not that grants and loans are wrong, but that 69% dependency on external funding creates fiscal vulnerability. source
  • Water rates will keep increasing. Citizens should understand the long-term cost.

What the Record Does Not Show

  • Detailed breakdowns of the grant vs. loan portions for each of the three projects
  • Interest rates on the loans
  • Projected water rate increases over the 20-year loan period
  • Whether the town considered alternative approaches or vendors
  • The full capital improvement plan showing how water infrastructure fits alongside other spending ($1M generators, $500K meters, $250K squad cars, etc.)
