This is the City's Amended Motion for Summary Judgment — its full argument, with record evidence, for why Stefen Wynn's whistleblower retaliation case should be decided in the City's favor without trial. (It amends a 2026-05-21 motion solely to correct three scrivener errors.) Everything below is the City's account, supported by its exhibits — depositions, declarations, emails, and meeting minutes — but not yet ruled on by the court. Wynn's side is in his Second Amended Complaint, summarized at Wynn v. City of Neptune Beach (Case No. 23-CA-8801).

The City's two-part argument

  1. Wynn was never a whistleblower. As City Manager he was the City's chief executive officer under the City Code (Sec. 2-81). Under Fla. Stat. § 112.3187, a protected disclosure about a local government must go to "a chief executive officer ... or other appropriate local official" with authority to remedy the violation. Every department Wynn complained about — police, building/permits, finance — reported to him. The only authority above him was the City Council, and the City Clerk's record search found no protected disclosure by Wynn to the Council (Ponson Declaration). In the City's phrase: Wynn "never 'blew the whistle' on anyone, but himself."
  2. Even if he had been, the firing was for cause unrelated to any whistleblowing — his performance failures around a Development Agreement and the "big green monster" water tank, plus dishonesty about what he knew (the same-decision defense).

The water tank record (City's evidence)

The termination centers on a 29-foot-high, 180,000-gallon water tank erected behind a new Publix in December 2022, in view of residents' backyards — dubbed "the big green monster."

  • The 2021 Development Agreement (FL Realty LLC, 540–580 Atlantic Blvd) limited improvements to those in its exhibits; no water tank appeared in them, and the City Manager was the designated checkpoint: he attended coordination meetings and had 9 business days to object in writing to deviations. Material changes required Council approval.
  • Summer 2021: Publix hydrant flow tests showed poor water flow; a tank and pump were determined necessary. A permit to construct the fire pump/tank enclosure was applied for 2021-12-12 and issued by the City 2022-03-02.
  • 2022-06-24/27: Wynn picked the tank's color. Developer rep Andrew Greene emailed the City about color selection (blue or green); Wynn replied "Green in color is good, but I'd like to see the Southern elevation that includes the tank." Community Development Director Sam Brisolara then emailed Wynn the only site plan showing the tank's placement along with its dimensions: 33' internal diameter, 29' to the eave, 180,000 gallons (Wynn Depo. Tr. 156:2–158:4, Exs. 11–12).
  • December 2022: the tank went up; angry resident emails reached Wynn and the Council (Nancy Vannoy: "massive eye sore"; Susan Cicchino: "not one meeting, announcement or discussion took place ... It is simply WRONG").
  • 2022-12-09: Wynn told the Council "The first time that I had seen the site plan for the Publix was today when they were attached to the previous email," and told a subordinate "Hi Piper, I've never seen this site plan before today." The City says this is directly contradicted by his June 2022 emails.
  • New CDD Heather Whitmore's declaration: while gathering records for a public records request about the tank, she found the June 2022 emails. She states Wynn came into her office, closed the door, and told her that if she found anything about the water tank, to run it by him first so he could decide whether it was OK to send to someone else. Uncomfortable, she called City Attorney Zach Roth, who had her forward everything to him.
  • Wynn requested a stop work order on the tank (issued 2023-01-05); the developer's attorneys filed a public records request the next day, which the City says created litigation exposure to the developer.
  • 2023-01-10: the mayor and City Attorney confronted Wynn with the emails. Roth: "Mr. Wynn told us to our faces that he didn't know anything about the tank, and when it was shown that he picked the color, that was proven to be a lie."
  • Roth issued opinion letters 2023-01-16/17 concluding cause existed; the 2023-01-17 letter noted Wynn's two explanations of when he learned the tank's size contradicted each other.
  • 2023-01-17: the Council voted unanimously to terminate for "Flagrant Neglect of Duty" and, in the alternative, "Willful Misconduct" (motion by Councilor Nia Livingston).

The "whistleblowing" documents (City's account)

  • Exhibit B (the claimed fraud report to auditors) was the auditors' standard annual questionnaire. Wynn's actual answer to whether he was aware of or suspected fraud: "I'm unaware of any fraudulent activity occurring during the FY-2021 budget year within the City of Neptune Beach's financial processes." Auditor Miller's declaration: "Mr. Wynn never reported fraud to me or my firm."
  • Exhibits D & E (October 2022 letters) addressed only potential fraud and internal-controls measures Wynn himself was implementing — his own job. City Attorney Roth: "I frankly didn't understand the letter because everything that Mr. Wynn allegedly was complaining about was within Mr. Wynn's purview to resolve." The documents never use the word "whistleblower"; only the copy Wynn emailed to Roth had an attachment renamed "Financial Management Irregularities (Whistleblowing)" — the hand-delivered copies to councilmembers did not, as Wynn conceded at deposition.

The Councilor Key accusations (City's account)

  • Shortly after arriving in 2020, Wynn fired the City's CFO and took over CFO duties himself; he hired Carl LeFleur as CFO in July 2020 and fired him a year later. The auditors attributed tracking problems to "the City's frequent turnover in financial personnel." Audits were significantly delayed under Wynn; the City caught up under his successor.
  • August 2022: Wynn told Councilor Lauren Key she was "towing a very fine ethical line" over her husband, Police Commander Mike Key. He then told the police chief that the Keys had "committed a felony" because he believed Cmd. Key was in the room during a budget phone call with Councilor Key. Chief Pike checked: Cmd. Key was not in the room — and being there would not have been a felony anyway.
  • City Attorney Roth reviewed and concluded "Mr. Wynn had wrongfully accused Councilor Key of unethical and wrongful behavior." Wynn signed an apology letter, then testified at deposition that the letter he signed was not accurate.
  • At deposition Wynn admitted he never directed the police chief to do anything about the "time theft" he claimed was occurring, and never set up a meeting with the chief about it between August 2022 and his January 2023 termination.

Other details

  • Before Neptune Beach (~100 employees, eight direct reports, Duval County), Wynn's only prior manager job was Albion, Indiana — population 2,300, ~20 employees, where the budget was the clerk/treasurer's responsibility. He was 35 when Neptune Beach hired him.
  • The Council fired him by unanimous vote at a noticed public meeting, on the record, after reading two City Attorney opinion letters; the 43-minute meeting video is public (neptunebeachfl.portal.civicclerk.com/event/438/media).

Why this matters for Paonia

The motion is the most detailed public account of the City's side, and several specifics echo documented Paonia patterns — as always, allegations supported by the City's exhibits, not findings:

  • "First time I saw it was today": the City's evidence that Wynn disclaimed knowledge of a site plan he had received and acted on months earlier parallels the 2024-11-04 email deception ("Oh, we do have an email from Pete").
  • Controlling records flow: Whitmore's declaration that Wynn asked to pre-screen water-tank records gathered for a public records request parallels Wynn's role in Paonia CORA handling and his 2026-03-26 review of the Verkada audit log 12 days before it was produced.
  • Accusations against elected officials: the wrongful-felony accusation against Councilor Key parallels the "nefarious ends" characterization of Trustee Brunner.
