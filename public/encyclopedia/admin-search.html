How Paonia Should Hire Its Next Administrator

From Paoniapedia, the free encyclopedia of the Town of Paonia
How Paonia Should Hire Its Next Administrator

A citizen proposal. This page is published by paoniatruth.site. It is not a Town of Paonia document and does not speak for the Town.

Paonia is about to hire the most powerful unelected position in town government. How we run that search is itself a decision — and right now the Board is poised to make it the old way, by default. There is a better way, and this is what it looks like.

Where things stand

Town Administrator Stefen Wynn's three-year contract is not being renewed. A Town finance memo dated May 19, 2026 — subject line "Town Administrator terms of nonrenewal" — projects his compensation running through mid-July 2026. On May 22, 2026 the Board held a special meeting to consider an interim administrator and "the process for hiring a full-time administrator." source

The packet for that meeting tells you which way the wind is blowing: it attaches the City of Florence, Colorado's 2022 City Manager search RFP as a template. The plan, by default, is a conventional national executive search run by a hired consulting firm.

Two things about that default deserve daylight.

The cost is hidden. The finance memo proposes paying an executive search firm out of the "Legal, Engineering and Professional Services" budget line — but it names no figure. Colorado town-administrator searches commonly run $15,000–$30,000 or more. The Board should not vote to hire a consultant whose price is not in the packet. Disclose the cost first.

We have done this before. Wynn was hired in 2023 through exactly this process — a national search, four finalists flown in from out of state. The Delta County Independent's own headline that season was "Paonia readies to conduct interviews again." The national-search model is not a guarantee of anything. It is just the familiar option.

The old way, and what it optimizes for

A national executive search firm is paid to do three things: source candidates, screen them, and structure a process. It does those things competently. But look at what the model is built to optimize for:

  • The longest résumé, sourced from the widest possible geography.
  • A candidate profile shaped in closed sessions with the firm.
  • A hiring committee that — in the Florence template the Board attached — is "2 Council and 3 Staff." Zero citizen seats.

What it does not optimize for is the thing Paonia actually needs: someone already rooted here, who knows this valley, who shares this community's values on day one rather than learning them from a relocation package. A nationwide search for an administrator structurally cannot deliver that. It is searching for the wrong thing.

The moment we are in

This matters more in 2026 than it would have in 2016. We are in the middle of the largest change in how knowledge work is done since the industrial revolution — and the Town is about to spend public money as if nothing has changed.

Two consequences follow directly:

  1. Running a search is no longer specialized, gatekept labor. Sourcing candidates, screening applications, designing a fair and lawful hiring process — a firm used to be the only practical way to get that done. It is not anymore. A committee of residents, working with modern tools and in the open, can run a rigorous search themselves.

  2. A capable local hire no longer needs a decades-long municipal résumé. The knowledge-heavy parts of the administrator's job — financial systems, grant research, code and compliance lookup, drafting — are increasingly tool-augmented. What cannot be augmented is judgment, integrity, and being genuinely invested in Paonia. Hire for those, and the rest is trainable.

This proposal — published, transparent, costed, sitting in public — is itself the demonstration. It is the kind of work a town can now do for itself.

The new way

1. Grow and train a local

Take a capable Paonia resident, give them the job, and invest in training them the way the town would otherwise invest in a consultant's fee. The money is roughly comparable. The difference is where it lands at the end: a national search sends $15K–$30K out of town with a firm; a training investment of the same size stays here, in a neighbor who is still here in ten years.

This is the heart of it: a chance for the people of Paonia to take ownership of their own government, instead of handing that choice to outsiders.

2. Unbundle the role

The current job is Town Administrator and Treasurer — and the finance memo shows the salary split across four separate funds: 25% Administration, 35% Water, 35% Sewer, 5% Trash. That split is more than accounting. It is a sign that one title has been carrying several distinct jobs, and concentrating a great deal of authority — administrative and financial — in a single person. Residents have already raised this publicly.

The search is the moment to fix it. Rather than recruiting one hard-to-find super-administrator, the Board should consider:

  • A full-time Town Administrator focused on operations and management.
  • A separate finance/treasurer function — part-time, contracted, or shared — so that the person spending the money is not the same person reporting on it. See "Controls all the money, alone": what the evidence actually shows.
  • Utility-heavy duties scoped honestly against that 70% Water/Sewer load.

Unbundling widens the candidate pool (more locals can do one of these jobs well than can do all of them), improves financial oversight, and reduces the single-point-of-failure risk the Town just lived through.

3. A Citizen Search Advisory Committee

The Board hires the administrator — that is its statutory job, and this proposal does not change that. But the Board should not run the search alone, and it should not outsource it to a firm. It should appoint a Citizen Search Advisory Committee with a real, defined role.

  • Purpose: help define the candidate profile, review applications, screen candidates, run public candidate forums, and recommend finalists to the Board.
  • Seating: members selected through a transparent, published application process against stated criteria — not by self-nomination and not by invitation. The roster, and the reason each member was chosen, is public.
  • Composition: a mix of residents reflecting the community — including someone with municipal-finance literacy and someone from the utility ratepayer base. Trustees and staff may sit alongside citizens, but citizens hold real screening authority, not a ceremonial seat.
  • Conflict rules: anyone with a personal stake in the outcome — including the author of this proposal — applies through the same process as everyone else and recuses where appropriate. The committee's credibility depends on no one crowning themselves.

4. Run the whole thing in public

Publish the job description, the candidate profile, the selection criteria, the timeline, and the finalist forum schedule — online, as the process happens. Colorado open-meetings and candidate-confidentiality law set the boundaries; within them, default to daylight. A transparent search is one residents can trust the result of.

What we ask the Board

  1. Disclose the cost of any executive search firm — in dollars — before voting to hire one.
  2. Direct staff to prepare a side-by-side comparison: a national search firm versus growing and training a qualified local hire, including the cost of each.
  3. Appoint a Citizen Search Advisory Committee with real screening authority and a transparent, criteria-based seating process.
  4. Direct that the search consider unbundling the combined Administrator/Treasurer role rather than recruiting a single position by default.
  5. Keep the interim appointment short and clearly time-bound, so it does not quietly become the answer.
