This second one packages a meeting with Verkada (April 22), an attorney response, and individual department staff statements into nine question categories.

Cover staff report (page 572)

"During the 4/14/2026 Board meeting, The Town received a series of questions from Trustees and the public regarding the implementation, use, governance, and legal compliance of the Verkada camera system. … This agenda staff report is intended to provide a concise overview and direct the Board to the attached memorandum and supporting materials. … I believe the review also identifies an opportunity for the Board to consider whether a more formal, Town-wide policy framework governing use, oversight, and administration of the system would be appropriate moving forward."

Attachment A — Wynn Executive Summary Memo (pp. 573–577)

Wynn organizes responses into nine categories. Recurring framing: "In my professional opinion," "It is my understanding," "Based on the information provided," "I believe."

1. Data Access, Storage and Control

"The Town retains ownership and control of its data. Footage is encrypted in transit and at rest, and it is my understanding that neither Verkada nor third-party providers access that data without authorization. Video is retained for approximately 30 days unless archived, after which it is no longer accessible."

2. Facial Recognition and Advanced Technology

"Facial recognition and similar features are currently disabled and require intentional action by a user with administrative permissions to activate. It is my understanding from discussing with Verkada representatives that these features cannot be enabled remotely by the vendor and that any changes would be logged."

3. Legal Compliance and Privacy Protections

"I believe the current use of cameras is consistent with Colorado law, particularly as it relates to recording in areas where there is no reasonable expectation of privacy. It is my understanding that warrants are not required for standard public security surveillance systems and that audio recording may be permissible under Colorado's one-party consent framework. … typical public security surveillance systems of this type are not governed by the Electronic Communications Privacy Act."

4. Use, Oversight, and Governance

"The Police Department operates under a formal policy with defined access controls, while Public Works and administrative staff appear to use the system primarily in response to specific incidents or operational needs. That said, I believe the questions raised highlight an opportunity to develop a more comprehensive, Town-wide policy."

5. Necessity, Purpose and Scope

"I believe the system was implemented in response to specific operational challenges, including vandalism, theft, and unauthorized access to facilities. … alternatives such as increased staffing or reliance on community monitoring would likely be less effective and more resource-intensive."

6. Financial Justification

"Funding was derived from multiple sources, including enterprise funds and capital allocations, rather than solely from general tax revenues. No grants were received specifically for procurement of the camera system."

7. Audio Recording and Surveillance Scope

"Certain cameras have audio capability, though it must be intentionally enabled and is not universally active. Only cameras equipped with audio capability include a toggle function on the system dashboard. Video is continuously recording with motion flagging to assist in review. … To the extent that prior descriptions have simplified how these features operate, I believe this additional context helps clarify the technical distinction while remaining consistent with the system's intended uses."

This is a partial walk-back from the April 14 memo's flat denial of audio recording. See Factual Corrections to the Administrator's April 14, 2026 Memorandum.

8. Physical Placement and Privacy Boundaries

"Camera placement is targeted based on documented operational needs. … Cameras are not intended to capture areas where there is a reasonable expectation of privacy, and certain recreational areas have been excluded."

9. Process and Decision-Making

"The system was implemented after being discussed across multiple publicly noticed meetings and formal Board action, including unanimous approval. … Vendor selection appears to have followed established procurement processes, including state bid options."

Conclusion:

"It is my opinion that the responses to the questions presented indicate that the system is being used in a controlled and purpose-driven manner, with attention to legal compliance and operational need. At the same time, I believe that the concerns raised highlight the value of establishing a formal, Town-wide policy framework."

Attachment B — Camera Discussion (pp. 578–582)

Compiled Q&A document. Includes notes labeled "FROM MEETING WITH VERKADA AT 2PM ON 4.22.2026" and several open follow-ups ("Stephanie Larratt will follow up").

Key items not in the executive summary:

  • Cyber-attack disclosure: Verkada told staff that "97 of 6000 customers were affected by an attack — neutralized within 2 hours and customers were notified very quickly." (Attachment B, Data Access section)
  • Retroactive facial recognition: "Yes, but only organization administrators can activate for stored footage, but this is only if the video is within retention limits or archived. Before sharing footage a setting pops up that can blur faces before footage is shared."
  • ICE warrant scenario (verbatim): "If ICE were to come to Verkada with a federal warrant, their answer would be we don't own that data, the user owns that data and federal agencies would need to go to the user with that warrant."
  • Audio capability: "Yes, the two cameras in the building have audio capabilities. They have been used to see how staff interacted with difficult situations." Audio "has to be installed and activated when the camera is initially installed." Models without audio: "Max Swensen is sending that information to us." (Confirmed in Attachment G: D50, D80, CD22, CH52-E, CM22, CM41-S, and Bullet series have no audio; "All other cameras will have that setting.")
  • Excluded recreational areas: "Apple Valley Park, Tennis/Pickleball Courts, former football fields."
  • Park surveillance rationale: "Cameras that have been installed were to deter vandalism and were placed in areas with vandalism, criminal mischief, and illegal dumping. With wifi in the park, there is infrastructure that is also being monitored."
  • Process / public input (Wynn's hedging): "(verify) I believe this was discussed at six separate public meetings, or various pieces of installing and purchasing cameras were discussed in public meetings. No public comment was given at those meetings, but it was offered."
  • Original requestor: "Cameras were requested originally by a former employee and supported by the administrator and department heads — there was significant vandalism throughout Town and there was theft of equipment and tools from public works. No one knew who had a key to what buildings. No security systems were in place. People were in the water plant without staff approval or escort. People were accessing the wastewater treatment plant — trespassing and illegal dumping."
  • Funding sources (full list): "general fund, water enterprise, sewer enterprise, trash enterprise — of which sales tax capital improvement, surplus disposal of equipment, and back the badge were all utilized to purchase this equipment."
  • Action item: "Departments will need to get uses to administrator so that we can get a practical use policy in place — need by 4.23 at 4PM."

Attachment C — PD Camera System Staff Report (pp. 583–585)

Dated 04/20/2026. Paonia Police Department public-safety video surveillance program report.

  • "Only the Chief of Police and the Patrol Sergeant are authorized to access and operate the video surveillance system."
  • "All requests to view video must be directly related to and in furtherance of a criminal investigation."
  • Annual review by Chief of Police: cost/benefit, public-safety effectiveness, supported investigations or prosecutions, training/discipline/policy issues.
  • Prohibited: "Intentional invasion of individual privacy or monitoring areas where a reasonable expectation of privacy exists"; discriminatory targeting; harassment.
  • Authorized uses include "Monitoring areas with known gang or narcotics-related activity" and "Monitoring pedestrian and vehicular traffic activity."

Attachment D — Public Works Use of Verkada (pp. 586–587)

Dated April 22, 2026. Lists current PW uses of cameras by location:

  • Shop: parking/storage security (historical theft), bulk water fill security ("recent severe damages that resulted in court case and restitution"), winter snow monitoring.
  • Water Plant: interior camera to verify alarm legitimacy; external camera for security. "The water plant is on private property. Historical trespassing issues of citizens and unauthorized individuals."
  • Wastewater Plant: aerator malfunction timeline ("aerators malfunctioning and blowing apart"); deters trespassing and illegal dumping.
  • Park: post-incident review of property damage. "Multiple times the fence has been ran over or broken, the large gazebo structure is vandalized with obscene drawings and language and the grills being used as toilets. There is/was high criminal activity between the skate park and the PW park shed."
  • Front Town Hall: used once after report of an alleged drunk driver "almost hitting a PW employee."

"Public Works reviews footage only after suspicious behavior or incidents occur."

Access structure: "Public Works has user access only. Director has access to all cameras. Water operators have access to both plants. Public Works Supervisor has access but has never logged in or completed his profile."

Attachment E — Clerk's Office Use of Verkada (pp. 588–589)

Dated April 23, 2026. Author: Samira M Vetter, Town Clerk.

Reasons used:

  • Verifying weekend cleaning crews; verifying Town Hall after alarms; tracking Board Room rental start/end; "out of the norm customer/employee interactions or to verify customer/employee complaints"; "At least one instance of vandalism of town property has been caught on camera"; "Verifying payment arrangement details or minutes."

Reasons to continue:

  • "Town Hall staff handles money, including cash for permits and utility bills"; "Building/staff safety after hours"; documenting "accusations/verbal threats/threatening behavior by customers towards front office staff multiple times during my time working here"; bathrooms left open during Town events.

Vetter's stated access: "I have the cameras at Town Hall and at the Town Shop and Bulk Water Fill station."

Attachment F — Town Attorney Response on Legal Questions (pp. 590–592)

From: Clayton Buchner, Town Attorney (cmblawllc@gmail.com), responding to Wynn's April 22 questions. Dated April 23, 2026 12:28 PM.

  • Live feeds / warrants: "Cameras may be but are not required to be live feed. … Warrants are not required for access/use of public surveillance whether active or passive. Note: SB26-071, if passed, would require a warrant to access facial recognition systems data."
  • Audio under ECPA: "The ECPA … does not apply to the Town's public surveillance cameras."
  • Colorado one-party consent: "It is legal to audio record in non-public areas as long as one party involved in a conversation is aware the conversation is being recorded and consents to the recording."
  • End user agreement: "The Town, as the end user, is bound by the terms and conditions stated in the contract as it is in any other third-party contract."

Attachment G — Verkada Vendor Resources (pp. 593–594)

Email from Max Swensen (Verkada Account Executive, max.swensen@verkada.com) to Police Chief Matt Laiminger, dated April 23 2026 6:05 PM, forwarded by Laiminger to Wynn.

  • "For State Contracts, we can go through Carasoft, NASPO, and Omnia."
  • Cameras without audio: "The D50, D80, CD22, CH52-E, CM22, CM41-S, and Bullet series cameras do not have audio. All other cameras will have that setting, which can be turned on and off in Command."
  • Resources offered: Verkada Trust Hub, FRT-responsibility blog, People Analytics User Guide, License Plate Recognition User Guide, AI-Powered Search User Guide and Moderation Policy/FAQ, Privacy and Security Checklist, Public Security Disclosures (auto-generated QR/PDF).

Notable

  • Staff did not put forward a motion to turn any cameras off, despite Item H.8 being titled "Consideration of Whether to Turn Some Cameras Off while an Adequate Framework of Governance is Put Into Place." The recommendation is "provide policy direction."
  • Wynn now confirms two cameras inside Town Hall have active audio capability and that audio "has been used to see how staff interacted with difficult situations" — one of the disputed claims from 10 Things You Should Know About Paonia's New Town Cameras / Lucy Hunter's Rebuttal Flyer - "10 Things" Response.
  • The attorney response flags pending Colorado bill SB26-071 as relevant to facial-recognition warrant requirements.
  • The Camera Discussion document still has multiple "follow up" items from the Verkada meeting (data encryption details, sale-of-company scenario) flagged as not yet answered.
