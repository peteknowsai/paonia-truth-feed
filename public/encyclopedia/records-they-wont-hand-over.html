The Records They Won't Hand Over

None of the rest of this would be knowable if the records flowed freely. They do not. Colorado's Open Records Act gives every resident the right to public documents — and the Town's record under that law is a string of denials, delays, and a judge's finding of bad faith.

A judge already found bad faith

In the Brunner litigation, a Delta County district judge found the Town had "failed to exercise reasonable diligence or reasonable inquiry" in responding to records requests and ordered it to pay Brunner's legal costs. Under Colorado's open-records law, that is a judicial finding of bad faith — not a resident's accusation, but a court's conclusion.

The audit logs that aged out

In 2024, the Clerk denied a request for the Town's Microsoft 365 audit logs as "not in the Town's custody." The Colorado Freedom of Information Coalition later confirmed the denial was improper. By then it no longer mattered in practice: the records were never produced and have since aged out of their retention window. A wrong denial, run out the clock, becomes a permanent one.

The four-part method

By 2026, three separate records responses showed a repeating method:

  • Redact the column that matters — release the document, black out the one field that would answer the question.
  • Withhold records as nonexistent — declare that responsive records don't exist rather than produce them.
  • Hand over a mislabeled file — deliver something that technically responds but isn't what was asked for.
  • Cap the export at the row limit — return a partial dataset truncated at the system's default maximum, with no flag that more exists.

Each move is individually deniable. Together they describe a posture: comply on paper, disclose as little as possible in fact.

Why this is the keystone

Records obstruction is not one more grievance alongside the others — it is what makes the others hard to prove. The retaliation, the cameras, the spending: each is documented only because some records got out. The method above is how the rest stay in. A judge has already named the cost of letting it continue.
