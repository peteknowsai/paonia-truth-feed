Summary

Point-by-point legal rebuttal of six grounds cited by the Town Clerk for rejecting the surveillance camera initiative petition. The response argues each ground misapplies Colorado initiative law and notes a pattern of two consecutive initiative rejections, both characterized as "administrative" in nature.

Key Facts

  • Rebuts 6 separate grounds for rejection:

1. Single-Subject Rule

  • Initiative has one subject: prohibiting surveillance cameras on public property
  • Structural provisions (enforcement, definitions, severability) are not separate subjects

2. Body-Worn Cameras

  • Section 4(a) of the initiative explicitly EXEMPTS body-worn cameras from the prohibition
  • "An exemption is the opposite of regulation"
  • The initiative does not regulate body-worn cameras; it carves them out

3. "Administrative Function"

  • Surveillance policy is legislative, not administrative
  • Cites ordinances from San Francisco, Portland, Oakland, and Cambridge as precedent
  • Cites Colorado SB 22-113 and HB 24-1130 as legislative treatment of surveillance

4. Contracts

  • Voiding contracts that violate the ordinance is standard enforcement mechanism, not an administrative function

5. Injunctive Relief

  • Creating a cause of action is a legislative act; courts adjudicate
  • Cites Oakland and Berkeley surveillance ordinances with similar provisions

6. Voter Approval Requirement

  • Even if this provision were invalid, the severability clause should save the remainder of the initiative

Pattern Noted

  • Two consecutive initiatives rejected by the Town
  • Both rejections characterized the subject matter as "administrative"
  • The STR initiative was the first rejection

Legal Remedy Threatened

  • Demands acceptance of the petition within 5 business days
  • Will file a Rule 106 action in Delta County District Court if not accepted
