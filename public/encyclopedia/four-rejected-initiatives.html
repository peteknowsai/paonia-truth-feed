Four Ballot Measures, Four Rejections

From Paoniapedia, the free encyclopedia of the Town of Paonia
Four Ballot Measures, Four Rejections

TypeAnalysis
First recorded2026-06-04

When residents tried to go around Town Hall and legislate directly, the door closed there too. Across two subjects — short-term rentals and surveillance — the Clerk rejected four citizen ballot initiatives. Every rejection leaned on the same objection.

The same word, four times

Each rejection recycled the claim that the measure was "administrative, not legislative" — a distinction that decides whether residents are even allowed to put a question on the ballot. Colorado reserves the initiative power for legislative matters; label a citizen proposal "administrative" and it never reaches the voters. The objection did the work that a substantive review would otherwise require, and it did it four times in a row.

The copy-paste tell

The fourth rejection carried a copy-paste error that named the wrong initiative. A rejection that misidentifies the measure it is rejecting is hard to read as a careful, case-by-case legal judgment. It reads as a template — the same answer reached before the specific proposal was even examined.

Why the standard itself is the problem

The deeper issue is not any one rejection but the elasticity of the test. When a clerk can keep redefining what counts as "legislative," the line moves to wherever it needs to be to keep a measure off the ballot. The citizen's right to petition becomes conditional on a judgment call the same office makes every time. A petition asking a Delta County court to review the rejections has been drafted; the legal question it raises is exactly this one.

The pattern it fits

This is the legislative-door version of the same dynamic the record shows elsewhere: residents' sourced facts dismissed without being disproved, and the channels meant to give the public a voice narrowing each time the public tries to use them.
