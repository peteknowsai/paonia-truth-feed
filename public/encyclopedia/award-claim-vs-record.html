What an Award Can and Cannot Tell Us About a Town's Books

From Paoniapedia, the free encyclopedia of the Town of Paonia

The Mayor invoked a financial award to address concerns about Town oversight at the April 28 meeting. The award itself, and what it actually measures, is worth understanding.

By Pete McCarthy — May 9, 2026

At the regular Board of Trustees meeting on April 28, 2026, after a citizen asked the Trustees to exercise greater oversight of Town finances, Mayor Paige Smith took the floor to respond. Speaking from her position on the finance committee, she described the internal controls she sees in operation, the invoices and checks she reviews, and concluded with a credential she clearly believed spoke to the question:

"There is nothing untoward going on. I'm sorry that you're getting the impression that there is, but there is not. Honestly, there is not. ... we have gotten award for our last two budgets from the government finance association."

The "government finance association" is the Government Finance Officers Association, or GFOA. It is the principal trade association for the people who manage public-sector finances in the United States and Canada. Its Distinguished Budget Presentation Award is one of the most widely cited credentials in municipal government.

This article is about three things. What the Mayor said, and how it compares to GFOA's published records. What the GFOA award actually is, in the organization's own terms. And what the appropriate use of such a credential might look like in a public meeting where citizens are asking serious questions.

The award count

GFOA publishes a complete public list of every government that has won the Distinguished Budget Presentation Award, by fiscal year, on its website. The lists are downloadable in spreadsheet form. Searching them for the Town of Paonia returns the following:

Fiscal year Total winners Colorado winners Paonia present?
FYB 2024 1,746 74 No
FYB 2025 1,561 (through 3/3/26) 82 Yes (1 cumulative win)
FYB 2026 30 (cycle in progress through 3/3/26) 5 No

The Town's own entry on GFOA's roster reads "Total Awards: 1." The award covers the FY 2025 budget. There is no record on GFOA's published lists of Paonia having won the award for any prior year, and Paonia is not on the partial FY 2026 list as of GFOA's most recent quarterly update.

The Mayor's reference to "our last two budgets" does not match the public record. Whether her statement reflects a misremembering, an internal pending status, or a conflation with another credential, I do not know. What I do know is that GFOA's official records, as of the date of the meeting and as of this writing, document one award.

What the GFOA award actually measures

This is the part of the story that requires a careful reading, because the award is genuine and the achievement is real, but the achievement is narrower than it sounds.

What this credential costs and what it tests

Cost to apply: Between $380 and $800 per application for a town Paonia's size, with member governments receiving roughly a 50% discount over non-members. The Town also pays annual GFOA membership dues separately.

Who reviews it: Paid GFOA staff and volunteer reviewers drawn from "government, public accounting, and academe" — that is, fellow public-finance professionals.

What it tests: Whether the budget document meets GFOA's checklist for organization, disclosure, and presentation. GFOA's own program literature states the goal is "not to assess the financial health of participating governments, but rather to ensure that users of their financial statements have the information they need to do so themselves."

How many governments win: Approximately 1,800 to 1,900 per year nationally, including roughly 80 in Colorado.

GFOA's plain statement that the program is "not to assess the financial health of participating governments" is significant. It is the awarding organization saying directly that the credential is not a verdict on whether the underlying finances are sound. It is a verdict on whether the budget document — the formatting, the section structure, the disclosures, the explanatory narrative — meets a set of professional presentation standards.

A useful analogy: the award is to a budget document what a clean copy edit is to a manuscript. It tells you the writing is properly organized, footnoted, and free of obvious errors of form. It does not tell you whether the writing is true.

The trade-association character of the credential

The Government Finance Officers Association is, as an organizational matter, a 119-year-old trade association of about 30,000 government finance professionals in the United States and Canada. Its function is roughly analogous to the American Bar Association for lawyers or the American Medical Association for physicians: a professional society that promotes standards of practice and offers peer recognition.

When a town applies for the Distinguished Budget Presentation Award, it is paying a trade association — of which the Town's own finance staff are likely members — to have a budget document reviewed against the association's checklist by other members of the same profession. The reviewers are not external auditors. They are not regulators. They are colleagues operating within the same professional norms that produced the document being reviewed.

This is not, in itself, an indictment of the program. Trade associations exist for good reasons, and peer-reviewed credentials can carry real signaling value. The Distinguished Budget Presentation Award demonstrates that a town has gone through the discipline of producing a budget document that meets a set of nationally accepted presentation standards. That is a real and useful thing.

But it is also, structurally, a paid credential issued by an industry body to its own members. The annual winners list runs to roughly 1,800 to 1,900 governments. In Colorado, it includes counties, cities, water districts, school districts, and small towns of every size. Receipt of the award places a government in a wide club, not a narrow one.

Sheila Weinberg, the founder and CEO of the nonprofit Truth in Accounting, has assessed the GFOA award programs in stronger terms. Truth in Accounting publishes annual analyses of municipal and state financial reporting and has criticized the GFOA's award programs for, among other things, conferring credentials on governments whose financial statements are issued long past GFOA's own stated deadlines. Weinberg's organization has called the program "the greatest participation trophy in government accounting."

That language is sharper than I would use. But the underlying observation it points to — that the award has a high pass rate, is paid for by the recipient, and is administered by the industry that the recipient belongs to — is structurally accurate.

How the award was used at the meeting

The context in which Mayor Smith invoked the credential matters more than the credential itself.

A citizen had spoken in public comment about her concerns regarding the concentration of authority in the Town Administrator role and about the need for the Board to exercise oversight rather than delegate that responsibility to hired staff. The Mayor's response was substantive in part — she described her own access to invoices and bank accounts, her review of checks, the controls that have been put in place — and concluded with the GFOA award as a closing point.

The award was offered, in effect, as evidence that the citizen's concerns were misplaced. As the Mayor put it: "There is nothing untoward going on... we have gotten award for our last two budgets from the government finance association."

This is where I would respectfully suggest the credential was asked to do work it cannot do.

A budget presentation award does not address whether the Town Administrator's combined role as Treasurer is appropriate for a town with our specific history. It does not address whether the internal controls in place would have prevented the 2012 embezzlement of approximately $480,000 from this very Town. It does not certify the integrity of the underlying numbers, only the clarity of how those numbers are disclosed in the published document. The GFOA itself says so, in plain language.

There is a specific reason this matters in Paonia, and the reason is on paper.

On April 14, 2026, the Town produced the Caselle Application Rights Report in response to a separate Colorado Open Records Act request, C 26-10. Caselle is the financial management software the Town uses to record, process, and approve all of its transactions. The report identifies, function by function, which users in the system are permitted to perform each task and which are denied.

The report shows that Mr. Wynn — listed under the user ID "TManager," for Treasurer Manager — is an "Allowed User" on virtually every financial function inspected. That includes Accounts Payable, Accounts Receivable, General Ledger, Human Resources, Timekeeping, and Cemetery Management. It also includes the two specific functions that, under standard governmental accounting controls, are required to be separated between two different people:

  • Enter Invoices, the function by which an invoice is recorded into the accounting system in the first place; and
  • Approve Invoices for Payment, the function by which a recorded invoice is authorized to be paid.

These two functions are normally separated for a reason that has nothing to do with formatting and everything to do with the integrity of the underlying records. The Government Finance Officers Association's own guidance on revenue control identifies the activities that should be separated: "initiation and authorization of transactions, execution of transactions (receipting and disbursement), recording transactions, reconcilement, and maintaining custody." Standard accounts-payable practice across the public sector is that the person who enters an invoice is not the same person who approves it for payment, and neither of those people is the same person who executes the disbursement.

When two different people perform these functions, the system's audit log captures one user originating a transaction and a different user approving it; the existence of two distinct user identifiers serves as evidence that one action was an independent check on the other. When the same user performs both functions, that evidence does not exist in the records at all. The two events become structurally indistinguishable as a control matter. There is no chain-of-evidence trail to demonstrate that the approval served as a check on the initiation, because there was nothing in the system that required them to be different people.

There is a well-recognized exception to this principle for small governments, and the language of the exception is itself instructive. The New York State Office of the State Comptroller, which publishes some of the most widely-cited guidance on local government internal controls in the country, addresses the situation directly:

"In a small village or fire district where the treasurer is responsible for all financial operations, it may not be cost-effective to hire another employee for the sole purpose of segregating the treasurer's duties. Instead, a more cost-effective control is to have a board member, for example, periodically review the work of the treasurer, especially monthly bank reconciliations, to compensate for the lack of segregation of duties. Small and medium-sized local governments and school districts may need to utilize this type of approach to ensure proper controls are in place..."

The point is not that small towns must separate duties through staffing. The point is that when they cannot — because they have one Treasurer who does the work — the required compensating control is board oversight. Periodic, substantive review by elected officials of the Treasurer's work, including bank reconciliations, in operational detail.

This is exactly what the citizen at the April 28 meeting was asking for. It is the concern that the dual role of Treasurer and Town Administrator concentrates too much authority in one unelected position when the position is also configured in the accounting system to perform every step of every transaction. It is the concern that arises directly from the 2012 embezzlement, in which the previous Town Clerk — also operating with comprehensive system access in a small-town environment — removed approximately $480,000 from public funds.

Whether the compensating control is actually in place

The Town's stated answer to this concern, on the public record, is that it has a Finance Committee composed of the Mayor and one Trustee that "reviews every invoice." That is the named compensating control.

Two questions follow.

The first is whether the review is documented and effective. As of this writing, no Finance Committee meeting minutes, agendas, invoice review logs, or records of disbursements flagged or questioned by the Committee appear on any of the Town's public-facing channels. The Town's CivicClerk public meeting portal — the official site where Town meetings are noticed and minutes are posted — contains zero Finance Committee meetings among the thirty events posted going back to January 6, 2025. The publicly noticed bodies during that period have been the Town Board, the Planning Commission, the Tree Board, and a one-time Board of Trustees Work Session. The Town's official "Town Staff, Board of Trustees, and Committee Members" webpage lists exactly one committee — the Short-Term Rental Ad Hoc Committee — and does not list a Finance Committee at all. A Colorado Open Records Act request targeting the Committee's internal records — minutes, agendas, sign-in sheets, and any log or tracking record the Committee maintains — was filed on May 9, 2026. As of this writing, the request is pending. The factual question — whether the Finance Committee has, in operation, ever served as a check on a single transaction — does not have a public-record answer at the moment. It has only an asserted one.

The second question is whether the Mayor herself, in her own words at the April 28 meeting, was describing the kind of review the compensating-control standard contemplates.

The compensating control the New York State Comptroller's office describes is not a courtesy review. It is an adversarial check — a board member examining the Treasurer's work in operational detail, including monthly bank reconciliations, with the willingness to question what is found. That is what makes the review function as a control. The function depends on the reviewer being willing to find a problem.

At the April 28 meeting, after describing her finance committee role, the Mayor said this:

"I'm on the finance committee and I can tell you I know what's coming and go and coming in and coming out and I have complete faith in our administrator."

And, a few sentences later:

"I can tell you from my personal experience for the last two years as mayor, I have no question about the integrity and the honesty of administrative [Wynn]."

These are statements made in good faith. They are also statements that, by their own terms, foreclose the possibility of adversarial review. A reviewer who has "complete faith" and "no question about the integrity and the honesty" of the person being reviewed is not, by the definition of the compensating control, performing the function the compensating control is supposed to perform. The function requires skepticism. The function depends on the reviewer being willing to ask whether the answer in the records matches the answer the Treasurer has already given. The Mayor's stated posture, on the record, is the opposite of that.

This is not a comment on the Mayor's character or sincerity. It is a comment on the structure she has described, in her own words, as the only check standing between Paonia's combined-role Treasurer and the absence of the staff-level separation of duties that the standard accounting controls would otherwise require.

The GFOA award is silent on this question. Not because the award is unimportant, but because the award is a credential of presentation, not of control. The award certifies that the Town's budget document is well organized and properly disclosed. It does not, and does not claim to, certify that the underlying system the budget describes is configured in a way that would prevent any one individual from initiating, approving, recording, and disclosing a transaction without an independent check anywhere in the chain.

When a citizen at a public meeting raises a concern about whether a Board is providing sufficient oversight, the appropriate response — the response we ought to be able to expect from elected officials — is to engage the substance. To explain what the controls are, in operational terms. To point to the specific records that demonstrate them. To welcome the question rather than to retire it with a credential that does not, in fact, address what was asked.

The narrower point, and the broader one

The narrower point is factual. The Mayor said the Town has won the GFOA award for "our last two budgets." GFOA's own records show one award, for the FY 2025 budget. The Town is not on the FY 2024 winners list and was not on the most recent published FY 2026 list at the time the Mayor spoke.

The broader point is one of category. Even if the count had been correct, the credential the Mayor invoked is a paid, peer-reviewed presentation award issued by a professional trade association. It is a credential of form, not of substance, and the awarding body is unusually clear on this distinction. Its use as a deflection of citizen oversight concerns — well-meant or not — is a small but consequential category error. It substitutes a stamp from the industry for a substantive answer from the elected body.

Citizens raising oversight concerns at public meetings deserve the substantive answer.
