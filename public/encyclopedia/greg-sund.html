Career at a glance

County Administrator Ellis County, KS 2010–2015 Abrupt end-of-meeting resignation ("exploring my options"); stepped down to a dept-head job. No stated cause.
Public Works Director Hays, KS 2015–2017 Left for Trinidad (was job-hunting; finalist elsewhere)
City Manager Trinidad, CO 2017–2019 Resigned Sept 5, 2019 to avoid termination. Last permanent post.
Interim City Administrator Walsenburg, CO 2020 Resigned mid-meeting Dec 2, 2020 after council declined to extend him
Interim Town Manager Telluride, CO Jan–Apr 2022 Finalist for permanent job, not selected; interim ended

Also a 2024 finalist for the permanent Silt, CO town manager job (not selected).

Two documented patterns

Pattern 1 — Perpetual short-term interim since 2019

Since leaving Trinidad in 2019, Sund has held only interim/short-term posts, none converting to a permanent appointment. He reached finalist for permanent jobs at Telluride (2022) and Silt (2024) but was not chosen. Read charitably, this is a turnaround-specialist niche; read plainly, Paonia would be hiring a short-timer, not a long-term manager.

Pattern 2 — Twice pushed out by his elected board in 13 months

  • Trinidad (Sept 5, 2019): Resigned to avoid termination after council members worked to remove him. Valentine Letter to Trinidad Council re: City Manager Greg Sund (2019)
  • Walsenburg (Dec 2, 2020): A motion to advertise his position tied 3–3; a motion to extend his contract failed 4–2; he resigned the same day, calling the meeting disrespectful — "lacking in all honor."

Two separate boards, two conflict-driven exits inside 13 months. This is distinct from the interim-churn pattern: it is a recurring breakdown in the manager–board relationship.

The Trinidad departure (the substance)

Trinidad's council never stated a public reason; Mayor Phil Rico expressed disappointment about the rumors circulating before the decision. The only detailed account of cause is the five-page complaint letter from department head Michael Valentine, delivered to council before the resignation. It alleges autocratic management, demeaning treatment of the finance director ("this is not a democracy"), personnel handling that risked discrimination lawsuits, repeatedly blindsiding council, and botched development/finance decisions (La Puerta agreement, a zoning rewrite, a landfill enterprise-fund ballot, the Sister Blandina Park plan, a "Noah's Ark" building). Full breakdown and the crucial caveat: Valentine Letter to Trinidad Council re: City Manager Greg Sund (2019).

The caveat that matters: Valentine became Trinidad's interim and then permanent city manager after Sund left. The most damning document was written by his successor. It is a documented allegation from an interested party, not an adjudicated finding.

What we checked and did NOT find

To avoid overstating the case, the following were investigated and came up clean or contrary:

  • No lawsuits of record name Sund or his employers for the discrimination the Valentine letter warned of — at any stop (CourtListener/federal-docket searches negative). The threat never ripened into litigation.
  • Walsenburg cuts the other way: he inherited books that were all "out of balance" (errors tracing to 2017, before him), worked the problem alongside the finance director, and quit in protest when the mayor and a split council turned on him. He reads as the reformer there, not the villain.
  • Mt. Crested Butte: unqualified public praise from the mayor; the December 2022 staff departures were not pinned on him.
  • Telluride: lost the permanent job to Vail's sitting town manager; no friction reported.
  • Georgetown: the mass staff exodus there happened in fall 2024, before he arrived, and was blamed on the prior administrator; he was the cleanup interim.
  • Kansas (2010–17): thin-to-clean record. The one oddity is the manner of the 2015 Ellis County exit. The commission minutes (retrieved) record only a one-line resignation letter read aloud with no discussion, followed by repeated closed "non-elected personnel" sessions, the reassignment of his duties before his last day, and the Kansas Association of Counties brought in on the separation — circumstantially consistent with a managed exit, but no cause is stated anywhere in the open record.

The honest read: the Trinidad "bad manager" account does not clearly replicate elsewhere. The well-supported through-lines are the two patterns above, not a claim that he wrecks every town he touches.

Relevance to Paonia

The Town is weighing Sund as interim town manager while the current administrator and the board are themselves the subject of governance and transparency concerns this project documents. Two things are worth the board's attention before the May 29 vote:

  1. The Valentine letter's central charge — a manager who withholds information from the elected board and steamrolls staff — is precisely the failure mode at issue in Paonia. Whether it is fair to Sund is contestable; that the board should ask about it is not.
  2. Paonia is itself a town in turmoil — the kind of post Sund has repeatedly taken on an interim basis. The realistic expectation is a short, stabilizing tenure, not a permanent manager.

Update — Paonia interview, May 29, 2026

At his Paonia interview, Sund confirmed on the record the "this is not a democracy" remark, attributing it to direction from the Trinidad board that "the manager has to take the responsibility and make the decisions, and not make it a democracy of the department heads." He said he never learned why his Trinidad tenure ended: "they never really said anything to me."

He also described the manager revolving door in his own words — that towns find it "easiest to let the manager go" amid board turnover, that he "worked for 14 different people on a five-member board" in Dickinson, ND, and that he chose Trinidad believing it had moved past its "fairly constant replacement of managers… [but] those issues still had remained."

His name reached Paonia through the CCMA senior advisor, who reported "no other interim candidates." The motion to hire him died for lack of a second; the Board set a June 3 follow-up for reference checks. Full record: Special Meeting — Interim Administrator Interview (2026-05-29).

Open questions

  • Trinidad's September 5, 2019 special meeting accepted the resignation as voluntary and named Valentine interim — the public record states no cause, no severance figure, and no vote count. Trinidad uses Revize; the 2019 minutes are not URL-addressable and would require a request to City Clerk Audra Garrett (719-846-9843 x135) to confirm whether anything further was placed on the record.
  • Why he left Ellis County in 2015 — resolved as far as the open record allows: no cause was ever stated publicly; everything was handled in closed personnel session. The only remaining avenue is a Kansas Open Records Act request to Ellis County for any separation agreement (much of which they may lawfully withhold).
  • Georgetown interim end: resolved — confirmed clean from Board of Selectmen minutes (orderly handoff to Dennis Durham; public thank-you; no friction).
