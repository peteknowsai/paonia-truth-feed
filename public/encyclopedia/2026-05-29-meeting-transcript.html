Outcome (the headline)

The motion to enter a consultant agreement with Sund died for lack of a second. The Board instead set a follow-up special meeting for the following Wednesday (June 3, 2026) at 5:30 p.m. to complete reference checks and finalize. Sund had offered to start June 15, overlapping with departing administrator Stefen Wynn "to learn from him." No hire was made on May 29.

How the candidate was sourced — the CCMA pipeline, on the record

Asked by a trustee to explain the search process and why only one name was before the Board, staff described the mechanism plainly. See The CCMA Pipeline: How the Same Managers Circulate Through Colorado Towns.

  • ~[1:00:00] "We met with the CCMA senior advisor, like the board directed us to… and he gave us Greg Sund's number. So we called and we talked with Greg."
  • ~[1:00:19] "There are no other interim candidates."
  • ~[1:00:30] "There are six people on this advisory board, and they are all constantly talking about who's coming and going." / "It's called the senior advisor to the City County Managers Association."
  • ~[1:00:45] "There are six that I know of town-manager openings in the state of Colorado… this is not a kind of position that a lot of people are just sitting around doing."
  • ~[1:01:03] "It's not unusual to only have a single candidate for this kind of a job."
  • The mayor said this mirrored how the Town found its previous interim — "this is a lot like what our last one looked like" — again by contacting CCMA. (The audio is garbled here; per Town records, Paonia's 2023 interim was Leslie Clement. The senior advisor's name is also unclear in the audio.)

Sund's account of his Trinidad departure — and the "this is not a democracy" line

After Pete McCarthy read his statement quoting the Valentine complaint, the mayor invited Sund to respond. He did not deny the quote; he attributed it to board direction, and said it was directed at department-head accountability — not at residents. ~[48:30]

  • "I don't know exactly what led to the end of my tenure with Trinidad. They never really said anything to me."
  • "The statement I made to the finance director was based completely on a direction from the board… they said I needed [to] take responsibility for all of the decisions that came before the board… I shouldn't be saying that the department heads agreed with or were counseled on things."
  • "They clearly told me… the manager has to take the responsibility and make the decisions, and not make it a democracy of the department heads. And I told them I could get a little tougher on the department heads, but that isn't the approach I really prefer to take."
  • On Fisher's Peak: "there were some people who clearly said, what kind of idiot would want to buy a mountain."

For analysis of why this remark is troubling regardless of its audience, see Interim TM Candidate Greg Sund — What the Record Shows.

Sund's account of the Walsenburg departure

Asked "how about Walsenburg," Sund framed that exit as a procedural conflict with the mayor over budget timing. ~[49:30]

  • The mayor's requirement "changed, differing from state law in regard to the budget" — demanding "a complete document delivered to him on that day, not a preliminary budget," two months after Sund had submitted the preliminary budget on the state's schedule.
  • "I can produce a final document, but… you can't produce 15 or 20 final documents. They take a lot of time."

Sund described the revolving door himself

Asked about towns parting ways with managers, the candidate confirmed the pattern this project has documented. ~[55:48]

  • "There's times when it's easiest to let the administrator — the manager — go, rather than addressing some of the issues… it's easiest to just part ways because there's a difference of opinion of what the direction of the community is."
  • "That happens probably more often than not when you get newly elected people who want to make some changes, and the administrator is seen — whether it's true or not — [as] being tied to the old board."
  • On Dickinson, ND: "the town recalled the remaining four members of the board… over a period of four years, I worked for 14 different people on a five-member board."
  • ~[57:42] On Trinidad: "When I applied to Trinidad, I thought it got over a lot of those issues because there had been a fairly constant replacement of managers throughout its history. And I found that a lot of those issues still had remained."

Sund on the administrator's role — disavowing the autocrat posture

Asked by a resident about polarization, Sund described a board-centered view of the job — the opposite of the posture the Valentine letter alleged. ~[52:00]

  • "The administrator does not have power that a lot of people think it does. It is a position that reports directly to the board… the board is the final decision maker and the center of policy for the entire community."
  • Earlier he drew the line as: "the board deals with what I would call ownership issues, and the administrator deals with the customer side of the city." ~[44:30]

A trustee and a resident both cited this approvingly as the answer to the Town's prior-administrator friction.

Why he only takes interims now

~[18:06] "I decided after Trinidad that I just wasn't in the mood to move out anymore. I just wanted to stay in one location. And the best way to do that for me is to serve in interim positions that have a start and a stop date, and then I can return home. And this is a field where if you actively pursue positions, you move all the time… I just didn't feel like doing it anymore."

The contract — a consultant agreement, not employment

Sund and staff read the key terms into the record. ~[1:13:30]–[1:16:00]

  • "This is different than [an] employment situation, because I'm a contractor for the town… This is not hiring an employee, and it does clearly say that."
  • "It's month to month for up to six months; extensions up to three months in duration may be granted by mutual consent of both parties. May be terminated by either party with or without cause upon 30 days' written notice."
  • The mayor praised Sund for agreeing to pay half his own housing and utilities — "and oftentimes the town pays the full cost" ([24:00]); Trustee Foster pushed for a dollar cap on the Town's half ([1:11:30]).

The process objection — from a trustee — and the reference dispute

Trustee Foster raised the same concerns this project had flagged, and the hire stalled on them. ~[59:30] and ~[1:09:00]

  • "For me, it was really surprising just to get one candidate."
  • "I have never hired someone within a 24-hour time period, and [I'm] deeply [un]comfortable… because I asked for references to be checked. It's actually nothing against him."
  • "A formal process of reference check is a normal way to go, even when there is, like, no other option, because it's part of doing our diligence."
  • ~[1:09:50] "Does this motion die for lack of a second? Yes."
  • "This is not a judgment on the person in front of us… [it's about] documented process… full and complete due diligence."
  • ~[1:10:41] "Cutting corners only sets us up for criticism in the future."

Countervailing, the mayor disclosed she had already informally checked one reference — "I actually know one of the clerks [from] one of his former [towns]… so I did call her… and she gave me a glow[ing] recommendation" ([1:01:30]) — and the clerk estimated formal checks could be back within days ([1:18:30]). The contract packet, a trustee noted, had only been posted the day before (~[1:04:47]).

"We are not a democracy" — from the floor

Minutes after McCarthy quoted Sund's Trinidad "this is not a democracy" line, former mayor Mary Bachran rose during public comment and used nearly the same words — approvingly. ~[51:31]

  • "First off, I would like to remind the board that this is not a democracy. We are a republic — which means that we, the people, vote for people who will make decisions for us. So we are not a democracy."
  • She thanked Sund "for bringing up the issue of continuity," then asked how he handles "extreme polarization," characterizing the Town's critics as "a small cadre of people who believe that they are the only ones who can correctly run the town."

The juxtaposition is the record's own commentary: the "not a democracy" framing the Valentine complaint attributed to Sund was, at the same meeting, voiced from the floor by a former mayor — alongside the move of recasting residents who push back as the problem.
