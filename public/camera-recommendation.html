Recommendation — Prepared for the Board of Trustees

The Simplest Camera That Can't Betray Us:
Records to a Card, We Pull It, We Play It

Prepared 2026-05-14  ·  Cheapest option  ·  No internet  ·  No app  ·  Town holds the footage

The bottom line

Buy a non-cellular trail camera — roughly $60–$130 each. It mounts on a wall, runs on batteries, and records to a memory card inside the camera. There is no app, no WiFi, no account, and no internet connection — the camera has no networking parts at all. To review video, a staff member pulls the card and plays it on any computer. It is the cheapest option, the easiest to maintain, and the only one where "closed loop" is a physical fact rather than a setting we have to trust. Start here. We can always add more later; we can't easily un-trust a system that's already on the network.

Why this, and not the "nicer" systems

The fancier camera systems all let you watch the footage on your phone. That sounds like a feature — but think about what it means: if the video reaches your phone, the camera reached the internet to send it there. Once a device can do that, "it stays local" is a promise the manufacturer is making to us, not a fact we can verify. That is the exact situation that got the Verkada cameras taken down.

A trail camera can't make that promise because it can't break it. It has no WiFi chip, no cellular radio, no network port. The footage physically cannot leave the memory card until a person walks up and removes it. That's not a security setting — it's the absence of the ability. That is what "the Town has total custody" actually looks like.

This is a record, not surveillance — and it's how towns actually do this

A monitoring system needs someone watching screens. Paonia doesn't have that, doesn't need it, and the community has made clear it doesn't want it. What we're building is something different: a record. The camera sits there. Nobody watches it. It's a quiet witness. If something happens at the site — tampering, vandalism, dumping, an accident, a dispute about what occurred — someone walks over, pulls the card, and finds out what happened. The rest of the time it does nothing and bothers no one.

That distinction matters, and the research backs it up: studies of camera programs find they help resolve incidents two ways — real-time monitoring and identifying what happened "after the fact through recorded video." The second one delivers the value without the monitoring room, the staff, or the surveillance footprint. For a small town keeping an eye on a piece of infrastructure, after-the-fact review is the whole job.

And this isn't a fringe idea — it's the mainstream municipal playbook for exactly this kind of problem:

  • Seattle ran an official Illegal Dumping Camera Pilot: motion-activated cameras that simply capture footage for later review — no live monitoring. It worked, and the city expanded it.
  • Milwaukee deployed hidden trail cameras — the exact category recommended here — at problem sites. Cheap, unmonitored, and they produced real results ($15,000 and $10,000 fines from two cases). The program is expanding from 10 cameras to over 100.
  • Springfield (MA), St. Louis, and Camden (NJ) all run the same model — modest cameras, footage reviewed after an incident — and all report citations and cases that came out of it.

The common thread: none of these depend on a person watching a feed. The camera records; someone reviews it when there's a reason to. That's the model that fits a town our size — and a non-cellular trail camera is the simplest, cheapest tool that does exactly that.

The contrast — and it's happening right now

The same week as our meeting, residents in Amarillo, Texas packed a council meeting to push back on a proposed Verkada purchase: cloud-based, a 10-year license, $128,000, tied into the city's other "AI mass surveillance" contracts. Towns all over the country are having the exact argument Paonia just had. A simple, offline, Town-owned camera isn't us falling behind — it's us being ahead of a fight other places are still stuck in.

Why we can't just "get a Ring camera"

This comes up every time, so it's worth being direct. The popular consumer cameras are the opposite of what the Board asked for:

Product Why it fails the test
Ring (Amazon) Footage is stored on Amazon's servers and requires a paid subscription to record at all. Amazon has handed footage to police without a warrant and without the owner's consent. This is cloud custody by a third party — the Verkada problem with a different logo.
Nest (Google) Cloud-dependent, subscription for real recording, footage on Google's servers. Google has also disclosed footage to law enforcement without the owner's consent.
Wyze and similar cheap WiFi cams Cloud-tied, app-dependent, and has had repeated incidents where customers saw other people's camera feeds. Not something to put on public infrastructure.
"View it on your phone" camera kits (incl. the wired NVR kits) Even the ones that store video locally reach out to the manufacturer's servers so the phone app works. If you can see it remotely, it's online. The phone feature is the hole.
"4G / cellular / no-WiFi" cameras "No WiFi" is a trap — these still phone home over a cellular connection. "No internet" has to mean no internet, not "a different internet."

What to buy

The category is "non-cellular trail camera" (also sold as "no-WiFi trail camera" or "SD-card game camera"). These are built for exactly this — mount it, it watches, it records to a card, you collect the card. Any of these are solid:

Model Notes Cost
Browning Strike Force HD Pro X Well-regarded, fast trigger, long battery life, rugged weatherproof case. A reliable default pick. ~$110–130
GardePro A60 Built for long, unattended deployments — efficient on batteries, takes large memory cards. Good value. ~$60–80
Stealth Cam / Moultrie — any non-cellular model Widely available; just confirm the listing says non-cellular / SD-card, not "cellular" or "WiFi." ~$60–100
Each camera also needs: a microSD or SD card (128–256 GB, ~$15–25) and batteries (AA, or a rechargeable pack). One 256 GB card holds weeks of motion-triggered clips before it loops over the oldest. ~$25

Where to look: Amazon — "non cellular trail cameras". The one rule when buying: the listing must say non-cellular / no-WiFi / SD-card. If it mentions an app, a SIM card, "live view," or a monthly plan — wrong one, put it back.

Total cost to start

2–3 cameras, fully equipped: about $250–$450, one time. No monthly fee, ever. Compare that to a cloud system's subscription, which never stops.

Be straight about the trade-off

This is the simple, cheap, trustworthy option — and simple means it does less. Worth saying out loud so nobody's surprised:

  • No live view. Nobody watches a feed. To see footage, someone goes to the camera and pulls the card. That's the cost of it being truly offline — and it's a fair trade.
  • It records motion events, not 24/7. The camera wakes on movement and records a clip. For watching a gate, a yard, or a piece of equipment, that's usually what you actually want — and it makes the card last far longer.
  • Batteries need changing every few months depending on activity. A small solar pack or rechargeable batteries cuts that down.
  • Image quality is good, not cinematic. Fine for "what happened here," not for reading fine print at a distance.

If, after living with it, the Town decides it needs continuous 24/7 recording or central storage, that's a known upgrade path — see the footnote. But none of that is a reason to wait. Start with the simple thing that everyone can trust on day one.

How to set it up — the whole process

  1. Put a memory card in and load the batteries. That's the hardware done.
  2. Set the date and time using the buttons and little screen on the camera itself. (This matters — it timestamps the footage. No phone or computer needed.)
  3. Mount it on the wall at the infrastructure site, pointed at what matters. It needs no power outlet and no cable.
  4. That's it. It is now recording. There is no account to make, no app to install, no network to join.

How to get the footage

When you need to review video: open the camera, take out the memory card, and put it in a computer (most laptops have a slot, or use a $10 card reader). The clips are ordinary video files — double-click to play. Put the card back when done. To keep a clip as evidence, copy the file to a Town computer and note the date, time, and who pulled it.

Adopt a one-page footage policy at the same meeting

Alongside approving the cameras, the Board should adopt a short written policy: how long footage is kept, who is allowed to pull a card and view it, how each viewing is logged, and how a clip is produced if someone files a records request. This protects the public from misuse and protects the Town by setting the rules before there's an incident. And note the upside of holding the footage ourselves: when video is a public record, the Town can answer a CORA request directly — there's no vendor in the way.

Maintenance — what little there is

  • Change the batteries every few months (or use rechargeables / a small solar pack).
  • Swap or clear the card when it's full — it loops over the oldest footage on its own, so this is only needed if you want to archive.
  • No software, no updates, no patches, no passwords to rotate. An offline device with no network has nothing to maintain on that front. That is the point.

Footnote — the upgrade path, if it's ever wanted. If the Town later decides it needs continuous 24/7 recording or one central place to review everything, the next step up is wired cameras feeding a local recorder box that is physically never connected to the internet (~$350–500 for a 4-camera kit). It keeps the same "footage stays in Town hands" principle but requires the network isolation to be set up and verified correctly. That's a deliberate decision for another day — the deeper comparison is in the companion document camera-options-matrix.md. None of it is a reason to delay starting with the simple cameras now.
