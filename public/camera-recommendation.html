Decision Brief · Town of Paonia

Camera Options for Town Infrastructure

Evaluating a closed-loop replacement after the removal of the cloud-based system — with a recommendation and the cost reasoning behind it.

Prepared 2026-05-14  ·  For Board review
01

The decision in front of the Board

The Board has removed the prior cloud-based cameras and agreed to look at a replacement the Town fully owns and controls. This brief sets the requirements, compares the realistic options, and recommends one.

What a replacement has to meet

Custody
Footage stored on Town-owned hardware, on Town property — not on a vendor's servers.
No internet path
The system cannot send video off-site, because it is not connected to anything that reaches the internet.
No subscription
Bought once and owned. No recurring fee that can lapse or lock the footage.

03

Why not the full closed-loop system

The Town could build a true networked closed-loop system — wired cameras feeding a local recorder, on a network deliberately isolated from the internet. It is technically sound and it would meet the three requirements. It is not recommended, for three reasons:

1. It needs a technical owner the Town doesn't have

"Closed-loop" is only true if the network isolation is set up correctly and re-verified over time. That is a standing responsibility, not a one-time install. The Town has no dedicated IT staff to carry it, and a system that depends on someone remembering to maintain it will not stay closed-loop for long.

2. The real cost is ongoing, not upfront

The recorder and cameras are inexpensive to buy. The expense is the years that follow — hard drives wear out under constant recording and get replaced, hardware is serviced, and the isolation is checked. A low purchase price becomes a recurring obligation on the Town's budget and staff time.

3. It pays for a capability the Town won't use

The advantage of a networked system is live monitoring. The Town has no one assigned to watch a feed, and the community has not asked for one. Spending money and staff time on monitoring that won't be used is not a sound use of funds.

Bottom line: a networked system is more expensive to own than to buy, and the part it adds — live monitoring — is the part the Town doesn't need.
04

Recommendation: standalone recording cameras

A standalone recording camera — sold as a non-cellular trail camera — mounts on a wall, runs on batteries, and records to a memory card inside the device. It has no app, no WiFi, no account, and no network hardware of any kind. To review footage, a staff member pulls the card and plays it on any computer.

Why it fits the Town

  • Custody is physical, not a setting. Footage cannot leave the card until a person removes it. There is no vendor and no network to trust.
  • No ongoing cost or staffing. Nothing to maintain, no drives to replace, no isolation to verify, no software to update.
  • It does the one job the Town needs: a record to review after an incident — see Section 05.
  • Lowest cost, by a wide margin — both to buy and to own.

This is a record, not a surveillance system. No one watches a live feed. The camera sits in place; if something happens at the site — tampering, vandalism, an accident, a dispute over what occurred — a staff member pulls the card and reviews what was recorded.

05

This is a standard municipal approach

Reviewing recorded footage after an incident — rather than monitoring a live feed — is the established model for towns and cities addressing site-specific problems. Examples:

Seattle, WA
Official Illegal Dumping Camera Pilot — motion-activated cameras, footage reviewed after the fact, no live monitoring. Worked; the city expanded it.
Milwaukee, WI
Deployed hidden trail cameras — the same device class recommended here — at problem sites. Produced enforcement results; expanding from 10 cameras to 100+.
Springfield, MA
Mobile cameras used to identify illegal dumping after the fact; multiple citations issued.
St. Louis, MO
Locally-recorded alleyway cameras credited with roughly 200 cases over one year — all from after-the-fact review.

For reference on the alternative: Amarillo, TX is weighing a cloud camera contract at $128,404 for 18 cameras on a 10-year license — the cost profile this recommendation is built to avoid.

06

What it does — and what it doesn't

What it does

  • Records video at the site, day and night.
  • Keeps footage in the Town's physical possession.
  • Provides a record to review after an incident.
  • Runs unattended with no network and no subscription.

What it doesn't

  • No live viewing — footage is reviewed by pulling the card.
  • Records motion events, not continuous 24/7 video.
  • Batteries need changing every few months.
  • Image quality is good for identification, not high-detail at distance.

These limits are the trade for a system with no network, no vendor, and no ongoing cost. For watching a piece of infrastructure, they are acceptable — and if continuous recording is ever needed, that is a separate future decision.

08

Recommended next steps

  1. Approve the purchase of standalone recording cameras for the priority infrastructure site(s) — a starting budget of roughly $250–$450 covers three cameras.
  2. Adopt a one-page footage policy at the same meeting: how long footage is kept, who may pull and view a card, how each viewing is logged, and how a clip is produced in response to a records request.
  3. Assign a staff member responsibility for mounting the cameras, setting the date/time, and retrieving cards when footage is needed.
  4. Review after the first few months and decide whether coverage is sufficient or should be expanded.
